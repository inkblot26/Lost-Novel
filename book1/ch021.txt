Chapter 21: Bond of Respect 
They walked until the sun stood high overhead, heat pressing down through the canopy in suffocating waves. The air beneath the trees turned thick and unmoving, sweat clinging to skin as Edmund finally raised a hand.
“We should wait here. Kel and Gian, you two patrol first. Then we’ll take turns,” he said.
“Okay, sir,” they replied in unison, peeling away into the trees with practiced caution. 
The rest of the group settled into a small clearing where the shade was thick and the air heavy with the smell of damp earth.
“This is the nearest agreed meeting point, right?” Michael asked, lowering himself onto a fallen log.
“Yes,” Edmund confirmed. “There are three points we agreed on before entering the forest, in case we got separated. Didn’t you listen to Sir Torren?” His brow arched sharply.
“Don’t look at me like that,” Michael muttered, raising his hands. “I just follow you guys. You know how much I hate memorizing things.”
Cedric flipped a silver coin between his fingers, its glint catching the stray beams of light that pierced the leaves.
“What time do you think Mom and Dad will be here?” he asked. 
“Soon, Ced. Don’t be impatient,” Clara said, trying to reassure him, though she was hiding her own worry.
Edmund’s gaze lingered on her a moment, then shifted to Michael. “So… what exactly happened to you three?” 
Michael exhaled heavily, glancing at Baldwin, who was crouched in the shade, carefully tightening David’s bandages.
“As you know, Baldwin and David were patrolling the opposite direction from Gian and Kel when it all started,” he began. “Then, out of nowhere, chaos broke out. The Blinkclaw attacked us, and I’m sure you heard Sir Torren shouting for everyone to run.”
Clara’s head snapped toward him. “Then… are Mom and Dad safe?” Her voice cracked with sudden panic.
“They’re safe… as far as I know,” Michael said, but his tone faltered. “But—”
He lowered his head, shoulders tight.
“That thing… it killed Margaret. I’m sorry, my lady.”
Clara’s lips parted soundlessly. Her eyes brimmed with tears. “No…”
She forced herself to draw in a ragged breath, fighting to steady her voice. “Then what happened?”
Michael rubbed the back of his neck, eyes distant. “That thing was fast, too fast. But what made it worse was the pack of Bloomfangs suddenly showing up. We don’t know how they got there so quickly. Last we heard from Margaret, before she took her break from scouting, they were still a good distance away. That’s why Sir Thomas, Hugo, and that short mercenary were sent to monitor them.”
His words hung heavy in the clearing before he continued.
“When the Bloomfangs came charging, Sir Torren finally gave the order to run into the forest. The Tremorhoof stayed behind to delay the Blinkclaw. I didn’t see what happened to the others after that. Everything blurred together—they came from everywhere. I got separated, and along the way, I found Baldwin and David, who’d already been bitten.”

After their patrol shift, Gian and Kel settled down beside Michael, the three of them sitting near the edge of the clearing. The late-afternoon sun slanted through the branches, scattering gold across the ground, while the quiet murmur of Clara and Cedric playing nearby softened the heavy silence.
“Sir,” Kel began, fiddling with a loose strap on his boot, “not to sound negative, but we’ve been waiting here for a few hours… do you think they’re safe?”
“Don’t worry,” Michael said, his tone steady. “Sir Torren is strong. You might not know this since you’re still young and this is your first journey, but he’s well-known in this region. He was a ranking officer in the kingdom’s army before pledging his loyalty to Lord Henry. I’m sure he’ll protect both Lord Henry and Lady Evangeline.” 
Kel blinked in surprise. “Wow… so that’s why he’s so stiff and strict.” 
Michael’s gaze drifted toward Clara and Cedric, a faint respect in his eyes. “And don’t underestimate our lord either. He may not be part of Tillara City’s ruling family, but he’s still a Rosendale.” 
“What do you mean?” Gian asked, leaning forward.
Michael stared at him for a moment. “Wait—you don’t know the Rosendale Clan?” 
“Are they super rich or something?” Kel asked, raising an eyebrow.
Michael let out a short laugh. “Guess you two really don’t know. Tillara City, as you probably know, is one of the border cities in the Verdant Ring. It’s governed by the noble Highlord of Rosendale.” 
“So Lord Henry’s part of that family?” Gian asked.
“He is, but from a branch family, not the main ruling line,” Michael explained. “I don’t know the full story, but from what I’ve heard, Lord Henry left at a young age and traveled across the Kingdom of Lanthea. He built his wealth as a merchant, thanks to his business skills.” 
“Then why does Sir Torren serve Lord Henry and not the main family?” Kel asked, curiosity sharp in his tone.
Michael’s eyes flicked once more to Clara and Cedric, ensuring they were still distracted. Then he lowered his voice, the weight in his words sinking heavier than the air around them. 
“What I’m about to say isn’t exactly pleasant, but I think you should know. Not all nobles are good people. The ruling Rosendales of Tillara… let’s just say their reputation is questionable. I won’t get into the details, but Lord Henry’s different. He’s honorable and fair. From what I’ve heard, he once saved Sir Torren’s life. That’s why Torren pledged himself to him.” 
“And then what happened?” Kel asked, eyes alight now, fully invested. 
Michael hesitated, his fingers tracing idle circles in the dirt. “I probably shouldn’t be saying this… but back when Sir Torren was in the army, he was ordered to do something that went against his beliefs. He refused and was punished for it. He was sanctioned, imprisoned. When Lord Henry heard about it, he respected Torren’s ideals and stepped in. Paid the fines, pulled strings, and got him released. As you know, Lord Henry is a brilliant businessman with a lot of influence…”
Michael’s voice drifted off as Gian rose quietly, drawn by the soft groan of David stirring. He walked over to check on him, leaving Kel and Michael in the fading light.

By afternoon, the worry was plain on Clara’s face. Her eyes kept darting toward the forest trail, as though sheer will might summon her parents from the shadows. 
“Don’t worry, my lady,” Michael said, his tone steady but gentle. “I’m sure they’re just trying to find the path. That’s why it’s taking them so long.” As he spoke, he crouched down and began arranging kindling, striking flint to coax sparks into a small bonfire. The light would soon be needed, as the forest was already starting to dip into shades of orange and gray.
Edmund looked up as Gian and Baldwin emerged from the trees. “Did you spot anything?” he asked. 
“Nothing much,” Baldwin replied, lifting a hand. “But Gian managed to catch a hare. And we found some fruit along the way.” He held up a few apples, their skins glinting faintly in the fading light. 
“Wow, you’re lucky you caught one,” Clara said, her face softening with a flicker of genuine relief.
“I’ll start cooking, Lady Clara,” Gian said, already kneeling near the fire pit to clean and prepare the hare. 
“Thanks. Kel, let’s go,” Edmund said, giving a brief nod before signaling the start of their watch shift. Kel groaned but followed.
“Have you seen Mom and Dad yet?” Cedric asked suddenly, looking up with wide, anxious eyes.
“Not yet, young lord,” Baldwin answered in his calm, steady voice. “But I expect we’ll see them tomorrow. The sun’s already setting, and it’s dangerous to travel now.”
He patted the ground beside him, offering a reassuring smile. “Come here, young lord. Let me tell you a story while we wait.”

“It’s getting dark. We should keep the patrol range short, just to be safe,” Edmund said as he and Kel returned to the camp.
“I’m starving. Any hare left?” Kel asked, eyes already darting toward the fire. 
“There’s plenty,” Gian replied. “They saved most of it for you.” Without another word, he rose and disappeared into the trees to take the next patrol. Baldwin gave Kel a peculiar glance before following after him.
“Uh… suit yourself, Kel,” Baldwin muttered, his tone unreadable. 
“There aren’t many apples left, so you’ll have to eat the hare,” Michael said, smirking. 
“I’m sorry,” Clara added quickly. 
“Why are you apologizing, my lady? I’m just grateful you saved me some meat!” Kel said cheerfully, until his gaze landed on the food. His grin froze. 
“Wh—what is this?” He stared in horror at the hare, its skin blackened to a charred husk. 
“Maybe it just looks bad on the outside,” Edmund said, laughing as he tore off a piece. He bit down, then his face contorted. He grabbed his waterskin and nearly emptied it in one go. 
“It’s… so salty!” he sputtered, coughing. 
Michael shook his head with mock gravity. “It was already too late by the time we saw it.”
Kel frowned, unwilling to be outdone. “How bad could it be?” He tore off a chunk, forced it into his mouth… and instantly regretted it. His jaw stiffened as he chewed, his eyes watering.
“I… I think it’s fine,” he croaked. “Maybe if we pair it with jerky or an apple—”
“There’s no more jerky,” Clara interrupted softly. She lifted the last apple in her hand. “And this is it.”
Edmund and Kel slowly turned toward each other, a silent, grim understanding passing between them.
“We don’t have a choice, Kel,” Edmund muttered. “We have to eat something.”
Kel wiped at the corner of his eye, swallowing another bite with a shudder. “I’ll never let him cook again,” he whispered. Then, lowering his head like a man in mourning, he added, “Gian… you’ve lost some respect from me, not as a warrior, but as a cook.”

On the tenth day, Edmund returned from patrol, this time with news.
At first, it was only movement—branches shifting where there was no wind. Then silhouettes took shape between trunks, slow and deliberate. Gian and the others froze as the remaining members of the caravan finally appeared. At the front walked Sir Torren, his heavy warhammer strapped across his back, alongside Lord Henry, who carried a dark wooden staff inscribed with unknown symbols. Behind them came Thomas, his once-proud red hat now torn and hanging limply, walking beside Lady Evangeline. The two guards trailed wearily at the rear.
When Lady Evangeline caught sight of Clara and Cedric, she broke into a run. She fell to her knees, gathering her children into her arms, tears streaming freely as the three clung to one another.
“Welcome back, Lord Henry,” Edmund and Michael said in unison, dropping to one knee.
Lord Henry quickly helped them rise, his voice warm but weary. “It should be I who kneels. You’ve protected what is most precious to me. For that, I am in your debt.” He turned to the rest of the group, his gaze steady. “All of you—thank you.”
“It’s our duty, sir,” Gian said quietly. 
Sir Torren stepped forward then, eyes sweeping the campsite with his soldier’s scrutiny. “What’s the situation, Edmund?”
“Nothing major, sir. We’ve stayed put since yesterday. A few minor wounds, but manageable. David’s injuries are severe, but stable for now.” 
“Good,” Torren said, his eyes shifting to David, who lay off to the side, still resting.
Lord Henry nodded. “We’ll rest here for the day. Gather as much food as you can, the road ahead will be long, and our supplies are gone.” 
“Yes, sir. Baldwin already went to forage,” Edmund reported. 
“I’ll help him! I’m fast!” Thomas volunteered, vanishing into the trees before anyone could stop him. 
Kel, scratching his head, looked around. “Uh… where’s the Tremorhoof, sir?” 
A heavy silence fell. 
Sir Torren’s expression hardened, shadowed by grief. “There were two Blinkclaws that night. One drove the Bloomfangs into our path, tearing through everything. The other struck our camp directly.” His jaw tightened, his voice dropping. “My partner stayed behind to buy us time. Even with its armor-thick hide, the Bloomfangs swarmed it. The Blinkclaws struck without mercy. I felt the bond weaken… then vanish.”
No one spoke.
“I’m sorry, sir,” Gian said quietly, the words heavy in the air.
Torren exhaled slowly, the faintest tremor in his tone. “Don’t be. The Tremorhoof fulfilled its last command. To protect. And it did—until its final breath.”
