Chapter 29: Treadmill of Tyranny
Walking out of the west gate, they were met with the grim sight of a massive grain mill. A giant wooden wheel turned under the weight of dozens of prisoners trudging endlessly along it, their bodies sagging with exhaustion. Some staggered, nearly collapsing, only to be prodded forward by the crack of whips in the hands of uniformed overseers. The air carried the sour mix of sweat, dust, and damp wood, heavy with despair. 
Noticing their horrified stares, Oldman spoke. “That’s a treadmill. Those people are prisoners, sentenced to walk on that wheel all day to power the mill.”
“Isn’t that a bit harsh? They’re out in the open heat,” Gian asked, frowning.
“Well… if they committed serious crimes, maybe they deserve it?” Kel offered, though his tone wavered with doubt. 
“That’s the thing,” Oldman said grimly. “Most of them were falsely accused. And even when it rains, they’re forced to keep walking. No shelter. Just punishment.”
“They’re stopping,” Kel said quietly, noticing the sudden stillness among the prisoners.
“It’s almost six. They must return to the city before the gates close,” Oldman explained. “I’ll tell you more when we get home. For now, stop gawking. Didn’t your parents ever tell you it’s rude to stare?”
He led them down a narrow dirt path that snaked past fields of ripening rice. The air here was calmer, touched by the chirp of crickets and the faint rustle of leaves. At the far end stood a crooked line of weather-beaten houses, their walls sagging from years of neglect. One house stood apart, shaded by a broad yellow tree.
Its wooden walls were patched with mismatched planks, the roof sagging in places as if it had fought too many storms and lost. A crooked fence encircled the yard, half-swallowed by tall grass and weeds.
“Welcome to my home, if you can even call it that,” Oldman muttered, nudging aside a crumpled shirt with his boot.
Inside, the clutter was worse. Broken furniture leaned at odd angles against the walls, sagging shelves bowed under a jumble of tools, jars, and dust. The faint scent of old wood mingled with dried herbs and the lingering smoke of a long-extinguished fire.
“Thanks for helping us back there,” Gian said sincerely, scanning the small, crowded space.
“Yeah, you were a big help, Oldman. Thank you,” Kel added, lowering himself onto what looked like a log repurposed as a bench.
“Feel free to stay as long as you like,” Oldman chuckled, prying open a creaky window to let in the cooling evening breeze. Outside, the sun dipped low over the rice fields, painting the sky orange.
“About earlier—” Gian began, but Oldman raised a hand.
“Let’s eat first. I’m starving,” he said, pulling food from a small basket on the counter.
“Okay, let me help you,” Gian offered, stepping forward.
“No, no—don’t tire yourself. Just sit here,” Kel cut in quickly, rising to assist Oldman instead.

The audience chamber of Tillara’s ruler was vast and austere, its polished marble floor catching the fractured glow of afternoon light streaming through tall stained-glass windows. Heavy emerald  drapes hung stiffly against the walls, muting the draft that crept through the stonework. Above, an ornate chandelier glittered faintly, more for grandeur than warmth, while the long stone table below stretched like a monument, ceremonial rather than practical. The air smelled faintly of wax, dust, and aged wood. 
“Henry, you’re finally here. My apologies, I’ve been occupied since morning,” Highlord Richard said as he entered, his footsteps echoing across the marble. His presence filled the chamber before his words did: silver-gray hair swept neatly back, a regal robe of deep green threaded with gold. He carried himself like a monarch utterly certain of his dominion.
Lord Henry bowed low, standing respectfully aside. “It is quite alright, my lord. It is I who should apologize for keeping you waiting these past few days.”
Richard eased into the throne-like chair at the head of the table, a seat designed to raise him above all others in the room. “True, true. I had begun to wonder if you were ignoring my summons. My men reported that you left Esmara over a month ago, yet only now do you arrive in my city.”
Henry inclined his head. “We encountered an Ascendant Wildborn during our journey, my lord.”
“So I’ve heard,” Richard replied, his tone lighter than the weight of the subject. “A pity for your loss, but with your reputation, I’m sure it won’t take long to find another woman.” He laughed, loud and unrestrained.
Henry only smiled politely. Behind his back, his fist tightened.
“Now then,” Richard continued, motioning a servant forward with a tray of fruit, “let us speak of business. Perhaps you’ve heard: our recent venture into the Unknown uncovered a mine rich with valuable metals. A discovery that could significantly boost the city’s wealth, if handled wisely.”
He plucked a grape, rolling it between his fingers before popping it into his mouth. “I need someone trustworthy to oversee this project. Someone efficient, someone discreet… preferably of clan blood.”
His tone darkened, the words carrying more weight than the casual delivery suggested. “As you know, my late uncle spent years orchestrating and eliminating many members of the main Rosendale bloodline, perhaps hoping to clear a path to the throne. Unfortunately for him, I got to him first.”
Henry remained silent, though the air between them grew taut.
“I’ve chosen you, Henry,” Richard said at last, eyes locking on him. “Your experience and connections make you the ideal candidate.”
“Thank you, Highlord,” Henry replied evenly. “But I am merely of a distant branch of House Rosendale. My strength lies in trade, not in managing an industrial operation of this scale.”
Richard waved the protest away with a careless flick. “Henry, Henry… Yes, you’re from a distant branch, but of those who remain, you are the most capable. Besides” he smiled without warmth “I need someone to provide the initial funding. You're wealthy, and I trust your discretion I’ll handle the political side. Profits will be split: eighty for me, twenty for you.”
Before Henry could answer, Richard added smoothly, “Yes, yes, I know it seems unfair. But the mine’s potential is vast. Even twenty percent will make you wealthier than most nobles in the kingdom. And” he plucked another grape “I will not tax your share. Consider it a gift from your benevolent Highlord.”
He spoke through mouthfuls of fruit, not even meeting Henry’s eyes.
Henry kept his voice calm, measured. “I appreciate the offer, Highlord. I would like to ask for some time to think it through and prepare the necessary arrangements.”
Richard leaned back. “Fine. Three days, then. But remember—I chose you.”
He rose, brushing an invisible speck of dust from his sleeve. “And one more matter. To bind our houses closer, I propose an engagement between my brother Ronald and your daughter, Clara to proceed as soon as possible. A union most fitting for one of Rosendale blood.”
Henry’s gaze sharpened. “I cannot decide such a thing for her, Lord Richard. Clara will choose for herself. I brought her to Tillara City only so she might meet him and judge the matter with her own eyes.”
Richard’s smile thinned. “No matter. They will meet tomorrow. But I suggest you remind her of the honor it is—to join the ruling bloodline.”
With a flick of his hand, he dismissed Henry as if he were a servant rather than a lord.

Inside the gently rocking carriage, the leather seats creaked with every rut in the road. Lord Henry and Torren sat opposite each other, shadows swaying across their faces as muted sunlight filtered through the curtains. 
“How was it, my lord?” Torren asked at last. His voice was low, but the rare spark of anger in his eyes betrayed his restraint. He was clearly frustrated after waiting since noon, knowing full well that Highlord Richard likely had nothing urgent, other than parties and indulgence. 
“It’s exactly as we anticipated,” Henry said, gaze fixed on the rolling fields beyond the window. “He wants me to fund the mining operation and oversee everything myself.”
Torren’s jaw tightened. “So all the effort is yours, while he waits comfortably for profit,” 
Henry’s lips curved in a humorless smile. “And he demands eighty percent of the income.”
Torren’s composure cracked. “Eighty? Outrageous! You’ll be risking your fortune, not him!” 
Henry’s eyes darkened. “He also wants Ronald and Clara married. Soon.” 
Torren’s fists clenched against his knees. “He’s trying to chain you, my lord. Through Lady Clara. We should never have come here. We knew what kind of man Richard was.”
“We had no choice,” Henry replied evenly. “Refuse a Highlord’s summons without hearing him, and it’s treason. We’d already be dead.”
The words hung heavy between them, broken only by the steady clatter of wheels on cobblestones.
Torren leaned forward. “What do you intend now, my lord?”
Henry finally turned from the window, his expression sharpened. “First, Clara will meet Ronald. Thomas and Edmund must not leave her side.”
“I’ve heard Ronald isn’t a good man,” Torren said grimly. “Not someone I’d want near Lady Clara.”
“I know. But Clara insists that through their letters, he seemed kind. I want her to see the truth for herself, Torren. She needs to experience the world, not be shielded from it. Besides…” His eyes narrowed. “There’s another reason I came to this city.”
Torren’s voice dropped. “Are we truly preparing to revolt, my lord?”
Henry’s tone was firm, final. “I’ll speak with her first. But make the preparations. When the time comes, you’ll move Clara and Cedric out of here.”
Torren gave a curt nod. “No need to worry. I already set Michael on it. If the worst happens, they’ll travel through Gohara Town and reach the capital, Liora. Lady Clara and young Lord Cedric will be safe there.”
Henry’s hardened expression softened into a quiet smile. “Thank you, my friend.”

Back at the house, the three ate a quiet meal by candlelight. The little room smelled of stew and wood smoke; outside, crickets began their evening chorus. 
“That was good,” Oldman said finally, belching with a loud burp. “Didn’t expect you to be a decent cook.”
Kel grinned. “Grew up in a family restaurant. Learned a few tricks.”
Oldman’s smile slid off his face. The air between them cooled. “Thanks for the meal. Now you two should leave this city as soon as you can.”
Kel blinked, startled. “Why? What’s wrong with Tillara?” he asked, remembering the hulking treadmill at the gate.
Gian’s tone sharpened. “You mean the noble we played with?”
Oldman nodded once. “That was Ronald, the Highlord’s youngest brother.” His voice went flat, edged with long-buried resentment.
Kel’s grin faded. “So we really pissed off the wrong people, huh?”
Oldman’s features hardened. He set his spoon down as if it suddenly carried weight.
“This city wasn’t always like this. It used to be beautiful. The late Highlord was a rare kind of noble—he loved his people and cared deeply for this kingdom. Under his reign, Tillara thrived in agriculture, exporting grain and produce across the kingdom and even to neighboring realms.”
“But a decade ago, the plague struck. He fell ill and never recovered. When he died, Richard took the throne. Since then, he’s ruled with an iron fist. On paper, the city grew richer—production, exports, and income doubled—but at a terrible cost. Unjust taxes. Public executions. Forced labor. Innocents thrown into prisons, worked to death in fields and mills.”
Kel’s brows furrowed. “But why him? Did the late Highlord have no other heirs?”
“He did,” Oldman said. “Three children. Richard, the eldest—cunning, steeped in politics, but he despises the common folk. The second, Lady Rose—quiet, mysterious, avoiding even the other nobles. And then the youngest…”
He paused, jaw tightening. “Ronald is spoiled, incompetent, useless. More concerned with chasing girls than anything else.” 
“So Richard became ruler,” Oldman added, “by law as the eldest, and by the strength of his connections.”
Gian watched the old man. “Do you know Ronald personally?”
Oldman didn’t answer. He simply stared into the dying light outside.
Gian spoke gently. “I can see it. The hatred in your eyes when you mention his name. It’s stronger than the hate you show for Richard Rosendale.”
Oldman’s voice dropped to a whisper, heavy with cold fury.
“He’s someone I must kill.”
