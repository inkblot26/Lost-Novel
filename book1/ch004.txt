Chapter 4: A Twist of Luck
Every weekend, during the brief reprieve from training, Gian would climb the quiet hill beyond the town. Beneath a sprawling old tree, he’d lie back, close his eyes, and let the soft wind drown out the shouts of sparring teens and the endless lessons about Wildborn and Echoes.
It had become his small routine, a pocket of peace.
But today, the peace didn’t last.
The sky darkened faster than usual. A wall of clouds rolled over the horizon, thick and heavy, smothering the warm afternoon light.
Gian cracked one eye open. “That’s… weird. It was sunny a second ago.”
A faint growl of thunder answered him.
“Great.”
A cool gust rushed across the hilltop. Then the first raindrop hit his nose. Then another.
“Why now, of all times?” Gian groaned, scrambling to his feet. “I just wanted one peaceful nap!”
He darted down the hill, slipping and sliding, his boots squelching in the muddy slope. As he reached the road, he spotted movement ahead.
An old wooden wagon stood at an awkward tilt, its left wheel splintered and sunk into the mud. Beside it, a tall, slim merchant in a worn cloak struggled to keep it steady. A scarf was wrapped high over his neck and lower face, hiding most of his features.
Despite the chaos, his movements were controlled—measured in a way that felt slightly out of place. The wind howled across the road, scattering his supplies as bundles of cloth, small boxes, and trinkets rolled into the mud.
“Ah, blast this cursed storm!” the man shouted, stumbling as the wind tugged at his faded blue scarf.
Before Gian could decide whether to help, his foot hit a slick patch.
“Whoa—!”
He lost his balance completely and crashed straight into the merchant.
Both of them tumbled off the roadside slope, rolling through wet grass and sliding down a small embankment.
Then—
CRACK!
A blinding flash of lightning tore across the sky, striking the wagon with a deafening roar. Light exploded across the road, and for a brief moment, flames licked the broken wood before the rain swallowed them.
Both of them froze in stunned silence.
“That… that could’ve hit me,” the merchant said, eyes wide. He turned to Gian, breathless. “…Kid… you just saved my life.”
Gian sat up, dazed. Could that have been… my luck?
“C’mon, let’s move,” the merchant said suddenly, grabbing his arm. “This place isn’t safe!”
Another rumble of thunder answered, louder than before. The rain intensified, hammering down and obscuring the view ahead.
Gian and the merchant scrambled back up the road, their boots slipping in the mud. When they reached the wrecked wagon, they saw it was half-destroyed, the supplies charred and scattered.
“Well, I’ll be damned. Most of it’s ruined,” the merchant muttered with a grimace. “Talk about bad luck... but at least I’m still alive.” He gave a tired chuckle. “Say, you know a place we can wait out this rain?”
“There’s a big tree up the road,” Gian pointed. “It’s not much, but it’ll do for shelter.”
They started running down the road, the mud sucking at their steps. The wind grew wilder, tugging at their clothes. A tree branch cracked and fell behind them with a sharp snap, narrowly missing the merchant’s head.
“Watch it!” Gian barked, yanking him forward.
Through the rain, Gian made out a familiar shape. It was a massive tree with thick, overlapping leaves. 
“There! That tree—it’s dense enough to block the rain!”
They made a final sprint and slid under the wide branches. The leaves above them filtered most of the downpour, leaving only a light dripping sound around them.
The merchant finally exhaled, leaning against the trunk. “Hah… saved twice in one day. First lightning, now falling branches. Kid, you really might be my lucky charm.”
“Or unlucky,” Gian muttered under his breath.
The merchant extended his hand with a crooked grin. “Name’s Carthur Kain. From Freymar.”
Gian hesitated, then shook it. “I’m Gian. And I… didn’t really mean to save you. I just slipped.”
“Slipped or not, you pushed me out of the way of death. That counts as saving me.” Carthur chuckled, his faded blue scarf dripping with rain.
Gian blinked. “Wait. Freymar? That’s the island kingdom of Faylora at the northwest, right? What are you doing all the way here?”
Carthur laughed heartily. “What do we have here, a smart kid? Yeah, I’m a traveling merchant. Been all over Faylora and beyond.”
“Sorry about your merchandise,” Gian said.
“Eh, it’s alright. My life’s worth more than those scraps.” He grinned, pulling out a translucent red crystal amulet hanging on a black chain behind the blue scarf. “Besides, I’ve still got the important stuff.”
Gian looked at it curiously. “What’s that?”
“This?” Carthur smirked. “This is a Relic. It can store items.”
“A... Relic?” Gian asked, puzzled.
Carthur chuckled. “Thought you were a bright one. Guess you don’t know this yet. A Relic is an extraordinary tool or weapon, crafted by rare individuals called Echo Shapers. They’re blessed with the Echo of Shape.” 
“If only I possessed that gift…I’d have amassed a fortune long ago.” He laughed, shaking his head.
Gian frowned. “So, what do you mean it can store items?”
Without answering, Carthur raised his hand. A burst of flame ignited in his palm. When the fire faded, a beautifully designed glass made from colorful shells was sitting in his hand.
Gian stared in awe. “How did you...?” 
“To answer what’s in your head,” Carthur began, “Relics are forged with power and purpose. They can store, transform, or unleash abilities. But crafting one isn’t simple. You need rare Wildborn materials and an Ascendant Core.” 
“What’s a Core?” Gian asked, leaning forward. 
“Ascendants are Wildborn who’ve evolved beyond simple instinct. Smarter. Deadlier. Inside their skulls is a crystallized essence of their power—their Core.” Carthur tapped his temple. “And there’s an old saying –  The strongest Relics aren’t forged. They’re hunted.”
He held up the amulet again. “This one’s called the Emberglass Pendant. It stores items in a flickering fire-dimension. When I call them out, they appear with a burst of flame. Pretty neat, huh?”
“Neat? It’s amazing!” Gian said, eyes shining. “You could carry anything with that!”
“Haha, not quite. There’s a limit. But they say Relics grow with their user... some evolve, some even whisper the memories of fallen Wildborn. Who knows? Maybe one day I’ll store an entire castle,” Carthur laughed.
Then he gave Gian a sly grin. “If you had a Relic, kid, what would you want it to be?”
Gian thought for a moment. “Maybe… a sword that can cut through anything.”
Carthur chuckled. “A sword, huh? Classic choice. Well, I don’t have that... but here.” He tossed something toward Gian.
Caught off guard, Gian fumbled, but managed to catch it. It was a plain dark ring, intricately inscribed with a sunflower symbol on the inside.
“It’s called the Void Ring,” Carthur explained. “It can store small items. Not flashy like the pendant, but still useful.”
Before Gian could respond, another sharp rumble of thunder rolled across the sky. The rain began to ease, but distant lightning still flashed.
Carthur straightened, brushing mud off his cloak. “Looks like the worst is passing. Time for me to salvage what’s left of my wagon.” 
“Wait—your ring!” Gian said, scrambling up. “I can’t take this! It’s too valuable!”
“Keep it.” Carthur waved him off. “Consider it a token of gratitude for saving my life.”
“I didn’t mean to save you. It was just… an accident,” Gian insisted.
Carthur laughed. “Kid, sometimes accidents are just luck in disguise. And besides—” he tapped the red crystal amulet at his chest “—I’ve still got my pendant. And my dream of storing a castle.”
He winked. “Take care of that ring.”
Before Gian could say anything more, Carthur stepped out from under the tree and started down the muddy road.
“Thanks!” Gian called after him.
Carthur raised a hand in farewell, his figure fading into the misty drizzle until he disappeared completely.
Gian stared down at the simple ring in his hand, feeling its weight. For something so small, it seemed to carry a strange, quiet promise.
