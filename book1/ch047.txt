Chapter 47: To Train, To Rest, To Ready
Inside the second enclosed carriage traveling a short distance behind Lord Henry’s, Gian, Kel, and Oldman sat across from one another on the cushioned bench seats as the wheels rolled steadily along the road back toward the mansion. 
“So, four to five months, huh?” Kel said, glancing at Gian.
“Yeah,” Gian replied, clenching his hands. “We need to grow stronger, Kel. We can’t keep getting injured and almost dying every fight.”
“Don’t be too hard on yourself,” Oldman said. “The others were right—you’re still young. You’ll get stronger.” Tata gave a cheerful squeak and hopped onto Gian’s lap.
“Maybe, but I don’t have much time. I need to train—and at least get comfortable using this thing.” Gian looked at the buckler resting beside him.
“Well, for starters,” Oldman offered, “I can teach you both some hand-to-hand combat. It's better to be ready even when you're caught without your weapons.”
“What about this thing?” Kel said, lifting the short staff relic from where it rested against the bench and extending and retracting it playfully in the narrow space between them.  “What’s it called anyway?”
“I asked Lord Henry about it earlier,” Gian said.
“And?” Kel leaned in, curious.
“Stickk.”
“…What?” Kel blinked.
“The relic’s name is Stickk,” Gian repeated, shrugging.
Oldman chuckled. “Well, that’s... unique. Didn’t expect that from Lord Henry.”
“Seriously?” Kel looked down at the relic. “I thought it’d have a more dramatic name.”
“Someone has to use it,” Gian said.
“I’d love to, but I already have my relic,” Kel said, patting the Crimson Blood Axe secured at his belt, its handle resting against his hip. 
“Same here. I’m still struggling to get used to my shield,” Gian added, glancing at the buckler again.
Gian and Kel exchanged a look, then turned their eyes to Oldman.
“What?” Oldman raised an eyebrow. “Why are you two looking at me like that?”
“Why don’t you use it?” Kel suggested.
“It’s not mine,” Oldman said, waving them off. “What would Lord Henry say if he found out?”
“Technically, it’s ours now,” Gian pointed out. “We can do what we want with it.”
“He’s right,” Kel added, nodding. “And I think Lord Henry would be fine with it.”
“You can think of it as borrowing,” Gian said encouragingly.
“Yeah, and when I get bored with this axe, then I’ll ask for the staff,” Kel smirked.
“Better than having it gather dust in my Void Ring,” Gian added.
Oldman looked at the two of them pushing him and sighed. “Fine, fine. I’ll borrow it for now.”
Grinning, Kel handed the Stickk over.
Examining Stickk, Oldman muttered, “Who created this relic?”
“What are you doing?” Kel asked, watching Oldman turn the relic over carefully, inspecting every detail.
“I’m looking for its symbol—to find out who the Echo Shaper is,” Oldman replied.
“What do you mean?” Gian asked.
Oldman pointed at a small symbol etched among the many inscriptions. “See this tiny eye inside a circle?”
Gian leaned in and squinted. “Yeah… I see it now.”
“That’s the personal mark of the Echo Shaper who created this. It’s different from the other decorative patterns.”
“Wait—you’re saying every relic has one of those?” Kel asked, surprised.
“Exactly. Every Echo Shaper leaves their mark. These relics are their life’s work—their pride,” Oldman explained, while Kel immediately began inspecting his Crimson Blood Axe, Tata curiously peering at it too. 
“Echo of Shape isn’t rare. Plenty of people have it calling themselves as crafter. They create all sorts of gear and items called echotech from natural materials and Wildborn parts. But only a few become true Echo Shapers. That title’s earned when they successfully create a relic.”
“How do I know which mark is theirs?” Kel asked.
“Some Shapers make their mark obvious. Others hide it well. But it’s always inscribed somewhere on the relic,” Oldman replied.
Kel narrowed his eyes. “Wait—don’t tell me this blood drop shape on the handle is the mark?”
“If there’s no other distinct symbol, then yes, that’s likely the one,” Oldman nodded.
I see… so this sunflower must be the symbol of the Echo Shaper who made mine, Gian thought as he examined the sunflower design etched faintly along the inner surface of his Void Ring.
Kel peeked at it and grinned. “A sunflower? That’s kinda girly. Maybe your Echo Shaper was a woman.”
Tata squeaked from the side.
Oldman chuckled and turned to Gian. “What about your shapeshifting shield? Let’s try finding the maker’s mark on that one.”
“Yeah, let’s check!” Kel said, now excited again as he and Gian began examining the shield together.
After several minutes, Kel groaned in frustration. “Maybe this one’s so old the mark already faded?”
“Wait… there’s something in the strap,” Gian said, turning the shield slightly on his lap and noticing a small, nearly invisible pocket stitched along the inner leather lining.
He carefully opened it and pulled out a small, tightly folded piece of paper.
“What’s that?” Kel asked, leaning over. Oldman also leaned in, curiosity plain on his face.
Unfolding the paper, Gian found a note written in messy handwriting:
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Dear Gian,

I hope you take good care of this relic. It came from a good friend of mine. It’s called Morphguard, a shapeshifting armament that adapts to its user. Its default form is buckler shield mode—lightweight and compact. The other form, kite shield mode, is a bit heavier but offers exceptional defense.
I’ve never been able to activate the second form myself, but… there’s something about you. I believe you can.

Stay safe always,
—Your friend, Baldwin

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
“So this thing’s called Morphguard,” Kel said, holding the shield as Gian quietly folded the letter.
“Wait—how do you change its shape again?” Kel asked, realizing he hadn’t tried it before.
“I don’t know,” Gian replied. “I just… think about it changing to a bigger or smaller shield, and it transforms.”
Eyebrows furrowed, Kel focused hard. “Why isn’t anything happening? I’m doing exactly what you said.”
“Let me try,” Oldman offered, taking the shield and holding it in his hands.
After a moment, he handed it back to Kel. “Nothing. That’s strange. Most relics respond just by imagining the action. Some take a little more effort, but not like this.”
“Wait… are you secretly some kind of genius?” Kel asked, eyeing Gian with mock awe.
“No. I’m just ordinary,” Gian said quickly, shaking his head.
“Who’s Baldwin, anyway?” Oldman asked curiously.
Kel began recounting the story of their journey from Tala Town to Tillara as the carriage continued along the main road toward Lord Henry’s estate, the steady rhythm of the wheels and harness chains filling the pauses in his tale while Oldman and Tata listened.
Gian, half-listening, returned the folded paper to the shield’s hidden pocket. Something about me? he thought, staring quietly at the buckler resting beside him.

The following morning, Ronald was publicly sentenced to death by guillotine for his many crimes, the execution carried out in the open square at the center of the Northern District.
Oldman, Gian, and Kel chose not to witness it, but they heard that many furious citizens had gathered, shouting and throwing things at him.

At noon, Highlady Rose departed through the northern gate of Tillara City for Liora, accompanied by her personal guards and Captain John, the senior officer overseeing the military base stationed in the Northern District. She left the city under the temporary care of Miss Shena, a capable woman with strong managerial skills—secretly a noble whose family had suffered under Richard’s rule.

Four days later, Lady Clara and young Cedric arrived in Tillara City, finally reunited with Lord Henry and spending peaceful days together exploring the city.

Meanwhile, Gian and Kel began their training under Oldman’s supervision in the open courtyard behind the mansion, working to master their relics, sharpen their coordination, and learn each other’s habits. Day by day, they grew stronger—preparing for the journey still ahead.
