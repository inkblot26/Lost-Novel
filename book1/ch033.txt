Chapter 33: Beyond the Walls
That night, in the grand dining hall of the Rosendale mansion, Lord Henry, Clara, and Cedric sat around the long candlelit table, enjoying a quiet meal. Torren stood nearby, hands behind his back, ever vigilant.
“Torren, why aren’t you eating with us?” Cedric asked, slurping a spoonful of soup.
“It wouldn’t be proper, young lord. It’s not my place to sit at the table,” Torren replied, standing stiffly.
Henry chuckled. “Torren, how many times must I tell you? Stop clinging to those outdated noble customs. Come, sit and eat.”
“Understood, my lord,” Torren said with a slight bow. At last, he pulled out the chair beside Cedric and sat down as a maid stepped in to set a plate before him.
Henry turned to Clara. “Now, how did your meeting with Lord Ronald go?”
He looked at his daughter with genuine concern in his eyes.
Clara sat in silence for a moment, staring down at her plate before speaking softly.
“You were right, Father… He’s not who I thought he was. I’m sorry, but… I don’t want to marry Lord Ronald.”
Henry offered her a gentle smile.
“It’s alright, my dear. I know you only agreed to help for the family’s sake.”
He set his utensils down and looked at both Clara and Cedric with a more serious expression.
“There’s something important I need to tell you, both of you.”
Clara and Cedric straightened in their seats as Henry continued.
“Something is going to happen in this city soon. And I need you two to be far away when it does. Tonight, Thomas and the others will escort you to Gohara Town. From there, you’ll travel to the capital, Liora.”
“Tonight?” Cedric frowned. “But I just got here! I haven’t even explored anything yet!”
Clara looked at her father, her voice edged with concern.
“Does this have to do with the Highlord?”
Henry nodded solemnly.
“Yes. I won’t go into details, but you’ll have to trust me, Clara.”
He turned to Cedric and placed a steady hand on his shoulder.
“Ced, I want you safe. When all of this is over, you’ll be able to return when it’s truly safe.”
Cedric sighed, reluctantly nodding.
“Fine. But if something bad’s going to happen… why aren’t you coming with us?”
Henry’s expression hardened, though his tone remained calm.
“Because I must stay. Someday, when you’re older, you’ll understand. As nobles, there are duties we cannot abandon, even in danger.”
Clara’s eyes narrowed slightly. “Is this because I refused the marriage?”
“It’s not just that,” Henry said gently. “There are many reasons. But don’t worry, Torren will stay by my side. I’ll be fine.”
He reached across the table and took Clara’s hand.
“I need you to promise me something—look after Cedric. Keep each other safe.” 
“I promise,” Clara said quietly, gripping his hand.
Henry nodded. “Good. Now finish your meal and pack your things. You’ll leave before the moon reaches its peak.”

Midnight.
The mansion stood hushed beneath a pale moon. Lord Henry remained by the tall arched windows, watching silently as a modest carriage rolled through the iron gates, its lanterns swaying like fading stars. 
“Sir,” Torren announced as he entered the room. “Lady Clara and young Cedric have departed with Thomas and the rest. They’re on their way to Gohara now.”
Henry gave a slight nod but said nothing, his gaze still on the darkness beyond the glass.
Torren continued, his tone steady. “Aside from Thomas and Edmund, I assigned six of our newly hired guards to accompany them. The road to Gohara is open, with flat terrain and no signs of Wildborn. It should be safe.”
“How about the Westgate?” Henry asked. 
“I already paid the guard on duty. There won’t be a problem,” Torren replied. 
“Thank you, Torren,” Henry said quietly, finally turning away from the window.
Torren hesitated a moment, then asked with a firm but respectful voice,
“Are we truly doing this my lord?”
Henry met his eyes, resolve hardening his features.
“I’ve made my decision. I will decline the Highlord’s offer… and put an end to this engagement. I will stand with Lady Rose and join the revolt.”
His voice carried weight, calm but unshakable.
“Ready the carriage. We leave at once to meet her.”
Torren bowed. “Yes, my lord,” he said, before hurrying out to make the arrangements.

Inside the old wooden house, the door creaked open and the scarred Oldman stepped in as he removed his hat. 
“Where have you been, man?” Kel complained from the floor, munching on a biscuit as he looked up.
“There were things I needed to take care of,” Oldman said as he walked in and dropped a sack of supplies on the table. “And here are your essentials.”
A small Driftmouse leapt down from his shoulder, landing with a squeak and scurrying onto the table.
“It’s good to see you’re safe,” Gian said.
“Why wouldn’t I be? I know this city like the back of my hand,” Oldman replied, lowering himself into a chair with a sigh. He poured some water into a wooden bowl and placed it on the table for the creature.
Kel eyed the little thing curiously as it lapped at the water. “Is that your Wildborn?”
“Yeah. Name’s Tata. Say hi,” Oldman said. The Driftmouse paused and looked up at them, blinking slowly with its narrow, beady eyes.
Kel leaned closer. “Where’s the Rootseal?”
“What do you mean? Oh—ha!” Oldman burst out laughing. “That’s right, you wouldn’t know.”
He leaned back, clearly enjoying himself. “I don’t use a Rootseal. I have the Echo of Tame.”
Gian perked up. “You have two Echoes?”
Oldman raised an eyebrow, then looked impressed as he noticed Gian casually storing items into his Void Ring. 
“Whoa. What was that?” he asked.
“This guy’s got a relic with storage,” Kel answered smugly, folding his arms.
“Hmm, nice,” Oldman nodded. “Yeah, I’ve got two Echoes. Tame and Healing. Comes in handy more often than you’d think,” he added, reaching out to gently pat Tata as it sat on the edge of the table.
Kel smirked. “Well, since we’re all revealing secrets, check this out.”
He bit his finger, drawing a single drop of blood, then raised his hand. The droplet levitated in the air, dancing between his fingers.
“Shh.” Oldman raised a hand as Tata suddenly leapt off the table and slipped out through the door.
“What is it?” Gian asked, tense.
“I’m not sure,” the Oldman said, keeping his voice low. “Driftmice rely more on hearing, scent, and vibration than sight. If she reacted like that, something’s nearby.”
"Tata says there's a group of riders nearby." he added, as Tata communicated with him through their connection.
Kel’s grip tightened on his Crimson Blood Axe. “Are they coming for us?”
Oldman shook his head. “Not sure yet. Let’s wait inside.”
He turned back toward the doorway, gaze sharp. “I’ve sent Tata to scout ahead. She’ll let us know if they’re a threat.”

Inside the carriage, the wheels creaked softly as it rolled past the last cluster of worn-down houses, beyond which stretched a wide expanse of moonlit rice fields, silvered and still beneath the night sky.
“Don’t worry, Lady Clara. We’ve already passed the western gate, we’re outside Tillara City now,” Thomas said reassuringly. 
“Good thing Sir Torren paid off the guards to let us through without issue,” Edmund added, glancing back toward the city’s silhouette.
Clara nodded, but her expression remained troubled. “I just hope Father will be safe…”
Before Thomas could respond, the carriage lurched violently to a halt, the horses snorting in alarm as reins pulled tight. 
“Why are we stopping?” Clara asked, startled by the abruptness.
Noticing Edmund’s expression shift, Thomas turned to him sharply. “What is it?”
“I was careless,” Edmund muttered, frowning. “I thought we were clear once we passed the gate, so I didn’t use my Echo to rest. But… there are men outside. I can hear them now.”
Thomas’s expression hardened. “Let’s go. Lady Clara, please stay inside with Young Cedric. We’ll handle this.”
Without another word, Thomas and Edmund leapt from the carriage into the night.
“Who’s in charge here?” the lead soldier, clad in a green military uniform, called out as eight other soldiers—some mounted, others on foot—spread out to surround the carriage.
“Good evening, sir,” Thomas replied calmly, his eyes scanning the area.
Nine soldiers… A patrol squad. How did they find us so quickly? Then he looked up—and saw it. A winged figure circled overhead, its wide wings cutting across the moon. A rider clung to its back, silhouette sharp against the pale sky. Of course. An Echo of the Pack. He must’ve spotted us from above.
“I’m Sergeant Claren, leader of this squad,” the soldier said, stepping forward. “Identify yourselves and explain why you exited the western gate after curfew.”
The surrounding soldiers dismounted from their horses and raised their weapons—swords, axes, and bows—all pointed toward the carriage.
Thomas’s mind raced. We might be able to fight through them… but I’ll need to deal with the Echo user above. 
“I’ll ask again, who are you?” Sergeant Claren demanded, eyes sharp with suspicion. 
“We’re personal guards of Lord Ronald,” Thomas declared, raising both hands slowly. “We were given direct orders to deliver something urgent beyond the city, which is why we left in haste tonight.”
One of the soldiers leaned in and whispered to Claren, his gaze never leaving Thomas.
Edmund moved closer, speaking in a low voice. “They’re not buying it. They didn’t receive any update from the barracks.”
 “I know,” Thomas whispered back. “I’m just waiting for the right moment. Get ready—we will have to fight.”
His hand shifted subtly, aligning toward the circling Echo user above.
Claren stepped forward. “We received no such orders. Do you have a written letter bearing Lord Ronald’s seal?”
“Yes, we have it,” Thomas replied, turning slightly toward Edmund as if expecting him to produce the letter. But under his breath, he murmured, “Stay close to the carriage. No matter what.”
Edmund heard it clearly thanks to his Echo and gave a subtle nod.
In a flash, the calm broke.
From Thomas’s raised hands, a sudden burst of fierce wind erupted, tearing upward like a coiled storm. It struck the dark shadow circling above, its wings thrown off balance, and the rider atop it tumbled down, crashing hard into the field below.
Gasps and shouts followed as the soldiers flinched in surprise.
“Attack!” Thomas shouted.
