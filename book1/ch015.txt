Chapter 15: Not Fast enough
From beside the carriage and supply wagon, a short distance behind the frontline, only Michael with his Echo of Bow and Thomas with his Echo of Wind could provide ranged support. Arrows cut through drifting dust while sudden gusts of wind tore across the road, striking Bloomfangs still several paces from Torren’s embattled unit.
At first, the frontline formation held. Steel rang, beasts fell.
But the forest kept spitting them out.
Through a narrow gap between David’s shadow wolves, one Bloomfang slipped past, paws barely touching the ground as it sprinted toward the caravan.
Panting from the strain of repeated ability use, Michael loosed an arrow at the beast. It darted aside, springing off a tree trunk to evade the shot — only to be caught mid-air by a violent burst of wind. The gust twisted its body unnaturally before hurling it into the dirt, skidding in a spray of leaves and dust.
“Everyone, be alert, they’re here!” Thomas shouted, his red traveling hat blown off by the winds he summoned. 
“Michael, focus on that one! It’s not dead yet!” he barked, already turning his attention toward another Bloomfang that had broken past David’s summoned wolves.
“Yes, sir!” Michael replied, releasing two consecutive arrows—one piercing its torso to pin it in place, the second burying into its head to finish it.
Two more Bloomfangs reached the carriage. Baldwin and another guard intercepted them with drawn swords, defending the carriage doors from both sides. At the same time, Thomas leapt high on a burst of wind, blade flashing as he descended on one of the beasts.
Gian tightened his grip on his sword, ready to rush forward, until a shout rang out behind him. 
“You stay here and protect the wagon!” one of the guards ordered, sprinting past toward the carriage. Another guard from the second wagon followed, moving to support Thomas and the others.
“Kel, Gian, our priority is this wagon,” Edmund said firmly, unsheathing his sword. “The food and supplies are here. If we lose this, we won’t last the journey.”

The Bloomfang’s claws slashed in a wide horizontal arc at Baldwin. He caught the blow on the buckler strapped to his left arm, the impact jarring his shoulder, then countered with a thrust of his sword. The beast sprang back on its powerful legs, narrowly evading the strike.
“Damn, they’re fast!” Baldwin growled, lunging after it.
He stabbed again, but the creature darted to the side. Snarling, Baldwin drove forward behind his buckler, ramming into the beast with brute force and cutting off its escape. With a sharp twist, he slashed across its flank, finally drawing blood.
Thomas closed in from the side and slashed across its leg, crippling the beast. Baldwin seized the opening, driving his sword clean through its chest.
“Thanks,” Baldwin said, catching his breath.
“No problem. Stay close to the carriage,” Thomas replied.
Even as he spoke, two more Bloomfangs burst through the thinning defensive line, claws gouging furrows into the road as they charged the carriage. The Tremorhoof snorted and stamped, reins snapping taut as it sensed the threat. 
Thomas surged forward, gliding with the speed of a gale. With a sharp sweep of his arm, a powerful gust of wind roared out, slamming into one of the beasts. It crashed to the ground, pinned by the invisible force, grass flattened in a circle around it. In a blur, Thomas circled and delivered a precise diagonal slash from ear to neck, ending it in a single stroke.
“Not on my watch,” he muttered.
At the same time, Baldwin and a non-echo guard intercepted the second beast. It lunged, snapping its jaws for Baldwin throat, but he deflected the bite with his buckler and smashed the rim of the shield into its skull. As it reeled, the other guard’s sword finished the job.

Meanwhile, on Torren’s side of the battle, he launched himself into a leaping strike, driving his full weight down and crushing a Bloomfang beneath his war hammer, splattering blood across the ground. He lifted his gaze.
The battlefield was widening.
Too many fronts. Too many gaps.
“I’m not fast enough,” he muttered, jaw tightening. “I need to kill faster.”
With a sweeping arc of his war hammer, Torren smashed into the surrounding Bloomfangs, scattering them in chaos. Behind him, the mercenaries struggled to hold the line. Agnes, claws bared, shielded Hugo as he loosed arrow after arrow, his stamina draining quickly.
Then six Bloomfangs broke through their flank.
Seeing it, Thomas muttered, “This is bad.”
He and a cluster of nearby non-Echo guards rushed to intercept, trying to drive the beasts back and keep them away from the carriage. But before they could fully recover, five more Bloomfangs burst from the treeline, charging straight for the carriage.
Thomas’s eyes widened. Sprinting back, he barked orders: “You three—stop the ones at the front!” he shouted, pointing sharply at the guards.
Baldwin closed in on the carriage as Michael loosed a flurry of arrows, trying to slow the incoming Bloomfangs.
As the wildborns closed in, the pack split with eerie coordination — two barreled directly toward the carriage doors, while three peeled off sharply, racing low and fast toward the supply wagon.
The ground trembled under their combined charge.

Dust kicked up around the wagon wheels as the three Bloomfangs approached, fangs bared and vines along their backs thrashing like living whips. They circled in the churned dirt beside the wagon, breaths steaming in the heated air despite the sun overhead. 
“Finally,” Kel grinned, stepping forward as Gian and Edmund lined up beside him.
“One for each of us,” Edmund said, unsheathing his sword with a glance at the others. “Don’t die on me.”
“Come on,” Gian whispered, tightening his grip.
Without another word, the three broke off to intercept their targets.
Gian met his head-on, thrusting for its left eye. The Bloomfang twisted away, but not cleanly—the blade scraped its leg, drawing blood.
Snarling, it swiped low, trying to rip the sword from Gian’s grip. He yanked it free and held fast.
The wolf-like beast lunged, fangs flashing.
Gian sidestepped, dragging his sword down in a vicious slash—only for the Bloomfang to spring back with unnatural speed.
They circled, breath ragged, eyes locked.
“You’re fast,” Gian muttered, blade raised.
The Bloomfang crouched, growling, ready to pounce.
“But not fast enough.”
Steel and fang clashed again and again, neither landing the final blow. Gian’s strikes were sharp, but the creature always retreated at the last instant.
This isn’t working, he thought. I need to change something.
He suddenly charged, feinting a downward swing. As expected, the Bloomfang leapt back—right into Gian’s trap. He released his sword mid-strike, hurling it forward. The blade sank deep into its stomach.
Before it could recover, Gian followed with a crushing left hook to its head, dropping it flat.
Seizing his sword, he drove it deeper, finishing the beast.
A sudden claw lashed for his back—too fast to dodge. But before it landed, a blood-red axe spun through the air and buried itself in the attacker’s skull.
“Don’t just stand there!” Kel barked, already beside another Bloomfang’s corpse.
Gian glanced around. Even the Tremorhoof had joined the fray, its massive hoof pinning a wildborn beneath it. On the far side, Edmund battled two at once, one already limping.
“Stay sharp,” Kel snapped, yanking his axe free before sprinting to aid Edmund.
“My bad,” Gian muttered, turning just in time as another Bloomfang lunged.
He deflected its claws, sliding his blade across its ribs to open a deep gash. As it flew past, he spun and hacked again, severing the vine-like growths sprouting from its back. Blood sprayed as it crashed hard to the ground.
Blood pooled dark beneath the creature as Gian drove the blade into its throat. The forest noise rushed back in around him — shouts, steel, the roar of wind — reminding him the battle was far from over.
