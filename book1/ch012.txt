Chapter 12: Refuted Echo	
When Gian reached the plaza, he immediately spotted the caravan.
A tall, broad-shouldered man clad in metal armor stood beside the carriage, a heavy war hammer resting against his shoulder. Behind him waited a single enclosed wagon, its massive frame pulled by a Tremorhoof—a towering beast of muscle and endurance.
The wildborn stood nearly nine feet tall at the shoulder, towering over humans. Its enormous body was built less for speed and more for power, with thick limbs capable of carrying heavy loads across the harshest terrain.
Its stone-colored hide was rough and scarred from countless journeys, and its heavy iron-like hooves were firmly planted into the earth. Despite its intimidating size, the beast remained still, breathing slow and steady, carrying the weight of the carriage as if it were nothing.
Even in its stillness, the ground seemed to carry a faint weight beneath it.
Behind the carriage stood two supply wagons pulled by sturdy draft horses. Around them, nearly twenty people moved with purpose—tightening ropes, checking crates, securing tarps. Others rested beneath the shade of a wide tree, conserving their strength for the road ahead.
This wasn’t a small merchant trip.
This was serious.
As Gian approached the man he had seen in the bar, he asked, “Are you Torren, the Guard Captain?”
Torren gave him a quick once-over, scanning him from head to toe. “You’re applying for the assignment?”
“Yes. Can I join?”
“Sure,” Torren said with a nod. “We’re almost finished resupplying, so there’s nothing left for you to do. Head over to that guy,” he said, pointing across the plaza. “He’ll explain everything.” He extended a hand.
“Okay, thanks,” Gian replied, accepting the handshake.
As Gian passed the Tremorhoof, he noticed a dark green collar of twisted roots coiled around its thick neck.
A Rootseal.
So that’s how they bound it… he thought. A crafted seal that tethered a wildborn to its master through Echo of Shape.
He approached a lean man wearing a light brown leather vest and a red traveling hat.
“You’re joining us?” the man asked as Gian walked up.
Gian nodded.
“All right. You can sit with the others,” he said, pointing to the shade beneath a tree. “I’ll explain everything to the newbies once we’re set up. Name’s Thomas,” he added with a smile.
Gian sat down among the group, scanning the faces of the other recruits—six strangers in total. He hadn’t spent much time in town anyway, always busy with training or picking up odd jobs. 
“Hi! Name’s Kel,” said a young man, just a few years older than Gian, slightly taller, wearing a dark red coat. His messy brown hair looked like it hadn’t seen a comb in days, and he spoke with enthusiastic energy.
“Gian,” he replied, his eyes drifting to the towering Tremorhoof nearby.
“Pretty cool, huh? I wish I had a Rootseal too. But those things cost a fortune,” Kel said, catching Gian’s attention.
“Well, what can we expect? They’re crafted by someone who can wield the Echo of Shape, using a primeflora roots. That stuff’s rare. Still… imagine controlling an ascendant wildborn  with one,” he added, eyes gleaming.
“That’s not possible,” a gruff voice interrupted. An old man with a gray, messy beard joined the circle, making it seven recruits in total. “I’ve never heard of anyone taming an ascendant wildborn with a rootseal. Do you know how strong you’d have to be? Just holding it still long enough to attach the seal, and not get killed, would be near impossible.” 
“I guess,” Kel admitted, “but just imagine it. If I could hire a strong Echo user, or even better an Echo Master, to help subdue it, I wouldn’t stop at a single drop. I’d spill every last drop of my blood if that’s what it took to seal the bond.”
The old man snorted. “Even if you miraculously hired someone of that level with your meager coins, the collar would probably wither and fall apart on its own. It would crack the moment it sensed your weakness. Remember—the bond weakens when respect fades.”
Kel’s face flushed with frustration as he glared at the old man.
“Anyway, what do you think happened to them?” Kel asked Gian, casually changing the topic.
Gian glanced around and noticed six other men wearing the same style of light leather vests as Thomas. So there are seven guards, plus the head guard. The rest must be hired like me, he thought, noting the differences among the caravan members.
“Not much of a talker, huh?” Kel said with a chuckle, undeterred.
“Well,” he went on, “I heard they started with about a dozen guards and a handful of mercenaries. Then they ran into a pack of wildborn that outnumbered them and killed a few of them.” 
“Whatever it was, it doesn’t concern us,” the old man nearby cut in. He adjusted the buckler strapped to his arm. “We’re heading in a different direction. I just want to get paid.”
“You’re right. I’m more concerned about what the young lady looks like,” Kel said with a dreamy smile. “I heard she’s a beauty, but cold. Always stays inside that carriage.”
“Don’t even think about it, kid,” the old man said flatly.

“Alright, listen up, everyone,” Thomas called out, raising his voice to get the group’s attention. “As some of you may already know, your assignment is to escort the family of Lord Henry Rosendale from here to the city of Tillara. If all goes well, the journey should take eight to ten days peacefully.”
He paused, his expression turning serious. “I won’t lie to you. On our last leg, we ran into a pack of wildborn with a stronger one among them. It killed a dozen of our men. Worse, our food supplies were destroyed during the retreat. Fortunately, this town was nearby, so we stopped here this morning to resupply and rest.”
“What kind of wildborn was the strong one?” asked a short, ugly man holding a heavy club lined with spikes.
“We’re not exactly sure,” Thomas admitted, his face clouded with unease.
“What do you mean you don’t know?” Kel asked, brows furrowed in curiosity.
“It was night,” Thomas explained, voice steady but strained. “We were already engaged with a pack of feral wildborns when something else entered the fight.”
He paused.
“Small. Fast. It broke through our formation before we even saw it clearly. Armor didn’t matter. Men were down before they could react. Then it vanished.” 
A murmur spread among the recruits.
“We believe it may have been a Blinkclaw,” Torren said, stepping forward. His presence alone quieted the group.
“We’re not completely certain,” Thomas quickly added, glancing at the recruits with concern. “We don’t want to cause panic.”
Gian’s eyes narrowed. He could feel the tension rise as the crowd stirred, clearly shaken by the name.
“Damn… how unlucky of you. That’s an ascendant wildborn,” Kel muttered, not realizing he’d spoken out loud.
“Maybe it was a Blinkclaw, maybe it wasn’t,” Torren said firmly. “But it didn’t pursue us. We’ve seen no signs since. We believe it has moved on.”
With a nod, he stepped aside and allowed Thomas to continue. 
“Now, you all knew this job came with risks. That hasn’t changed,” Thomas said. “I won’t force anyone to stay. Some of the original mercenaries chose to remain here in town, whether out of fear or injury, yet Lord Henry Rosendale, in his generosity, still compensated them for the days they accompanied us.”
Thomas opened a small leather pouch. Silver glinted inside.
“A hundred silver now. Nine gold upon safe arrival.”
Silence followed.
One man stepped back.
Then another.
Then another.
Fear spread faster than courage.
When it was over, only three remained.
Gian.
Kel.
And the old man with the buckler.
“I guess three isn’t bad. At least we’ve got someone,” Thomas said as he handed them their share of silver. “Do any of you wield an Echo?”
Kel shook his head.
“Not me,” the old man said.
“I don’t,” Gian added, just as he noticed Torren walking toward the carriage.
“Guess we’ll stay at seven Echo users,” Thomas murmured, walking away after giving the payment.
The curtain pulled back to reveal Lord Henry, a man with straight posture, a well-groomed beard, and neatly combed gray hair. He exchanged a few words with Torren, gave a nod to signal their departure, then drew the curtain shut.
With a confident stride, Torren approached the front of the caravan and leapt onto his horse beside the towering Tremorhoof.
“Alright, everyone! Let’s move out!” he called.
The caravan lurched forward.
Townsfolk gathered along the road.
“What is that?” a child whispered, pointing at the Tremorhoof.
“Only the wealthy can afford such things,” an old man replied.
Eventually, they reached the northern gate. For the first time, Gian was about to go farther than the hill that marked the edge of his world.
He paused just past the gate.
Didn’t speak.
Didn’t look for anyone.
He simply tightened his grip on the worn leather of his sword hilt.
Then he stepped forward.
Not luck.
Not fate.
This path was his.
And he would carve it with his own will.
