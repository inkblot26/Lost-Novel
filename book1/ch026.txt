Chapter 26: More Than a Job
Gian immediately rushed to Kel, who had collapsed on the blood-soaked earth, unconscious after the brutal fight. Kneeling, he pressed two fingers to his neck—still a pulse. Relief loosened the knot in his chest.
Turning toward the source of the water surge, he spotted Clara staggering forward, her chest heaving, the Whispering Scroll of Water crumpled in her trembling grip. The glow from the orb above caught the sheen of sweat on her face.
She steadied herself and hurried over.
“How is he?” she asked, voice raw with worry.
“He's alive, but barely. If we don't stop the bleeding soon, he won't last.” Gian replied, pulling bandages and cloth from his Void Ring. His hands worked quickly, wrapping Kel’s bleeding side with practiced precision.
“Thank you for saving us,” he added.
“I should be the one thanking you two... for holding that thing off,” Clara said softly as she knelt beside him and began assisting.
Gian’s eyes flicked to the glowing orb above. Unease gnawed at him. “What’s happening back at camp?” 
“I don’t know,” Clara admitted, her voice trembling. “When I left, Father was already weak from an arrow wound. He told me to find you… and to use the Scroll if I had the chance. But when I arrived—” she faltered, eyes on her shaking hands, “—seeing you both torn and bleeding, I wasn’t sure I could even control the water.”
“You did splendidly, Lady Clara,” Gian said firmly, taking her hand and meeting her eyes with quiet steadiness. “But I need you to hold on a little longer.”
He pushed himself to his feet, pain biting deep into his own cuts as fresh blood seeped through torn chainmail. With a grunt, he stooped and hooked his arm under Kel’s limp frame, hauling him up.
“Kel’s bleeding out. He needs a healer now, or he won’t make it. Help me carry him. Once we’re near camp, you stay low and hide until I give the signal. Understood?”
Clara swallowed hard, then nodded. She moved to Kel’s other side, slipping her arm beneath his weight. Together, staggering under the burden, they began their slow, painful trek back through the ruined forest.

But before they could get far, two figures emerged from the trees: Thomas and Edmund, rushing toward them. 
“Are you alright?” Thomas called out, his eyes flicking past them to the ravaged forest, where shattered trunks and torn earth bore silent witness to the battle.
“Where is it?” Edmund demanded, his voice sharp, meaning only one thing—the Blinkclaw.
“It’s dead, sir,” Gian managed, breath ragged. Relief had barely settled over him before his strength gave out. His knees buckled. The world tilted sideways, sounds stretching and warping—then darkness swallowed him whole.

Time passed in a haze of muffled voices, jolting movements, and warmth pressed against his side. Gian drifted in and out—aware of being lifted, of someone calling his name, of a hand pressing firmly against his wounds—then nothing.
When he finally stirred again, the swaying beneath him told him he was moving. His eyes fluttered open, heavy and burning, and he tried to sit up. The wooden ceiling above him came into focus. He was inside a wagon. The faint rattle of wheels over uneven stone echoed beneath him, and the scent of dried herbs mixed with iron filled the cramped space.
Beside him lay Kel, still unconscious, his face pale but breathing steady. At the front opening, Baldwin sat with his back turned, shoulders squared, gazing at the road ahead.
Noticing movement, Baldwin turned—and a grin broke across his tired face.
“Hey, you’re finally awake! Thought you’d be sleeping ‘til we reached the city.”
“What’s going on? How long was I out?” Gian rasped, instinctively reaching to check his bandages.
“Easy, lad,” Baldwin said, lifting a hand. “You’re safe now. We’re on our way to Tillara City—should reach it before evening. Both you and Kel have been out since last night. Sir Torren sent Thomas ahead in the dark to find help, since so many of us were wounded.”
“Turns out Thomas ran into a Tillara patrol that had been sent to investigate Bloomfang sightings,” Baldwin said, his tone softening. “Once they realized Lord Henry was from the Rosendale Clan, they rushed to escort us back. Would’ve been grim without them. Talk about luck, eh?” He handed Gian a waterskin.
“Yeah... some luck,” Gian muttered after a sip. “How is everyone?”
“As you know, Lord Henry caught an arrow in the back, but he’s stable now. One of the Tillara guards has a Healer Echo. Lady Clara’s fine, though completely exhausted. Can’t blame her after what happened.  Poor little Cedric cried himself hoarse all night… and Lady Evangeline—” Baldwin paused, his voice heavy. “She didn’t make it. Clara held Cedric until he cried himself empty. They’re riding in the wagon ahead of us.”
His words hung in the air, solemn. After a moment, he went on.
“Torren only took minor injuries. Same with Thomas, which is why he was sent ahead—his Echo of Wind made him the fastest. Michael took a heavy blow shielding Cedric, but the Healer treated him. Edmund’s wounds were light. As for us mercenaries… well, we’re what’s left.”
Gian nodded faintly, then asked, “What happened after we left?”
Baldwin rubbed the back of his neck, his grin fading. “We managed to hold the bandits for a while, thanks to Sir Torren’s strength. Then Clara bolted after you. I tried to follow, but Michael went down, and someone had to guard Cedric. I’m sorry, Gian—I wanted to be there. I really did.”
“It’s alright,” Gian said quietly. “None of us expected any of it. We all just… did what we could.” He hesitated, then added, “But even if you didn’t follow, your Echo of Glimmer saved us. Without that light, we’d have been blind in the dark. Thank you.”
Baldwin froze, then chuckled softly—but there was something distant in his eyes. “At last… my Echo did something worth remembering.” His gaze drifted toward the horizon. “Thank you for surviving, Gian.”
Gian studied him, noticing the shadow behind Baldwin’s smile, but chose not to press.
“Eventually,” Baldwin continued, “Thomas and Edmund returned after dealing with the Bloomfangs. With their help, we wiped out the rest of the bandits. Then Torren sent them to check on you and Kel while the rest of us guarded the family.”
Baldwin finally looked him in the eyes.
“Tell me, Gian. Even with the light to see by… how did you two hold out against an Ascendant Wildborn? And even kill it?”
Gian hesitated, memories flashing—the desperate struggle, Clara’s trembling hands, Kel’s last burst of strength.
“I don’t know,” he admitted at last. “Everything happened too fast. Lady Clara arrived with the Whispering Scroll… we just seized the moment and finished it.”
“I see,” Baldwin said after a pause, turning back to the road. “Good thing nothing happened to you—and somehow, the rest of us managed to survive.”

Later that afternoon, just before they reached the city, Kel stirred awake at last. Baldwin leaned over and quietly explained everything that had happened while he was unconscious. Afterward, the three of them fell into a weary silence, the wagon rocking gently as the road carried them forward.
By the time the sun dipped below the horizon, the sky was painted in hues of gold and crimson, its fading light casting long shadows over the plains. Ahead, the towering stone walls of Tillara City rose into view—massive, weathered, and crowned with watchtowers where banners of the Kingdom of Lanthea, bearing the crossed hoe and sickle within a green circle of vines, snapped in the wind.
The southern gate groaned open, iron hinges echoing against the stone corridor as the weary caravan passed beneath its shadow. Inside, the world changed. Lanterns flickered to life. Merchants called out from closing stalls. The scent of roasted meat and fresh bread drifted through the streets—life continuing, indifferent to the blood that had been spilled beyond the walls.
From there, they were escorted to Lord Henry’s estate, a sprawling mansion tucked safely within the city. Servants ushered them inside, and at last, each was given a private room to rest, the weight of the past battles finally lifting as exhaustion claimed them.

Morning light filtered through the tall, arched windows of the Rosendale mansion’s dining hall, washing the polished wooden floor in pale gold. Despite the grandeur of the room, an unusual silence lingered within its walls. Servants moved quietly, speaking only in hushed whispers as if afraid to disturb the mourning that hung over the estate. Fresh white lilies rested beneath a framed portrait of Lady Evangeline near the far wall, their faint fragrance mingling with the aroma of warm bread. Even the clinking of silverware seemed restrained.
Only four places were set at the long dining table—Lord Henry at the head, with Gian, Kel, and Baldwin seated across from him.
“I want to thank you three for everything you’ve done for us on this journey,” Lord Henry began, his voice warm, hands folded together on the table.
Torren stepped forward and placed a heavy pouch of coins before each of them with precise, almost ceremonial care.
“I’d like you to accept this additional payment,” Henry continued. “I know it may not be enough, considering you saved the lives of my children.”
The three men immediately shook their heads.
“These two did more than I did, Lord Henry. I don’t deserve this,” Baldwin said with his easy grin, though his tone carried genuine humility.
“We did what we were hired to do, sir,” Gian replied quietly. 
“There’s no need, my lord. I would do anything for you and Lady Clara,” Kel said, bowing his head, his sincerity plain in his voice.
Lord Henry’s eyes softened. “The journey was far beyond what anyone could’ve expected. To encounter an Ascendant, and one that hunted us for sport…” He trailed off for a breath, then pressed on. “Please accept this not as coin from a noble to his hirelings, but as a father’s gratitude to the men who protected his family.”
The three exchanged glances, silent understanding passing between them, before finally nodding. “Thank you, Lord Henry.”
Henry leaned back, his gaze lingering on each of them in turn. When he finally spoke, his voice was calm but deliberate. “One last thing. Before you depart, I must ask—would any of you consider staying… and becoming part of my personal guard?”
