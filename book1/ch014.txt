Chapter 14: Fangnotes and Bloomfangs 
It was noon, and the sun hung mercilessly overhead, its heat baking the dirt road until the air shimmered above it. Even the trees bordering the path seemed to droop under the weight of it. Gian slipped into a thin stretch of shade cast by a wagon, grateful for the brief shelter as the caravan halted for rest.
“You alright?” he asked, offering a drink to Kel.
“Leave him be,” Baldwin said as he settled down with a sigh. “He’s just upset he still hasn’t gotten a glimpse of the young lady.”
During breaks, the guard shifts rotated, and this was the start of a fresh round.
“She’s been holed up in that damn carriage since we left,” Kel muttered gloomily. “Even last night, Margaret was the one who brought her food.”
“Well, it’s a big enough carriage. I’m sure she’s fine,” Baldwin replied.
“Hey, young lord,” he added as he noticed Cedric approaching them.
As Baldwin adjusted his pack, a silver coin slipped from his pouch and clinked to the ground.
“What’s that?” Cedric asked curiously.
“This little thing is called money, most important thing in the world,” Baldwin said with a smirk.
“Women are the most important thing in the world,” Kel added, grinning.
“Don’t corrupt the boy, Kel,” Gian cut in as he began sharpening his sword, keeping it in good condition.
Still puzzled, Cedric pointed at the coin. “Why is it a different color?”
“They’ve got different values,” Baldwin explained. “This silver one could buy you a piece of bread. This gold one,” he said, holding another coin up, “gets me a private and comfortable room at a nice inn.”
“Ten copper equals one silver. Ten silver equals one gold,” he added, pulling out a gleaming gold coin.
“Wow! I want that!” Cedric said, eyes wide.
“Not so fast, kid. Go ask your daddy, not me. Or better yet,” Baldwin laughed, “get a Fangnote and hand it over.”
Cedric’s eyes still sparkled from seeing the gold coin. “But… I want one of those!” he said eagerly, pointing at Baldwin’s hand.
Baldwin chuckled, tucking the coin away. “Gold's nice, kid. But let me tell you about something even better—Fangnotes.”
Cedric blinked. “Fang… what?”
Baldwin reached into the inside pocket of his coat and pulled out a thin, smooth strip of treated leather. It shimmered faintly in the sunlight, etched with fine markings and a stamped seal.
“This,” he said, holding it up, “is a Fangnote. Not metal like coin, but don’t be fooled, it’s worth plenty. Made from the hide of a wildborn and treated to last a lifetime. Waterproof, fire-resistant, and damn near impossible to tear.”
Cedric leaned closer, fascinated.
“See the markings?” Baldwin continued, tapping the faint golden inscription. “Each one tells you its value. This one’s worth a full gold. Traders love ’em, easy to carry, hard to fake.”
Kel let out a low whistle. “They say all four kingdoms agreed to use Fangnotes after the Last Pact, right? One currency for everyone.”
“Exactly,” Baldwin said, nodding. “Gold and silver still work, but if you’re making big deals or crossing borders, this is what you want in your pocket.”
Cedric’s eyes widened. “I want one.”
Baldwin snorted. “So does everyone, lad. Now go ask your father for one. You’ve got better odds than robbing me.”
"Anyway, let me tell you how it all started," Baldwin said happily, puffing his chest as he prepared to launch into a story.
"Can't you see he doesn’t understand a thing you just said?" Kel muttered, rolling his eyes.
Unbothered, Baldwin carried on, going into the history of the currency and how it unified the four kingdoms.
Gian, already losing interest, glanced away mid-story. Beyond the wagons, he spotted mercenary David approaching Torren, riding the wolf-shaped shadow conjured by his Echo of Pack. The creature’s form rippled like smoke given muscle, silent paws gliding over the dust. David leaned down and whispered something into the captain’s ear.
The change was immediate. Torren’s posture stiffened.
“Something’s up,” Gian murmured.
“Wh—what?” Baldwin stammered, mid-sentence.
A ripple of unease passed through the resting guards as Lord Henry quietly retreated into the carriage, while one of the guards, Michael, hurried toward them. 
“Young lord, you need to return to the carriage,” Michael said firmly.
“But why? The old man’s still telling me a story!” Cedric protested, pouting.
Torren stood abruptly, voice cutting through the lazy hum of midday.
“Everyone, get ready! A large pack of Bloomfangs is heading this way. We can avoid them — but only if we move now!”
The drowsy stillness shattered. Bedrolls were kicked aside, waterskins snatched up, boots grinding against dirt as the caravan lurched back into motion.
“See you later, young lord!” Baldwin called after Cedric with a grin. “I’ll tell you an even better story next time.”
“You better!” Cedric shouted back, laughing as he ran.
“Stay on your guard, both of you,” Baldwin reminded Gian and Kel as he headed to his post.
“Do you think we’ll finally get to fight something later?” Kel asked, his voice tinged with excitement as he and Gian walked toward the first wagon.
“I hope so,” Gian replied.
“Me too,” Kel said with a grin.

After traveling some distance, Margaret rode up fast, her mount’s paws barely seeming to touch the ground.
“This is bad, sir,” she said, breath controlled but urgent.
A faint tremor ran through the earth beneath them — distant, rhythmic.
Torren’s eyes narrowed. “Report.”
“The good news is we avoided the large Bloomfang pack. But up ahead, we’ll run into a smaller one—and we won’t be able to avoid it,” she reported quickly.
“I see. How many?” he asked.
“I can’t give an exact count, but I’d say between thirty to forty.”
“Hm. That’s manageable. Just keep an eye on the larger pack behind us,” he ordered.
“Yes, sir,” she replied before turning her mount and racing back down the caravan's trail.
Torren turned to the group and shouted, “Everyone, get ready! A pack of Bloomfangs is ahead. They’ll be on us in a few moments!” 
“How many?” Thomas asked, moving closer.
“About thirty to forty. It’s not too big. We’ll intercept them. Just stay in formation and protect the Lord.”
Thomas gave a firm nod. “Understood. I’ll stay close to them.”
Torren glanced toward the Tremorhoof pulling the carriage. Without a word, he gave a silent command, and the beast responded with a nod—there was a bond between them, forged through the connection of Rootseal.
“Alright,” Kel muttered to himself, gripping his axe weapon.
Gian said nothing, but the fire in his eyes betrayed his anticipation.
The first shapes appeared between the trees ahead — low, fast silhouettes weaving through trunks with predatory precision. Then more followed.
The undergrowth rustled violently.
A heartbeat later, the Bloomfangs with their dark green fur burst from the treeline in full charge, paws pounding against the forest floor, vines along their backs thrashing wildly as if alive.
The ground trembled under the weight of dozens of beasts moving as one.
Mercenary Hugo and Guard Michael using the Echo of Bow summoned their longbows in unison, the weapons forming from flickers of light in their grasp. They drew and released with mechanical precision.
Each arrow manifested mid-draw, solidifying as it flew. With every shot, a faint flicker of strain crossed their faces — stamina siphoned into deadly force.
One Bloomfang dropped.
Then another.
But the pack did not slow.
They only grew louder.
The surviving beasts closed the distance in seconds.
Torren stepped forward alone.
With his Echo of Strength, he swung his warhammer in a brutal arc, splitting the air around it. The impact thundered across the clearing as the lead Bloomfang’s skull collapsed inward. The body lifted from the ground before crashing backward into its packmates, tangling fur, vines, and snapping jaws in a violent heap.
Chaos erupted. David summoned two shadowy wolves at his side to support him, while Agnes manifested bestial claws through her Echo and joined the front line with Torren, intercepting the pack. The other three non-echo mercenaries fought just behind them, using swords and axes to hold the line. Hugo stood at the center of their formation, loosing arrows from a summoned shortbow—switching from his earlier longbow for quicker shots.
