Chapter 2: Rare Echo
The afternoon was calm, the dirt path warm beneath Gian and Ren’s feet. Then a shout split the stillness.
A man came sprinting down the main road, drawing every eye as he cried, “They’re back! They’re back!”
Gian and Ren exchanged a confused glance, until Ren’s eyes lit up with realization. 
“They’re here! Gian, your brother and the patrolmen are back! Come on, let’s get to the south gate!”
The two boys broke into a run, pushing through the growing crowd gathering at the town’s wooden entrance. Dust hung thick in the air, stirred by the steady march of two to three dozen weary patrol members. Their faces were lined with exhaustion, their battered armor bearing the marks of three grueling weeks near the Gobe desert’s edge — scouting, culling Wildborn, and hunting for meat.
As Gian and Ren squeezed through the crowd, they caught the tail end of a thrilling story.
“And Warren here,” one patrolman was saying, “finished off the last Bramblehawk with a single blast from his Echo of Fire! You should’ve seen it. Massive bird, wings covered in thorns, screeching like death itself. It dive-bombed us so hard some of the lads couldn’t hear for hours. But we got it. No one died, just a few scrapes and burns.”
Another patrolman clapped a tall man on the back. “And luckiest of us all—Boss Warren! Snagged a Rare Echo from that thorny beast!”
Warren stood in the middle of the group, his red mohawk unmistakable even through the haze of dust. A scar cut across his jaw, but he only grinned, calm and unbothered by the attention.
Gasps and murmurs rippled through the crowd.
“Whoa… our town’s first Rare Echo User,” someone whispered in awe.
Ren glanced sideways at Gian and smirked. “Not a big deal. You’re still the first.”
“Shh.” Gian frowned. “Keep your voice down. Besides… I can’t even control mine.”
Ren opened his mouth to say something more, then just sighed.
At that moment, an old man in the crowd called out, “So, Warren! What kind of Echo did you get?”
“Yeah! Show us!” others chimed in, forming a loose circle around the patrol leader.
Warren, tall and weathered, his armor streaked with soot, held up his left hand. “Someone get me an orange.”
A boy dashed off and quickly returned with an orange, which Warren took in his left hand. After a brief pause, a second orange shimmered into existence in his right, almost identical, just a little smaller, before fading back into nothing. 
“I got the Echo of Duplicate,” Warren said with a grin, soaking in the attention.
The crowd gasped—then erupted into cheers.
Ren’s jaw dropped. “That’s so cool...”
Warren scratched the back of his neck, looking a little embarrassed, though the pride on his face was impossible to hide.  “Still figuring it out. But hey, maybe one day I’ll be duplicating food for the whole town. Extra rations for everyone.”
The crowd roared with laughter and applause.
While the crowd’s excitement built, Gian quietly slipped out of the circle and approached two veteran patrolmen, one tall and muscular, gripping an old axe, the other lean and sipping from a water jug. 
“Hey… where’s Gideon?” he asked.
The muscular man glanced down at him and chuckled. “You’re getting taller, Gian. Not bad. Maybe someday you’ll be as tall as me.”
The lean man wiped his mouth. “Your brother? He’s already gone home. Took a few hits, but nothing your mother can’t handle. Don’t worry.”
Relieved, Gian turned and broke into a run, shouting his thanks over his shoulder.
“Hey, wait up!” Ren called, dashing after him. “Man, I really envy Warren. He’s strong, has a cool Echo... Girls are gonna be all over him now.”
Gian rolled his eyes, but a grin tugged at his lips. “Someday we’ll get Rare Echoes too—famous adventurers, just you wait.” 
“Pfft.” Ren puffed, nearly out of breath. “I don’t even want to join the army. I’m fine just signing up with the patrol here in town. As long as I’ve got money, a Rare Echo, and a cute girlfriend, I’m good.”
Gian laughed. “Instead of thinking about girls, maybe train your stamina. You’re already out of breath.”
“You’re just fast,” Ren groaned. “Anyway, I’m taking this road. See you tomorrow!”
“Yeah. Later!”

If there was one thing Gian was proud of, it was his older brother. Fresh out of the kingdom’s mandatory military training, Gideon had been recommended—and accepted—into the town’s patrol, the youngest to do so.
On his very first expedition, he managed to claim a Verdant Echo from a Wildborn he felled, further sharpening his combat skills. Now at the age of eighteen he led a five-man unit, and many were already saying he had the makings of a future patrol captain. Strong, capable, and well-liked, Gideon was both a warrior and a leader.
After running for a while, Gian finally spotted his home. He burst through the front door, nearly tripping over himself in his rush. The old wind chime hanging from the door clattered behind him, its hollow wooden chimes trailing his entrance. 
Inside, the small home smelled faintly of herbs and cooked rice. His mother, Helen, one of the town’s few healers with a Root-level Echo of Heal, sat by the low bed, her hands glowing faintly as she tended to Gideon. Her dark hair was streaked with early gray and tied back in a simple braid. Her calm hazel eyes, the same warm shade as Gian’s, were focused with practiced care.
On the bed lay Gideon. His sun-darkened skin and calloused hands were proof of long days spent training and working in the fields while not on patrol. Despite the bandages wrapped around his arm and the tired look in his eyes, he flashed Gian a wide grin, full of warmth. 
“Hey, kiddo. How was training today?”
Gian dropped his bag with a soft thud. “Not much. Just more talk. They say we’ll start with body training this year, before any real combat.”
“Enjoy the peace while it lasts,” Gideon said with a chuckle. 
But Gian’s eyes went straight to the crude splint on Gideon’s arm. His brow knit in worry.
“What happened to you?”
“Nothing,” Gideon said with a shrug. “Just broke my right arm. Bramblehawk clipped me mid-air after I pulled Willy out of the way. I’ve had worse.”
“I told you not to accept Willy into your unit. Look what happened.” Gian crossed his arms, giving him a knowing look.
Gideon chuckled. “It’s fine. He’s a little slow, sure, but he’s got a sharp mind for strategy. We just didn’t expect a whole flock of Bramblehawks to swoop in out of nowhere.”
“Stop moving,” Helen said gently but firmly, laying her hand over his arm. A soft golden glow spread from her palm. “You want this to heal straight, don’t you?”
Gideon sighed and leaned back, then glanced at Gian. “Did you hear? Warren got lucky and found a Rare Echo.”
“Yeah,” Gian said with a nod. “Echo of Duplicate. Everyone’s talking about it. If he masters it, it could really change things around here.”
Helen adjusted the cloth on Gideon’s arm. “A Rare Echo with real utility… that’s a blessing. Imagine what we could do if more people had those.”
Gian crossed his arms, a trace of frustration slipping into his voice. “Must be nice. Having a Rare Echo… and being lucky.”
Gideon raised a brow and smirked. “Hey, you’ve got amazing luck, kid. Don’t forget, you won the Harvest Day raffle two years in a row.” 
“That’s because of your Echo,” Helen chuckled as she tied off the final bandage. “But we had to ban you from joining again. The other kids were starting to whisper about rigging.”
“I would’ve won a third time,” Gian said with a small grin. Then his smile faltered. “Still… I’ve got bad luck too. Dad’s accident—it’s my fault. If I hadn’t… if he hadn’t jumped in, then…”
The room fell quiet.
Helen stood slowly, her healing glow fading, and wrapped her arms gently around Gian. “It’s not your fault, sweetheart. Your father did what any parent would do. He saved you because he loved you. It wasn’t about luck. And it wasn’t because of your Echo.”
Gian didn’t answer. He just leaned into the hug.
After a moment, Gideon stood, stretching his now fully healed arm.
“Well, enough of the heavy talk. Look what I brought!” he said, trying to cheer Gian up.
He rummaged through his backpack and pulled out a large cut of meat wrapped in thick green leaves.
“Bramblehawk thigh. A big one. Tonight, we eat until we burst! If I see one more bowl of plain rice from the village fields, I might actually scream.”
Helen raised an eyebrow but smiled softly. “Well, I suppose this is better than the usual plain meals.”
At the sight, Gian’s stomach growled loudly. He laughed, embarrassed. “Alright! I’ll help with the cooking.”
Gideon ruffled his little brother’s hair. “That’s the spirit. You bring the plates, and I’ll bring the stories while I cook.”
