Chapter 27: Bonds and Buckler
After the trio departed the mansion, Torren lingered beside Lord Henry, who remained seated at the long table. His gaze was fixed on the pale morning light seeping through the eastern windows.
“Are you certain about this, my lord? We could press them further. Men like that, brave and loyal, would be invaluable to us,” Torren said quietly.
Lord Henry shook his head, his voice low but firm. “It’s all right. They’ve done enough. I won’t ask more of them… not with what’s coming.”
Torren’s jaw tightened, resolve flickering in his eyes. “Don’t worry, my lord. I’ll do everything in my power to protect your family this time.”
Henry’s expression softened, shadowed by memory. “Eva’s death was not your fault, Torren. No one’s fault. What matters now is the future, my children’s future.”
He rose from his chair and walked toward the window. The first rays of sunlight gilded Tillara’s spires in the distance, though a sharp wind curled through the trees below, carrying with it a chill that clung to the air. “Ready the carriage. I’m going to see Highlord Rosendale.”
Torren hesitated. “Sir… are you certain you wish to meet him now? Wouldn’t it be wiser if I gathered some information first?”
“No,” Lord Henry said, his tone final. “I need to see him as soon as possible. He’s not the kind of man known for patience. We’re already late, and if we delay any longer, something worse might happen.” 
“…Understood, sir. I’ll make the arrangements,” Torren replied, bowing slightly before hurrying off.

As the trio stepped out of the Rosendale mansion, the morning air was cool and crisp, carrying the faint scent of dew from the gardens. Behind them, hurried footsteps rang against the stone steps. They turned to see Clara rushing from the doorway, her breath quick as she tried to catch up. 
“Good thing I managed to catch you,” she said between breaths, smoothing her dress. “Cedric’s still asleep. Why did you leave so early?”
She paused, then bowed her head, her voice softening. “I just wanted to thank you—for everything you’ve done for us.”
All three reacted at once. 
“No, no, my lady. Don’t bow your head to us,” Baldwin said, waving his remaining hand dismissively, though his grin stayed warm. 
“It was an honor to protect you with my life, my lady,” Kel declared, straightening his posture like a knight before his queen.
“There’s no need for that, Lady Clara,” Gian said quietly with a smile.
Then, without warning, Kel stepped forward, cleared his throat, and with a nervous breath, dropped to one knee. “My lady,” he said softly, “I think I’ve fallen for you. If you’ll have me, I’d like to guard your heart—forever.”
Clara’s expression softened, but she shook her head gently. “I’m sorry, Kel… I already have a fiancé. That’s part of the reason we came here. Aside from answering the Highlord’s summons, I was meant to finally meet him.”
Kel’s eyes lit with sudden hope. “Wait, you’ve never seen him before, right?”
She nodded. “Yes. It’s an arrangement proposed by the Highlord to my father, likely to secure loyalty and bind our families closer under his rule.”
“So… you don’t like him, do you?” Kel pressed, desperation edging his voice. “We can run away if you want, my lady!”
Clara giggled softly, a blush touching her cheeks. “We’ve exchanged letters for weeks now… and I think I like him.”
“Noooo!” Kel cried, collapsing to the ground in exaggerated despair.
“It’s alright, my lady. Don’t mind him,” Baldwin said, sighing as he grabbed Kel by the collar and dragged him back to his feet. “Take care of yourself. And please, say goodbye to young Cedric for us.”
“Goodbye, Lady Clara. Stay safe,” Gian said, his voice steady, already turning away.
“Goodbye, everyone,” Clara replied, smiling warmly.
And just before they disappeared down the road, she whispered, almost to herself, “Thank you.”

The tavern was alive with noise—clinking mugs, the smell of roasted meat, and the low hum of laughter and chatter all around. Amidst it all, Kel sat hunched over the table, sniffling into his drink. 
“Would you stop crying? You’re embarrassing us,” Baldwin muttered, pushing away his empty plate. 
“But I miss her,” Kel whined, wiping his nose on his sleeve before gulping down another swig. 
“It’s only been—what—two weeks at most?” Gian said, raising a brow. 
“You guys don’t understand true love,” Kel mumbled dramatically, slamming back the rest of his beer. 
“If that’s the case, why didn’t you accept Lord Henry’s offer?” Baldwin asked, leaning back in his chair. 
“I wanted to be Lady Clara’s personal guard—not Sir Henry’s,” Kel replied stubbornly, arms crossed. 
Baldwin chuckled. “So what are you going to do now?” 
Kel tilted his head. “What about you? Why didn’t you accept his offer?” 
Baldwin raised his left arm and gestured at the empty space where his right had once been. “With one arm like this? There’s no point in adventuring or standing guard anymore. I’ll rest a bit, then head back to my hometown.”
“I see… Take care of yourself, Baldwin,” Gian said sincerely. 
“That’s right, old man,” Kel added with a mischievous grin. “You’re not getting any younger. Better settle down, maybe find yourself a wife.”
“Hah! As if I’d find someone now,” Baldwin snorted, though there was warmth in his eyes.
He turned to Gian. “And you? What’s your plan?”
“I’ll rest and resupply for a week,” Gian replied, lightly tapping the Void Ring on his finger. “After that, I’ll look for another assignment.”
“What? No way,” Kel protested. “It’s our first time in this city. We should explore a bit!”
Seeing Gian’s unamused look, Kel quickly added, “Hey, I’m serious. I said I’d stick with you, didn’t I? Besides, someone’s gotta cook. You can’t survive on jerky alone.”
“Fine,” Gian relented at last. “But we leave in a week.”
Kel rubbed his chin as if negotiating a business deal. “How about... two weeks?”
“One.”
“Twelve days?”
“One.”
“Ten?”
“One.”
“Nine and a half?”
Gian simply stared at him. 
“…Eight?”
“One week.”
Kel threw his hands into the air. “You drive a hard bargain.”
Baldwin chuckled, shaking his head.
“Why the rush?” he asked.
“I want to get stronger,” Gian said simply, his voice calm but resolute. “I need experience.”
“I see… Maybe that’s for the best,” Baldwin murmured, a small smile tugging at his lips.
Baldwin reached under the table and slid a buckler toward Gian—scarred, plain, yet sturdy, bearing the marks of countless battles. “Here. Take it.’”
Gian blinked. “I can’t take this. It’s been with you for so long.”
“That’s exactly why you should,” Baldwin said with a grin. “It saved my hide plenty of times. Maybe it’ll save yours too.”
“…Thanks, Baldwin,” Gian said, bowing his head as he accepted it with both hands.
“Hey, what about me?” Kel piped up, extending his hand with a hopeful grin.
“Here,” Baldwin said, dropping a small faded leather pouch into Kel’s palm.
Kel peeked inside. “A single copper coin?!”
“That pouch has been with me since I was a kid—before I ever became an adventurer,” Baldwin said with a laugh as he rose to his feet.
Kel pouted, clutching the pouch. “That’s so unfair.”
Baldwin ignored him, shaking Gian’s hand firmly. “It was good fighting beside you, lad.”
Then he gave Kel a hearty pat on the back.
“Take care, kids,” he said warmly, raising his remaining hand in a wave as he walked toward the tavern door.

The midday sun shone brightly over Tillara, bathing the cobbled streets in warm light. The stone beneath their boots was worn smooth by decades of carts and caravans, patched in places with mismatched slabs where repairs had been rushed rather than perfected.
Gian and Kel threaded through the midday crowd. Merchants barked their wares from beneath faded canvas awnings—spices from the south, iron tools stamped with the crest of the Kingdom of Lanthea, bundles of dried herbs tied with twine. Children darted between stalls with sticky fingers and quick apologies. Somewhere nearby, a smith’s hammer rang rhythmically against steel.
The smells layered thick in the air—roasted meat and fresh bread mingling with horse dung, stale ale, damp stone, and the sour tang of too many bodies pressed too close together.
Above them, timber-framed buildings leaned inward like conspirators, their upper floors jutting over the street. Laundry fluttered between windows on sagging ropes, while uneven rows of tiled rooftops stretched into the distance. 
“Do we really have to go?” Gian asked, tone flat, his eyes already wary.
“Man, you need to lighten up,” Kel said, practically bouncing beside him. “Did you even see what was in the pouch Lord Henry gave us? No matter what you say, we’re going to the Dice Den.”
“Fine,” Gian muttered. “But just a few bets. I’m not wasting money in some rat-hole gambling den.”
He still remembered the moment they had opened their pouches—more silver and gold than either had ever carried, and even neat stacks of fangnotes.
“We’re rich!” Kel had crowed, spinning in circles like a boy who’d found treasure.
“For now,” Gian had warned. “Don’t come crying to me when you lose it all.”
“Don’t worry,” Kel said now, jingling Baldwin’s old pouch, heavy with a fraction of his newfound wealth. “I’ll let you hold the rest for safekeeping.”
“You’re seriously trusting me with your money in my Void Ring?” Gian asked, arching a brow.
“Honestly, I’m not good with coin. If I keep all of it in my pack, I’ll probably get robbed—or just drop it.” He patted the stiff leather of his newly bought backpack, still smelling of dye.
“Tomorrow we’ll need to stock up on supplies.” Gian said, his tone practical.
“My friend, we’ll handle that. For now, just relax.” Kel grinned, scanning the street. “Now… where’s that Dice Den the bartender mentioned?”
“He said it was around here,” Gian replied, eyes roving over the crooked row of buildings.
Kel glanced around and frowned.  “I must say, I’m quite disappointed. The city isn’t much. Why is it so dirty? It’s just bigger than our town.”
Gian remained silent, but he couldn't disagree. The city looked far dirtier than he'd expected. 
Kel pointed. “That it?”
At the far end of the crooked street squatted a narrow structure wedged between a shuttered alehouse and a pawn broker’s shop. The building leaned slightly, as if tired of standing. A rusted iron sign dangled from a squealing hinge, painted with the image of a one-eyed vulture perched atop a heap of coins. The paint had chipped, leaving the bird’s grin warped and sinister.
Two narrow windows were barred with black iron. Light flickered behind them. A broad-shouldered bouncer stood at the entrance, arms folded across his chest, neck thicker than Kel’s thigh. His gaze moved slowly over passersby, measuring.
From inside came the muted rhythm of a lute, the clatter of dice across wood, and low laughter—thick, knowing laughter, the kind shared by men who believed fortune favored them.
“Looks like it,” Gian said dryly. A place for luck.
