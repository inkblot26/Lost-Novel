Chapter 5: The Burden of the Blade
It had been over a year since that day.
Gian, now taller and sharper around the edges, stood in the dusty town plaza in the center district, gripping his training sword tightly with both hands. Wooden blades clashed in rhythmic unison as he and the other students practiced under the sunset, sweat dripping from their brows and soaking their tunics.
A sharp voice cracked through the air.
“Plant your feet, grip your sword, and slash like you mean it! If you grow up weak, don’t come crying when life knocks you flat!”
Elder Eldon stalked the line of students like a hawk, cane in hand, eyes keen. When someone faltered, the cane smacked their leg or shoulder with a sharp thwack.
Ren groaned mid-swing, dragging his tired arms through the air. 
“Gian, seriously, how many more times do we have to repeat this? It’s getting dark. My arms are about to fall off.”
Gian smiled without turning.
“Didn’t you beg for sword training just a few months ago?”
“Yeah, but no one said we’d be stuck on the same stance for days,” Ren muttered, dragging his wooden blade through the air as if it weighed a hundred pounds.
Elder Eldon’s steps halted behind them.
“If you’ve got the stamina to complain, you’ve got the stamina to train! Ren. Gian. One more hour. Congratulations. Now shut up and fix that posture!”
“What?!” Ren yelped, shooting Gian an apologetic look.
Gian met his eyes briefly, then turned away without a word, adjusting his footing and raising his sword again. The wooden blade felt heavier than ever, but he didn’t let it drop.

It was night by the time Gian and Ren finished their training. Panting and drenched in sweat, they trudged down the quiet dirt road toward their home in the north district.
“My bad, man,” Ren muttered, his voice ragged with exhaustion.
“No worries,” Gian replied, flashing a tired smile. “But you owe me a drink. I’m dying of thirst.”
“Fair. Let’s stop by the shop up ahead—”
A sharp, inhuman screech split the night.
They froze.
Above them, silhouetted against the pale moon, loomed an enormous flying creature. Its wings stretched like black sails, gliding silently before vanishing into the darkness.
Then a thunderous BOOM shattered the night.
A fiery blast struck in the direction of the plaza they had left. Then another. And another.
Houses erupted into flames. Wooden beams splintered and collapsed. Screams tore through the streets as burning debris rained down, smoke choking the air.
“Run!” Gian shouted, grabbing Ren by the arm.
They darted through the panicked crowd, dodging falling houses, trees, and overturned carts. Amid the chaos, Gian glimpsed the attackers—figures in dark violet robes, faces hidden behind jagged black masks. They moved like reapers, cutting down anyone in their path with terrifying precision. Echoes of all kinds crackled in their hands.
“Who are they?!” Ren yelled, barely keeping pace.
“I don’t know! Just keep going!”
A blast struck a house near them, blowing it apart. The explosion threw them off their feet. Gian hit the ground hard, ears ringing, vision spinning. He forced himself up, dragging Ren with him.
They spotted a small cottage still standing, barely holding together, and rushed inside.
But fate wasn’t done.
The roof groaned above them, then caved in.
Gian heard Ren cry out, felt the crushing weight of debris slam down—and then—
Darkness.

Time blurred.
A moment passed. Or a minute. Or an hour.
Gian opened his eyes to smoke, dust, and pain. His body screamed with every breath. Something heavy pinned his leg beneath shattered wood. Blood dripped into his eye, warm against the cold ash on his face.
“Ren…?”
His voice barely worked.
No answer.
Another breath.
“Ren!”
He didn’t move at first. Not because he couldn’t—but because the sound of his own voice felt wrong, distant, like it belonged to someone else.
Slowly, he turned.
A few feet away, half-buried under broken beams, a hand reached out from the rubble.
Still.
Gian stared at it.
It didn’t register at first. Just a hand. Just fingers in dust.
Then the silence around it became heavier.
“No…”
The word came out late, like his mind had to catch up to his mouth.
His throat tightened. Breath stopped halfway in his chest. Tears blurred the ash on his cheeks, but he didn’t wipe them away.
Outside, the fire kept burning like nothing had changed.
With a strained groan, Gian pushed the debris off his leg. The pain hit late too—like everything inside him was delayed. He crawled out, unsteady, half-aware of his own movements.
Then he stood.
Not because he was ready.
But because there was nowhere left to stay.
He stumbled into the burning night.

Chaos engulfed the town.
Flames danced along rooftops, casting long shadows of fear. Townsmen armed with weapons clashed with the masked invaders, but it was clear they were outmatched. One by one, they fell under the precision and brutality of their attackers. 
“Fall back! Regroup at the army barracks by the west district!” shouted one of the academy’s elder instructors, a battle-hardened veteran who had trained the class above Gian’s.
He was locked in a desperate clash with a masked attacker wielding twin blades with an Echo of Lightning as each strike accompanied by bursts of electricity that left the elder reeling. Sparks danced across his armor, and his body jolted with every hit.
He staggered, nearly falling, until two other elder instructors rushed in, parrying the masked attacker’s next blow and forcing him back. Their support gave the veteran a moment to catch his breath.
But just as his voice rang out over the chaos, an arrow whistled through the air—silent and sudden.
It struck him cleanly in the eye.
The veteran fell without a word.
“What’s happening?! Who are they?!” someone screamed.
Elder Eldon emerged from a smoke, his cane was drawn, revealing the hidden blade shaped like a crane’s beak. He swung it in a wide arc, then slammed its tip to the ground, releasing a burst of thick smoke to shroud the retreating defenders.
Yet the two enemies remained relentless.
One of the masked men raised his hand, and the wind itself became blades, slicing through the smoke. Eldon twisted mid-spin, deflecting the worst of it with his cane-sword, but the blast grazed his arm, tearing through cloth and flesh. He winced but held firm.
Behind him, two defenders weren’t as lucky. One was beheaded in an instant, and the other screamed as his legs were slashed open, collapsing to the ground in agony.
Through the swirling chaos, Elder Eldon’s eyes locked onto someone—Gian.
“Gian!” he shouted, rushing toward him, ignoring the blood streaming from his injured arm.
“Where’s Ren?” Elder Eldon asked as he reached Gian, his voice urgent but steady.
Gian didn’t answer. Something in his expression made Eldon pause. He could see the grief in the boy’s eyes and didn’t press further. 
“Can you stand? We need to move,” Eldon said as he deflected an incoming arrow with a flick of his cane-sword. The arrow hissed through the air, trailing a stream of dark purple smoke.
The elder inhaled a whiff and his eyes widened. He grabbed Gian and pulled him back.
“It’s poison. Don’t breathe it in.”
Everything was a blur—fire, screams, steel clashing against steel. Somehow, in the chaos, they managed to put some distance between themselves and the two masked attackers. Maybe someone else had stepped in to hold the line.
But they weren’t safe.
No one was.
The sounds of fighting rang out in every direction. 
Eldon dragged Gian behind a collapsed wall, both of them catching their breath.
“Are you alright? Did you inhale a lot of that poison?”
“No… I’m fine. Just a little dizzy,” Gian replied, wiping blood and ash from his face.
“Alright, just hold on. We need to keep moving. “The north gate’s closer than the west district. You must get out of town. Down the hill. Hide in the woods. Don’t stop.”
Gian frowned. “Shouldn’t we stay with the others? Isn’t it safer in numbers?”
Eldon shook his head grimly. “We can’t take that risk. Whoever can control a Skareth… they’re not here by accident. This isn’t just a bandit raid, it’s a message—or something worse.”
A shadow passed overhead.
Gian looked up.
Its wings spanned over twenty meters, blotting out the moonlight. Golden eyes burned with intelligence. Each powerful wingbeat sent gusts strong enough to topple debris. Metallic feathers gleamed, and its cry split the sky with a sound so sharp it made the earth itself tremble. 
“Don’t worry.” Eldon stepped in front of Gian, gripping his cane-sword with unwavering resolve despite the blood dripping down his arm. “I’ll protect you. No matter what.”
He turned his head slightly.
“We move. Now.”
