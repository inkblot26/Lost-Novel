Chapter 54: Survival Is the Only Law
Before the group could even react, another blazing yellow cross of lightning slammed into a nearby tree. Sparks exploded outward, racing along the rain-soaked bark and instantly frying the Weblings clinging to it. 
Then, one after another, lightning struck more trees in rapid succession. Four in total, striking trees scattered around their position. Each impact sent a surge of electricity rippling through the rain-soaked wood, killing clusters of Weblings in a crackling instant. Only a handful of trees remained untouched, the nest of creatures nearly wiped out. 
“Move! Now!” Thompson barked, seizing the moment as he pushed forward through the rain-soaked ground. He grabbed Jones—his arm gone, blood still dripping—and dragged him forward. Kel snapped out of his daze and rushed to help, slinging Jones’ other side over his shoulder.
On the collapsing perimeter, Oldman rushed to the side of the exhausted Jimmy, who was barely standing after holding the Weblings at bay for so long. “Gian! Help me with him!” Oldman shouted. After struggling free from the webs, Gian finally managed to sprint over and grabbed Jimmy’s other side to steady him. 
“Donny, take the front! Jessica, guard the rear!” Thompson ordered sharply as his eyes flicked briefly to Nathan’s body—now cocooned in web and pinned lifelessly to a tree.
I’m sorry… all of you. The thought was bitter, but there was no time to grieve.
“Go!” Thompson urged, his voice cutting through the storm as the team began moving, leaving behind the scorched trees, the fallen Duskfang, and the Webling nest.
As they pressed deeper into the forest, Gian risked one last glance back. Through the curtain of rain, he saw the faint glow of the twin lightning crosses still crackling on the fallen Dustfang, their light slowly fading into darkness.

Moments later, the rain stopped. Kel slumped against the base of a trunk, panting. “Is… this tree safe?” he asked, glancing nervously upward.
“You better hope so,” Jessica replied tiredly, her voice flat from fatigue.
“Don’t worry,” Oldman said as he removed his gambler’s hat, running a hand over the old scar on his forehead and through his damp hair. “I sent Tata up into the branches to keep watch. If anything moves, we’ll know.” His gaze lifted briefly toward the dense canopy, trusting the little driftmouse’s keen ears and sharp nose.
Nearby, just beside the tree, Donny and Gian worked to wrap the torn stump of Jones’ missing arm. The soldier clenched his teeth, a muffled groan escaping with every touch.
“Hold him still,” Oldman said as he sat cross-legged in front of Jones. Taking a deep breath, he exhaled slowly, and tiny yellow-green light of fireflies began to swirl gently around him. They floated toward Jones’ wound, glowing faintly as they began to close the worst of the bleeding.
A few feet away, Jimmy lay unconscious, completely drained from overusing his Echo while fending off the Weblings. Thompson stood a short distance from the group at the edge of their resting spot, fists clenched tight, his sharp eyes scanning the forest even as his thoughts weighed heavily.
“Don’t beat yourself up, Sergeant,” Jessica said softly, sensing the weight on his shoulders.
Donny stood as soon as Oldman began healing Jones. He approached Thompson with quiet resolve. “All of us knew the risks when we set foot in the Unknown. That’s our duty as soldiers of the outpost.”
Without waiting for a reply, Donny added, “I’ll take perimeter duty sir,” and walked off, keeping just within visible range as he moved between the trees.
Kel stared at the ground, guilt shadowing his face. “If anyone should feel ashamed, it’s us, sir…” he muttered.
“You came here to help us,” Gian added quietly, still holding Jones steady. “I’m sorry.”
“It’s not your fault,” Thompson said after a pause, his voice low. “We were following orders. It’s just…” His gaze darkened. “What if we had fought the Duskfang from the very start? Maybe… the outcome would’ve been different.”
“We’ll never know that now, sir,” Jessica replied gently.
“You’re right,” Thompson said with a tired sigh. “Rest while you can. I’ll cover the other half of the perimeter.” Without another word, he turned and walked off into the dim forest, leaving the group in silence.

Finally given a moment to breathe, Oldman leaned back against the tree and closed his eyes, letting the exhaustion sink in.
“How’s Tata?” Gian asked, glancing up into the branches, though he couldn’t see the little creature.
“No problem,” Oldman murmured, still resting. “No Wildborn nearby. Tata would’ve squeaked by now if there was.”
Kel, sitting cross-legged and still catching his breath, frowned. “So… what was that yellow cross thing? The lightning. How did it even happen?”
No one answered at first. The silence stretched until Jessica finally spoke. 
“I’m not sure… but whatever it was, it’s the reason we survived.”
“Could it have something to do with the military?” Gian asked, curiosity sharpening his tone.
Jessica shook her head slightly. “I don’t know. Honestly, this is only the third time our squad’s ever gone into the Doom Forest. We’re a new unit, just formed a few months ago.”
She paused, then added, “This is my first squad. But I heard Sergeant Thompson and Donny served together before. When he got promoted, this squad was put together. Maybe he knows something.”
Then they saw Sergeant Thompson come back.
“Nothing’s out there.”
“Don’t worry, sir. I sent my partner up the tree. Now that the rain’s stopped, he can help guard at least the area near us,” Oldman said.
Thompson nodded. “Thanks. We’ll rotate at least one person to walk the perimeter, close enough to stay in sight from here.” 
“Yes, sir. I’ll go after Donny,” Jessica said, lying down on the ground.
“I can switch first if you’re tired,” Kel offered, glancing at her.
“Thanks for the offer, but no need. I can manage. You can go after my turn.” Jessica winked, and Kel once again blushed red.
Gian summoned three Nightlily and let Jones and Jimmy used it to rest, leaving the last for whoever needed it during the rotations.
“Um, so, Sir Thompson… do you have any idea what that yellow thing was?” Kel asked, curiosity clear in his voice. The others leaned in slightly, eager to hear the answer.
“I’m not a hundred percent sure,” Thompson said quietly, “but I’ve heard the stories.”
“Stories?” Kel asked.
“It’s probably from that Pathfinder—Code A-26. I’ve never seen him myself, but he’s a living legend.”	
“Wow. Why?” Kel asked, even more intrigued.
“It’s strictly confidential, I’m sorry. But I can tell you this much. The Pathfinder’s job is difficult and deadly. They travel into the Unknown, investigating what others fear to face. Survival is the only law in the Unknown. No one weak survives in their line of work,” Thompson replied.
He glanced at the sky before continuing. “And out of all the Pathfinders, he’s famous for being insanely strong. He rarely returns to the outpost. I heard he has the Echo of Lightning and the Echo of Weapon in the form of a bow … and somehow managed to combine them.”
“Are you saying… that was an arrow?” Gian asked, amazed.
“Probably. It is said that his signature is cross-shaped yellow lightning, so I figured it was him who helped us. I just don’t know what he’s doing here, in the Doom Forest.”
“What do you mean?” Kel pressed.
“Haha, nothing. It’s something you shouldn’t know. Honestly, even I’m not supposed to know. I only found out because my father is a high-ranking officer in the army,” Thompson chuckled.
“So he’s that strong… maybe he’s an Echo Master,” Gian muttered. 
“I think he’s a level even higher than that,” Thompson said.
“What do you mean?” Kel asked again.
“Well, as you might know, there are two tiers for those with Echoes. First are the Echo Users — people who can basically wield their Echo abilities. Then there are the Echo Masters — highly skilled individuals with great control over their Echoes, capable of shaping their Echoes into countless forms and applications, rather than being limited to one or two techniques. For example my usage of the Echo of Water, which only forms like this.” Thompson created a spinning, star-shaped construct of water.
“Or take those with the Echo of Pact, who can summon translucent animals in great number, or those with the Echo of Tame, who can bind more than one Wildborn,” he added. 
Kel glanced at Oldman.
“What are you looking at? It’s hard to bond two Wildborns, you know. I'll never reach that level in my lifetime.” Oldman muttered.
Gian remembered Elder Eldon wielding his sword cane that night, smoke trailing from each slash, the first Echo Master he had ever seen fight. He tried to push the memory of that tragic night from his mind. 
“So, what’s the higher level? I never heard about it in the mandatory training,” he asked, looking at Thompson. 
“Well, it’s not exactly top secret, but it’s not common knowledge to normal people. The last tier is called Echo Weaver. They’re transcendent wielders whose Echoes have evolved beyond normal limits — maybe even fusing different Echoes together. But I’m not sure myself, since I haven’t even reached the Echo Master level.” 
“So… since he combined the Echo of Weapon and Lightning, he’s a Weaver?” Kel asked, excitement creeping into his voice.
“Maybe. I wouldn’t even be shocked if he had another Echo that allowed him to pinpoint our location, even through that heavy downpour,” Thompson added. 
“Wow,” Kel breathed out.
“Well, he's a legend, so plenty of stories about him are probably exaggerated," Thompson said with a faint laugh. "Still, every legend starts somewhere. What I do know is that he's formidable, and he's contributed greatly to the military.”
“Damn, I’m just weak,” Jessica laughed from the side.
“Same here,” Oldman agreed.
“Well, only the truly gifted ever make it to Echo Master. But Echo Weaver? That’s… something beyond comprehension,” Thompson said with a faint shake of his head.
Gian stayed silent, his thoughts heavy. Can I even hope to master my own Echo…?

When the sun finally rose, its rays pierced through the dense canopy above, scattering streaks of golden light across the damp forest floor. The group decided it was time to return to their previous camp.
Jimmy, now fully rested, sent out his rabbits as scouts, while Oldman led at the front with Tata perched on his shoulder, sniffing and sensing for danger.
When they arrived back at the abandoned campsite clearing, they quickly scanned the area, then began packing the supplies they had left behind.
“Good thing you two spotted the Duskfang’s mist in time,” Thompson said.
“Yeah… many more of us would’ve died if you hadn’t,” Oldman added, glancing at Gian and Kel.
“Well, you should thank Gian,” Kel said, pointing at him with a smirk. “We’re lucky he felt the urge to leak. Otherwise, we never would’ve noticed it.” 
“It was just coincidence,” Gian muttered, glancing toward the direction where the mist had crept through the trees the previous night. Luck has nothing to do with it, he thought. 
“Anyway, Gian, take these. You might need them,” Thompson said, handing over two folded tents.
Gian stored them in his Void Ring, then shook Thompson’s hand. “Thank you for everything, Sergeant.”
“Good luck to you three,” Thompson replied firmly.
“Will you be okay?” Kel asked Jessica, his gaze shifting between the remaining soldiers—Sergeant Thompson, Donny, Jimmy, Jones, and Jessica—who had escorted them this far.
“Don’t worry about us. You three are the ones heading deeper into the Doom Forest,” Jones laughed, even as his missing arm was wrapped in bandages.
“Thanks for your concern, Mr. Gentleman,” Jessica added with a playful wink.
“Thanks,” Oldman said simply to Thompson.
“If you manage to reach Graylatch, send a messenger bird back. The outpost needs to know what happened,” Thompson said.
“Hopefully the village is still there,” Oldman replied.
With that, the trio finally parted ways with the soldiers, heading further east into the Unknown. Thompson watched them for a moment, then turned to his squad. “Alright, let’s move. We’re heading back.”

An hour later, the trio continued deeper through the towering trees, Tata scouting ahead along the path. Kel kept glancing back nervously.
“Would you stop that?” Oldman said, his tone edged with impatience.
“What if Private Jessica and the others encounter Wildborns? She could be in danger,” Kel muttered.
Oldman rolled his eyes. “Well, you can always run back. We’ll wait here.” Then he suddenly raised a hand, stopping Gian and Kel in their tracks. 
“What is it?” Gian whispered, scanning the surroundings.
“Tata heard something up ahead,” Oldman warned, crouching low as the driftmouse darted back to his side. “Probably a group of Apelangs heading this way,” he added in a low voice. 
“Let’s hide in that bush,” Gian suggested, pointing to a dense patch of undergrowth beside the path.
The three crouched in silence as a cluster of agile Apelangs swung from branch to branch overhead. When the path was finally clear, they continued onward.
