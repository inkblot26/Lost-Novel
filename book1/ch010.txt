Chapter 10: In the Ashes' Wake
A faint warmth stirred against the cold.
Gian’s senses began to return—first the sound of rubble crumbling nearby, then the heat from scorched stone, and finally… the touch of something gentle. Familiar.
His breath hitched. Slowly, his gaze focused.
She was there.
His mother.
Lying beside him, her face was pale and still, eyes closed in a peace that seemed out of place here. Her hand rested over the wound on his stomach, now fully healed, her sleeve soaked in blood, yet her palm pressed firmly and protectively, as if trying to absorb the pain into herself.
His lips parted, but no sound emerged.
“M-Mother…” he rasped, throat raw and cracked.
She didn’t respond. Didn’t move.
She wasn’t breathing. She was gone.
Yet somehow… before she left, her final act reached for him. To hold him. To shield him.
Tears welled in Gian’s eyes. He didn’t scream. He couldn’t. Grief tightened around his chest like iron. Slowly, with trembling fingers, he reached up and laid his hand over hers—cold now, but still there.
Still holding on.

After what felt like hours, with time having lost all meaning, Gian finally looked up.
The town was quiet.
Too quiet.
A silence so unnatural it felt as if the world itself had paused to mourn. No screams, no fighting. Only the faint crackle of smoldering debris and the occasional clatter of stone settling in the ruins.
He had laid his mother and Elder Eldon to rest in a narrow alley behind a collapsed house, the only shelter left to give. There was no ceremony. Only stillness.
And then, he walked.
Toward the plaza. Toward the center of it all.
As he moved through the broken streets, the aftermath revealed itself. Shattered buildings leaned like broken bones. Debris littered the road. Blood stained the stones.
Injured townsfolk staggered or crawled, some helping others with shaky hands. Children cried out for missing parents. Others sat in silence, dazed and vacant-eyed, their souls seemingly left behind in the chaos. The scent of ash and iron clung to everything.
Gian kept walking, each step heavier than the last.
At last, Gian reached what remained of the plaza, though the word no longer felt fitting. It bore no resemblance to what it once was. The ground was scorched and shattered, the once-familiar stonework buried beneath rubble and ash.
As he stepped closer, he began to hear it—cries, wails, and shouts. Townsfolk calling out the names of loved ones, their voices breaking as they stumbled through the wreckage.
And then he saw it too.
Across the plaza, the dead hung crucified on twisted spikes of black, liquefied material—hardened into warped, unnatural forms.
Some bodies were little more than pulp—limbs twisted at impossible angles, bones jutting through torn flesh, faces obliterated by blunt force or shredded by metallic feathers.
Others were charred husks, their skin blistered and split open, revealing muscle and sinew beneath scorched armor. The stench was unbearable—a sickening blend of burned hair, blood, and smoke.
On some of the crucifixes, limbs were missing. Heads were gone. The dead were displayed like warnings, silent and brutal messages left behind by monsters.
The ink-spires rose tall and terrible, like monuments to suffering.
Silence hung heavy, thick with death.
Gian couldn’t move.
Couldn’t breathe.
Then, his eyes locked on a single body.
One he knew.
Only the upper half remained.
The lower limbs were gone, severed at the waist, the torso nailed to a black crucifix.
His breath caught.
“Gi... Gideon,” he whispered, voice cracked and hollow.
No scream came.
No sob.
Just silence.
Stunned.
Shattered.
Lost.
