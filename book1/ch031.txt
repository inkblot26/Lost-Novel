Chapter 31: The Faces We Wear
The morning rain fell in a steady drizzle, pattering against the roof and dripping from the eaves. A faint mist clung to the fields outside, blurring the horizon into soft grays. Inside the small house, light filtered through thin curtains, throwing a warm glow across worn floorboards and the rough-hewn table where Kel slouched half-awake, chewing on a crust of bread.
“You two should stay here. I'm sure Ronald's still pissed about what happened yesterday," Oldman said, tugging a brown vest over his white long-sleeved shirt.
Kel yawned, lazy-eyed. “Where are you going?”
“I need to check on Tata. While I’m out, I’ll grab some food and supplies, since you two are sticking around. Need anything?”
"Yeah, we need to resupply. First aid kit, some tools, and a few other things," Kel said, giving Oldman a quick once-over from head to toe.
“Alright. Write it down and hand me some fangnotes.” Oldman waited by the door, rolling his shoulders.
Gian frowned. “Is it safe for you to go back out? Didn’t they see you too?”
Oldman waved a hand dismissively. "I know this place like the back of my hand. Besides, they didn’t get a good look at me. My favorite hat covered most of my face, and I wasn’t wearing this vest." He chuckled, then noticed Kel staring at him a little too long.
"Okay… just be careful," Gian said, handing him a folded paper and a pouch of fangnotes.
Oldman glanced at the list and raised an eyebrow. "You boys are loaded. I can get most of this, but weapons and gear. That’s another story. Also, why’s he staring at me like that?" He jabbed a thumb toward Kel.
Kel smirked. “Gian, maybe scratch the clothes section.”
"Why?" Gian asked, puzzled.
Oldman arched a brow.
“Look at him,” Kel said, pointing. “He’s dressed like a cowboy. What if he buys us the same outfit? I’m not wearing that.”
"What’s a cowboy?" Gian asked.
Kel blinked. “…You seriously don’t remember Baldwin’s story? The Tale of the Phantom Rider? The one he told Cedric at lunch?”
Gian shook his head. Oldman just looked amused.
“Anyway,” Kel continued, “Long story short, there was this legendary wanderer everyone called the Cowboy. Always wore boots, black pants, a white shirt, brown vest, leather hat. Just like Oldman. He fought the Grim Reaper, saved his friends, but died doing it. Now it's considered bad luck to dress like that. Superstition, according to Baldwin.”
Oldman scoffed. “Never heard that tale. Don’t give a damn either. I like this vest and gambler hat. Makes me look manly.” He puffed out his chest, striking a pose.
Gian ignored Kel and turned to Oldman. "About the weapons and gear, what’s the issue?"
Oldman’s expression turned serious. "Highlord Richard tightened regulations after some farmers tried to revolt five months ago. You can still buy gear, but you have to register your real name and explain your reason. Especially weapons.”
He tapped the list. “These arrows? Best not to leave a paper trail. I’ll find another way, but it might take time.”
“That’s fine,” Gian said. “There’s no rush.”
Oldman nodded. “Alright. I’ll be back by afternoon. You two lay low here.”
He pulled the brim of his brown gambler hat low, shadowing the scar across his forehead, and stepped out into the drizzle. The door shut with a dull thud, leaving only the steady tapping of rain against the roof and the faint hiss of water running off the eaves.
"Well, at least he doesn’t have a lasso," Kel muttered as they watched him go.

“Thomas, Edmund, you don’t need to follow me. I’ll be safe. We’re in the city, and I’m only meeting Lord Ronald at the restaurant,” Clara said. Her steps quick and precise on the damp cobblestones of the central district, where carriage wheels hissed through shallow puddles and merchants hurried to shield their wares from the lingering drizzle. Her voice was steady, but her grip on her satchel was tighter than she realized. 
“That’s exactly what worries me,” Edmund muttered. Thomas caught it and nudged him with a sharp elbow to keep him quiet.
“My lady, are you sure you’re ready to meet Lord Ronald?” Thomas asked softly, matching her stride while Edmund kept a watchful eye behind. His tone held genuine concern, especially since it had only been a few days since Lady Evangeline’s death.
Clara hesitated, her pace slowing. Her expression remained composed, though her eyes flickered with distance. “I know what you mean, Thomas,” she said at last. “But my mother always told me to be strong. So, I will be. If this marriage happens, it could help my father… strengthen our family’s tie to the main house.”
Her voice was calm, yet beneath it lingered a sadness she would not allow to surface.
“But my lady—” Thomas began, only for Clara to cut him off with a faint, fragile smile.
“Don’t worry. He’s been kind to me in his letters. Perhaps he isn’t like the stories you’ve heard. Lords always attract rumors. I’ll meet him first, then decide.”
The three slowed as the restaurant loomed ahead, grand and unwelcoming. Its tall, arched windows gleamed with light, stone pillars etched with fine carvings rising like watchful sentinels. Above the doorway hung a golden crest, polished to perfection. From within drifted the rich scent of herbs and grilled meats, clashing with the faint odor of rain-soaked streets.
“My lady, we’ll stay close,” Edmund promised, his hand brushing the hilt at his side. “Call, and with my Echo, I’ll hear you no matter what.”
Clara gave him a grateful nod. “Thank you, Edmund.”
“Take care, my lady,” Thomas said, bowing his head slightly.
“I will,” she whispered, and with a breath she stepped forward. The ornate doors opened, spilling warm light across her figure before closing behind her, leaving Thomas and Edmund in the cool gray of the street.
“Are you Lady Clara?” the waiter at the entrance asked with practiced courtesy.
“Yes,” Clara replied, steadying her voice.
“Please, this way. Lord Ronald has reserved the finest room for your meeting,” he said with a polished smile, leading her up the sweeping staircase.
They climbed to the third floor, where only velvet-carpeted halls and gilded doors marked the exclusive rooms. At the end, the waiter opened one, bowing her inside.
The scent of roasted meat and spiced wine hung heavy in the air, thick enough to taste, mingling with cloying perfume and the faint grease-smoke trapped beneath the ceiling.
Clara’s gaze caught on a bloated nobleman with slicked-back gray hair, laughter spilling from him like cheap ale. Two women lounged against him, draped in garish silks that revealed more than they concealed. They giggled, feeding on his attention as they plucked morsels from overfilled plates. Neither even glanced at Clara.
“Sir, Lady Clara has arrived,” the waiter announced before bowing and withdrawing, the door shutting quietly behind him.
“Good day, Lord Ronald,” Clara said with a small, respectful bow, her eyes briefly flicking to the women still clinging to him.
“Clara! At last, we meet.” Ronald’s grin stretched wide. “Even lovelier than the letters said. Elegant too. One would hardly guess you’re only half-noble.” He slapped his thigh and gestured to the chair across from him.
“Girls, please leave for a while. I’ll call for you later,” Ronald said with a grin.
With sultry smiles, they rose, their perfume lingering as they drifted out. The moment the latch clicked, Ronald’s grin soured.
“Ugly things,” he sneered. “Past twenty, can you believe it? I like them younger. The younger, the better.” His laughter boomed against the walls, vulgar and unrestrained. His eyes gleamed as they settled on Clara. “But don’t worry, Clara. You’re still young. We’ll have plenty of fun.”
Clara lowered her gaze, her face composed but her stomach twisted. Her hands rested neatly in her lap, though her thumb pressed hard into the carved wood of her chair until her knuckles whitened.
“I heard your mother died?” Ronald asked casually as he tore a greasy wing from its bone, juices dripping down his fingers.
“Yes, Lord Ronald,” Clara said quietly.
“Well, that’s something we share. I lost my mother too. Died giving birth to me!” He barked out a laugh, spraying crumbs across the table.
Clara forced a polite smile. “I… I didn’t know that, my lord.”
“What? You didn’t? Tavin didn’t tell you?” Ronald chuckled, amused by her confusion.
“I’m sorry, my lord, but… who is Tavin?” she asked, brows knitting faintly.
“My assistant. Handles everything for me. Not as good as Jonas, my last one, but good enough. He wrote our letters back and forth. I’ve got better things to do.”
Clara’s breath caught. Her heart sank like a stone. The letters—the poems she had cherished, the kind words she had thought his—were nothing but the work of a stranger. Her fingers curled tighter, nails digging shallow grooves into the underside of the chair.
“I-I see… so it wasn’t you, my lord…” she murmured, barely holding her voice steady.
Ronald waved a dismissive hand, his mouth still full. “Anyway, on to business. Marriage. My brother’s nagging me to finish it soon. Honestly, what’s the rush?”
He chuckled again, and the sound was more grotesque than the chewing of his meat.

Outside, across the street from the restaurant, Edmund stood on the opposite curb like a coiled spring—fists clenched, jaw tight, eyes burning with a quiet fury. The lively sounds of the market felt obscene against the stifling room above, where Clara sat alone with that bloated noble.  Through his Echo he could hear fragments—Ronald’s oily laughter, the scrape of a chair, Clara’s measured replies—and each shard of sound made his teeth grind harder.
“Edmund, what’s going on with Lady Clara?” Thomas asked softly, reading the tension on his friend’s face.
“That scumbag deserves to die,” Edmund muttered, low and cold. He fed Thomas the barest outline of what he’d picked up. Thomas’s expression hardened, but he kept his voice level. “Don’t do anything rash. Wait until she leaves, then we tell Lord Henry. Keeping her safe comes first.”
Edmund didn't respond immediately. His eyes remained fixed on the third-floor window of the restaurant as he continued to listen through his Echo, carefully tuning into Clara and Ronald’s conversation.
“What’s happening now?” Thomas asked again, more urgently.
“Lady Clara… she’s just playing along,” Edmund said, his voice tense. “She’s staying calm. Trying not to provoke him.”
Thomas gave a slow nod, his jaw clenched. “Good. She’s smart. Let’s stay ready.” 

Meanwhile, Lord Henry rode with Torren in a modest, unmarked carriage, its wheels softly rolling through the quieter edge of the western district. The rain had eased into a mist, blurring the outlines of humble homes and shuttered shops, the air carrying the faint smell of wet earth and chimney smoke.
The carriage slowed to a halt before a narrow lane’s end, where a plain, timeworn house stood tucked against the corner.
“Is this the place, Torren?” Henry asked, his voice low and steady, eyes scanning the surroundings.
“Yes, my lord,” Torren replied. “This was the location she gave me.”
Henry gave a single nod. “Alright. Let’s go.”
He stepped down from the carriage, the damp stones tapping beneath his polished boots. He stepped onto the porch and gently pushed the door open. 
Inside, the hush was almost reverent. The air was warm, carrying the faint fragrance of lavender layered with the dry musk of parchment. Candlelight wavered across worn wooden floors, throwing long, restless shadows. Further in, one room remained lit, its door ajar and the glow within soft and golden.
As Henry entered, he saw her. A lone woman sat by the window, her figure etched against the pale, mist-washed daylight. She gazed through the glass at the street below, where workers hurried to finish repairs and vendors rushed to pack up their stalls before another downpour.
Then, sensing their presence, she turned.
Her eyes met his, and she smiled, a quiet, knowing smile that spoke of patience and long-awaited meetings. “It’s nice to finally meet you, Sir Henry.”
Henry removed his hat, inclining it with subtle courtesy. His voice matched her calm grace, cool but respectful. 
“Good day, Lady Rose.”
