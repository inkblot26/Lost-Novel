Chapter 52: Halfway to Graylatch
Their journey continued for several more days. Sometimes they managed to avoid Wildborn entirely. Other times, Thompson’s soldiers sent an advance party to silently deal with approaching threats. And on a few occasions, even Gian’s group joined the fights.
On the tenth day, they were finally halfway to Graylatch Village. As night settled in beneath the forest canopy, the group rested in a small clearing after a simple dinner.
“Well, this is sad, but…” Kel rubbed the back of his neck, looking at Jessica. “I think I’m gonna miss you, Private Jessica.”
Jessica laughed softly. “You’re quite the charmer, aren’t you?”
“Tomorrow morning, we’ll be turning back to Outpost Two,” Thompson said, glancing at Gian and Oldman, who was absently rolling the Direstone in his hand. “I hope you find what you’re looking for out there.”
“Thanks for helping us get this far,” Gian replied earnestly.
“No need to thank us. We’re used to this, patrolling the Doom Forest, clearing out Wildborn for training and to thin their numbers,” Thompson said with a faint grin. 
“Though, let’s be honest, there’s no end to Wildborn,” Jimmy added with a tired laugh as he sat near the edge of the clearing after returning from scouting with Baron.
“Yeah,” Baron said, dissolving his summoned bow on his shoulder. “I sometimes wonder if humanity will ever reach the deepest parts of the Unknown.”
“Personally, I think it’s possible,” Oldman said, his tone suddenly serious. “But it would take mountains of corpses to get there. Too many would die for even a slim chance.”
“That’s assuming the kingdom leaders would ever unite for such a suicidal risk,” Thompson added grimly.
Kel, shifting uncomfortably, glanced at Gian. “Hey, uh… how will we even know where Graylatch is? It’s not like there’s a signpost out here.”
The group laughed softly. Oldman shook his head. “You really aren’t very perceptive. We’ve been walking for days already and you’re only asking now?”
“Here,” Thompson said, pulling a rolled parchment from the pack resting beside him and handing it to Kel across the campfire.
Kel blinked. “What’s this?”
“A military map of this section of the Unknown,” Thompson explained. “It’s not perfect. It just marks noteworthy landscapes, known Wildborn territories, and a few lesser villages. Things change out here constantly, but this is the latest version, compiled from Pathfinder reports about three months ago. It’s yours.” 
“Wow, thanks. I didn’t think you’d actually agree if I asked,” Oldman chuckled.
“No need to. Before I left the outpost, Captain Kirk already authorized me to share it with you,” Thompson said matter-of-factly.
Kel shot Gian a look. “You didn’t know that either, right?”
Gian nodded. “I figured the military would have their own maps from their surveys. Still, like Oldman, I wasn’t sure Thompson would actually share them before we part ways tomorrow.” 
“And what if they didn’t?” Kel frowned.
Gian shrugged. “I was hoping Oldman’s experience as a former mercenary would at least give us a rough idea of the area.”
Oldman let out a dry laugh. “Sorry to disappoint, kid. It’s been too long. And besides, if there’s one rule about the Unknown, it’s this—never trust what you think you know out here.”
Kel tilted his head, confused. “What’s that supposed to mean?”
Thompson answered for him. “It means territories shift. Wildborn migrations change the land. New threats appear overnight. Lesser villages rise and fall. Even the landscape itself doesn’t always stay the same. You’ll never know for sure.”
“Still,” Oldman said, unrolling the map over his knee to study it briefly before handing it over, “having this helps. Thanks again.”
“Store it in your Void Ring. It’s important,” he told Gian.
Gian nodded silently and slipped the scroll away for safekeeping.

Inside Captain Kirk’s office in the main command building of Outpost Two, he was just about to leave for his quarters when a sharp knock came at the door.
“Enter,” Kirk said.
A soldier stepped inside, slightly out of breath from haste. He saluted smartly. “Sir, we’ve just received a report from a Pathfinder returning from his latest exploration in the Unknown.” He handed over a folded letter.
Kirk gets it. “Who is it from? And why the urgency?”
“It’s from Pathfinder A-26, sir,” the soldier replied. “He briefly mentioned something connected to the incident at Graylatch Village. I thought you’d want to know right away.”
“Is he returning to the outpost now?” Kirk asked.
“Unfortunately, he didn’t say, sir. He only mentioned that he’s resting in the Doom Forest after coming out of the Death Zone.” 
“I see. You may go.”
“Yes, sir.” The soldier saluted again and left the office.
Kirk sat back behind his wooden desk and read the letter under the dim lamplight. “So… that’s what happened,” he muttered. “This information is vital to them, but it’s troublesome. There’s no direct way to get it to them.”
He stared at the desk, his fingers tapping against the wood as he thought. I don’t have any spare Echo specialists to relay this quickly. The best I can do is send multiple messenger birds and hope at least one makes it through.
With a sigh of resolve, Kirk pulled out parchment and ink, his expression hardening. Then, Captain Kirk began to write. 

Deep in the night, one of the two patrol soldiers, a non-echo, walked the outer edge of the camp’s perimeter, moving between trees in silence. In the dense grass, something shifted, moving with a predator’s grace, unnoticed. Then, in an instant, it struck.
A massive coil wrapped around him, crushing the air from his lungs. A pale, sickly mist seeped into his mouth and nose, choking him before he could even scream. His eyes widened in terror as the coils tightened, bones snapping one by one. Then, the creature’s head emerged from the darkness, its hooded form revealing a gaping maw lined with glistening fangs. With one final constriction, it unhinged its jaws and swallowed him whole.

Gian woke in the middle of the night, lying on the Nightlily bed at the center of the clearing, feeling the urge to relieve himself. As he stood, his movement stirred Kel awake.
“What are you doing?” Kel mumbled, half-asleep.
“I just need to pee,” Gian whispered, stepping away.
“Wait for me. I need to take a leak too,” Kel said, getting up to follow.
After walking a short distance beyond the edge of the clearing, they both froze. A strange mist was creeping silently from one direction, curling low over the ground.
“What…?” Kel rubbed his eyes, trying to see more clearly.
“Kel, let’s go back. I don’t think that’s normal,” Gian urged, his voice low but tense.
They quickly returned toward the clearing, where the rest of the group lay sleeping around the dim campfire. Jimmy was on watch, leaning casually against a tree while whistling softly.
“Hey, you’re back,” Jimmy greeted, noticing them approach.
“Jimmy, have you heard anything from the patrols?” Gian asked, glancing back at the mist.
“Nothing yet. Those two are still making rounds on opposite sides of the perimeter. Why?” Jimmy raised a brow.
“We saw… that.” Kel pointed toward the creeping haze. “Is that normal?”
“Mist? There shouldn’t be—” Jimmy cut himself off mid-sentence. He stared toward the mist creeping in from between the trees, and his expression instantly darkened.
“This is bad,” he said sharply. “Wake the others, but quietly. Don’t make a sound. We don’t want to alert it.”
As he spoke, he summoned two shadowy rabbits, each darting silently into opposite sides of the forest beyond the clearing toward where the two patrols should have been.
“Why? What is it?” Gian asked, already tense.
Jimmy’s voice was grim. “I hope I’m wrong… but if I’m right, that mist belongs to an Ascendant Wildborn—the Duskfang.”
Realizing the gravity of his words, Gian hurried to help Kel wake the others in silence.

After storing the three Nightlily back into his void storage, Gian crept closer to the others. Everyone was awake now, gathered low near the center of the clearing, keeping silent so as not to draw attention from whatever lurked beyond the trees.
“How is it, Jimmy? Any word from them?” Gian heard Thompson’s low voice as he questioned Jimmy about the two scouts.
“No, sir,” Jimmy replied, his tone tight. “My rabbits are still out there, but I haven’t received any letters back. They should’ve returned by now, just like our routine.”
He summoned another shadowy rabbit beside him, its form flickering briefly before solidifying. He gave it a silent command, sending it toward the creeping mist.
“Even Baron?” Thompson pressed.
Jimmy hesitated, then shook his head grimly.
“Don’t you think he’s…?” Jones muttered, leaving the implication hanging, that Baron might already be gone. 
“But he has the Echo of Sight,” Jessica argued quietly, her hand tightening on the hilt of her short sword. “How could he not detect it early? He didn’t even have time to shout a warning?”
Gian stayed silent, his eyes scanning the darkness, his body tense and ready. He could feel the dread in the air settling heavier by the second.
Then Jimmy froze, his expression paling. He slowly shook his head.
“It’s gone, sir,” Jimmy said, his voice hushed. “The rabbit I just sent… it vanished.”
“What now, sir?” Donny, who had been silent until now, finally asked, his voice low but steady.
All eyes turned to Thompson. The sergeant exhaled slowly, his expression grim.
“We can now assume they’re dead,” he said flatly. “We have two options: fight it… or escape. I want to hear your votes.”
The soldiers exchanged uneasy glances, weighing their chances in the oppressive silence of the Doom Forest.
Before anyone could speak, Thompson continued, his tone heavy. “I’ll be honest. Part of me wants to fight, if only to recover what’s left of the scouts. But you all know what fighting in the dead of night means here. The forest is too quiet. Any battle will shatter that silence and might draw other Wildborn to us. That’s why my recommendation is to avoid it. Escape while we can.”
Kel leaned closer to Oldman, whispering, “Wait, we’re leaving Baron and the other scout? Just like that?”
Oldman’s face hardened. “Kel, they’re already dead. I could send Tata to check, but it’s too dangerous. The sergeant is right. We should avoid it if possible.” Tata peeked slightly from Oldman’s pocket but quickly ducked back down. 
“What about our supplies, sir?” Jones asked, glancing at the tents that were still set up, faint outlines in the darkness. 
“Leave them,” Thompson said firmly. “We can return for them when the sun rises. If that thing’s still around, we’ll face it in daylight. At least then, it’ll be easier to spot.”
The soldiers nodded one by one, still crouched within the clearing as the quiet decision passed through the group. The decision was made—they would leave, for now.
