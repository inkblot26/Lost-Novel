Chapter 50: Beyond the West
As the trio sat at one of the long wooden tables in the bustling Outpost Two cafeteria, trays of simple food before them, they noticed Sergeant Thompson approaching along the tiled aisle, a young soldier in a plain gray shirt walking at his side. 
“Good morning, gentlemen,” Thompson greeted, nodding as the chatter of other soldiers hummed around them. 
“Good morning, sir!” Kel said enthusiastically, drawing glances from nearby soldiers.
Thompson chuckled. “Someone had a good night’s sleep.”
“Good morning, Sergeant,” Gian said. Oldman tried to respond as well, but his mouth was full of food.
Thompson continued, “I just came to introduce someone. This is Private Joey.”
“Morning, everyone,” Joey said with a polite smile.
“Apologies for the abruptness,” Thompson added. “But if you want to speak with him, now is the best time, over breakfast. He’s part of the dispatch platoon departing within the hour.” 
“Wait, he’s going with them? He just recovered, right?” Kel asked, confused.
Joey nodded. “I insisted on going. I have the Echo of Camouflage. It might be useful in the fight at Outpost One.”
Oldman gave a firm nod. “You’re a good soldier.”
Joey sat down, the smile on his face fading slightly. “But sadly, not strong enough.”
“Alright, I’ll leave you to it. I’ve got patrol duty,” Thompson said, stepping back. “Good luck, all of you.”
“Thank you,” Gian replied.
Kel gave a playful salute. “Stay safe sergeant.”
Joey looked between them. “So, what do you want to know?”
Gian slid his tray aside. “Whatever you can tell us about what happened on your last mission.”
Joey took a breath. “We received a report, sent by message bird from one of our pathfinders, stating that Graylatch Village had been attacked by a small horde of Wildborn. So we were given a mission to deliver food supplies as aid to the village.”
“Pathfinder?” Gian asked, tilting his head.
Oldman explained, “They’re elite scouts. Special forces trained to travel alone through the Unknown—map terrain, observe Wildborn, report threats. It’s a tough job.”
Joey nodded. “Exactly. Anyway, Graylatch is about eighteen to twenty days east of here. We were on day sixteen, almost there.”
His expression darkened. “That day, we encountered a flock of about fourteen Scorchbeaks. We managed to kill them, took only minor injuries, but we were worn out.”
He stared down at his hands. “Then, out of nowhere, it started—arrows rained down on us. Most of the squad was killed in the first volley. I was hit too and thrown into a bush.”
“Did you see who it was?” Gian asked.
“Yes, but I didn’t see their faces. They wore masks in violet robes,” Joey said. “Everything happened so fast. There were shouts, chaos… we were surrounded. Some of our men fought back and even killed a few of them, but it wasn’t enough. We were outnumbered and exhausted.”
He clenched his left hand around his right wrist. “I used my rare echo to stay hidden… didn’t move an inch.”
Joey closed his eyes. “When it ended, they took everything—food, weapons, even the horses that pulled the relief cart. Then they walked into the forest and disappeared.”
A long silence hung in the air.
“After that, I ran. Took me a few days, but I found a patrol. They brought me back here.”
He looked up at them. “That’s everything I know.”
Kel glanced at the others. “What do you think?”
“Not sure,” Gian admitted.
Oldman nodded. “Yeah… I feel you. The one we’re after doesn’t seem like the looter type.”
“Right? I mean, why ambush a troop just for food and weapons?” Kel asked, frowning. 
“Or maybe to stop them from reaching the village,” Gian added thoughtfully.
Joey shrugged. “I wish I could tell you more. We've been wondering the same thing—who they are, what they want. That’s why we sent a bird to the village, hoping for answers.”
He paused, then added, “But still no word.”
“Anyway, thanks for sharing what you could,” Gian said.
“No problem. I’d like to help more, but that’s all I’ve got,” Joey said as he stood.
“Even the smallest detail helps,” Kel replied with a grateful smile.
“Then I’ll leave you to it. Good luck out there,” Joey said, shaking hands with the three before heading off.

Back in Captain Kirk’s office, a small, sparsely furnished room with maps pinned to the walls, Kirk looked up from his desk. “So, what do you three plan to do now?” His gaze shifted between Gian, Kel, and Oldman, while Tata perched quietly on Oldman’s shoulder.
“We’ve decided to continue to Graylatch Village,” Gian said. “We’ll try to gather more information from there.”
“Hopefully the village still stands,” Kel muttered.
Captain Kirk nodded solemnly. “Highlady Rose has instructed me to assist you however I can. I’ll be assigning a squad of ten soldiers to accompany you, with Sergeant Thompson leading them since you’re already familiar with him.”
Before they could respond, Kirk lifted a hand. “But I should be honest. My men can only escort you halfway. After that, they’ll return here. We need every soldier we can spare to defend this outpost. I mean no offense, but that village lies beyond our official jurisdiction. Sending more troops beyond this point isn’t feasible.”
“That’s more than we expected, Captain,” Oldman said respectfully. “We didn’t think you’d send any troops at all.”
“Thank you for your help,” Gian added sincerely.
Kirk gave a curt nod. “You’re welcome. Rest for the day. You’ll leave at first light tomorrow.”
He stood and extended his hand. “I wish you three good luck.”
Gian, Kel, and Oldman shook it in turn, then turned to leave the office.

As the sun crested the horizon over the open training field of Outpost Two, soldiers moved in formation, sparring and drilling on the dewy grass. Their voices rang out in unison: “We Till, We Stand!”
Gian, Kel, and Oldman spotted Sergeant Thompson approaching with nine soldiers clad in the green military armor of the Lanthea Kingdom.
Kel straightened up and gave a salute. “Good morning, Sergeant! We Till!”
Thompson chuckled and returned the salute. “We Stand!”
Gian and Oldman simply nodded in greeting.
Thompson pointed at the soldiers behind him  “Good morning, gentlemen. My squad’s been ordered to escort you halfway to Graylatch Village. We’ll clear any Wildborn we come across along the way. As long as the numbers aren’t overwhelming, you can leave the fighting to us.”
“Thank you. We appreciate it,” Gian replied.
Thompson gestured to the soldiers behind him. “Let me introduce you to my squad. This here is Private Donny, my right hand. If I’m unavailable, he leads the unit. He’s an Echo of Fire user.”
A short, serious-looking man stepped forward with a curt nod.
“Next are my scouts—Baron, who wields the Echo of Perception allowing him to see great distance, and Jimmy…” Thompson smirked, pausing. “…who can summon, well… rabbits.”
The squad burst into light laughter.
“Hey, don’t laugh! They’re useful rabbits!” Jimmy protested. “And cute, too!”
Still grinning, Thompson moved on. “That’s Jones. Tough as an ox.” He gestured toward a towering figure, his arms lined with thick scars. 
“And the only woman in our squad, Jessica. Don’t underestimate her. She’s strong and reliable.” Jessica gave a polite nod.
The remaining four soldiers introduced themselves in turn. None possessed Echoes, but years in military training had made them seasoned swordsmen and archers in their own right.
Once the introductions were done, Thompson clapped his hands. “Alright, now that everyone’s acquainted, let’s move out.”
Together, the squad and the trio passed through the gates of Outpost Two, leaving the safety of the Verdant Ring behind as they stepped onto the dry, cracked earth of the Unknown. 

Out in the wild, the landscape opened into rolling fields under the morning sun. The dry, cracked soil shimmered faintly in the heat, dust rising with each footstep.
“Ughhh… it’s so hot,” Kel groaned, dragging his feet along the uneven terrain.
Oldman smirked from beneath the brim of his brown gambler’s hat. “Bet you’re wishing you had a hat like mine now.”
Tata peeked its tiny head out from Oldman’s front pocket, safely shielded from the sun.
Gian glanced around the horizon. “I don’t see any Wildborn—or even any signs of life out here.”
Walking beside him, Sergeant Thompson gestured toward the open expanse. “This area is deliberately cleared. Patrols maintain these fields so any Wildborn can be seen from a distance. We’ll be traversing three full days like this before reaching the edge of the Doom Forest.”
He gestured ahead, where two tiny figures moved far ahead of the group. “And don’t expect to see Wildborn here. Patrols usually deal with them before they ever reach this field. Even if any slip through, Donny and Baron will take care of it before we’re in danger.”
Jessica, walking a few paces behind, grinned and gave Gian a playful wink. “Don’t worry, you’ll get your share of Wildborn once we reach the Doom Forest.”

As dusk settled over the open fields, the group began setting up camp. Thompson’s squad moved efficiently, hammering tent pegs into the dry earth and stretching canvas against the cooling wind.
“You three sure are lucky to have a dimensional storage,” Thompson remarked, sitting casually on a flat stone while his soldiers worked.
Oldman chuckled. “Look who’s talking. You’re not even carrying your own stuff.”
Thompson laughed. “Well, I am a sergeant. Gotta enjoy the little privileges, right?”
Jessica finished setting up her officer’s tent, then turned toward Gian. “Hey, aren’t you going to start on your tent? It’s getting dark.”
Gian shook his head. “No need. We have this.” He pulled a Nightlily from a small bottle of water, gave it a light shake to dry, and tossed it gently onto the ground. The plant rapidly unfurled, its petals stretching wide in the cool evening air to form a cushioned, portable bed. Under the emerging starlight, it looked soft and inviting.
“Whoa… that’s amazing,” Jessica exclaimed as nearby soldiers paused in curiosity.
Thompson leaned closer, intrigued. “Wait, is that the echotech Nightlily? I’ve heard about those! Some officer from Outpost One said they’re getting popular in the Aethelgard army.”
Tata immediately scampered over and hopped onto the bed, spinning once before lying down comfortably.
“Yeah,” Gian said, watching the little creature settle in. “We got it from some crafters who came to Tillara City.”
“Damn… I need to take a vacation when we’re back,” Thompson muttered. “If I don’t buy one soon, they’ll be sold out.”
Kel walked over carrying a pot of steaming stew, with the tall, scarred Jones helping him. “Don’t worry, Miss Jessica, you can use mine,” Kel offered with a wink.
Jessica smiled playfully. “I won’t say no to that. In return, you can use my tent, Mr. Gentleman.”
Kel blinked. Maybe the Unknown wasn’t the only wild thing out here. His face instantly turned bright red, much to the amusement of the nearby soldiers. 
“Hey, food’s ready!” Jones called, holding another pot as soldiers began lining up for rations.
Oldman stood, pocketing the Direstone he’d been idly tossing. The three-pointed stone had been spinning in the dirt moments ago, briefly levitating before settling to point east, north, and west.
“Oldman,” Gian asked as they walked toward the food line, “how can we really use that thing out here? I mean, in the Unknown we don’t even have a detailed map. If we get lost in the forest, what then?”
Oldman smirked. “It’s not useless. There’s an old saying around here: When you’re lost, just keep going west.”
Gian thought for a moment, then nodded. “Because west is toward the kingdoms… and east leads deeper into the Unknown.”
“Exactly, kid,” Oldman said, patting Gian’s shoulder. “Now come on. Let’s eat. I’m starving.”
