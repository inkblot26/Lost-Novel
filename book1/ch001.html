Chapter 1: Town of Tala

The sun crept slowly over the hills, spilling soft gold across the valley of Lanthea. Its light touched the quiet town of Tala, where wooden roofs glistened faintly with morning dew. Thin smoke curled lazily from chimneys, carrying the warm smell of grain porridge and fresh bread. Children darted barefoot along dirt paths, laughter echoing between small houses. Out in the fields, farmers were already at work. They knelt in rows, watering the crops, checking the soil with calloused hands. Their movements were steady, unhurried, the rhythm of lives untroubled. Tala was simple. Peaceful. Removed from the scars of the old world. At least, for now. In the town plaza, the air buzzed with chatter. Teenagers stood in loose rows under the morning sun, some stifling yawns, others shifting restlessly on their feet. One boy in a plain gray shirt and black pants, dressed like everyone else, strolled lazily through the crowd. His eyes lit up when he spotted a familiar face, and he headed over. “Hey, Gian! You lose. I got here first. Hand over the prize money,” Ren called, grinning wide. His dark, curly hair was a windswept mess, his brown eyes gleaming with mischief. Gian dragged himself over with a yawn. “Shut up, Ren. I never agreed to that bet. And why do we even have to be here this early?” “Aren’t you excited? It’s our first day!” Ren said, practically bouncing in place. Before Gian could reply, a sharp whistle cut through the plaza. An Elder stepped into view, silencing the crowd with nothing but his presence. His face was weathered, marked by pale scars that told quiet stories of old battles. Sharp gray eyes scanned the teens like a hawk sizing up prey. He leaned on a sturdy cane, not out of frailty but more like a warrior resting a hand on a familiar weapon. “Good morning,” Elder Eldon began, his deep voice carrying easily across the plaza. “Today, we welcome a new batch of students. You’ve all turned thirteen. That means you’re officially old enough to join our ranks. Those of you who are older, I expect you to guide the newcomers.” He clapped his hands once. The sound cracked through the silence like a whip. “Older students, to the back. Newbies, stay put. I’ll explain.” The older teens peeled away, grouping with their respective instructors. The remaining thirteen-year-olds shuffled nervously, unsure if they should be excited or terrified. Elder Eldon’s gaze swept over them. “You’re no longer children. As of today, your military training begins. For the next three years, until you reach adulthood at sixteen, you’ll report here every morning.” His tone sharpened. “After that, do whatever you want. But until then, this is your duty.” He paused. “Any questions?” A girl timidly raised her hand, but the Elder paid her no mind and continued. “What you learn here is for one thing—your survival. Every citizen of the Lanthea Kingdom is trained to face beasts. At the very least, you’ll learn how not to die cowering. You’ll also learn how to endure in the wild, if the time comes.” His eyes narrowed, sweeping over the crowd. “Now, who here knows anything about beasts?” Another hand shot up. Again, ignored. “These creatures are called the Wildborn— vicious and deadly. They are classified as either Ferals or Ascendants.” He let the words hang for a moment. “Ferals are driven purely by instinct. Animalistic. Territorial. They don’t reason with you, and they sure as hell don’t negotiate. Their behavior is all about survival, whether it’s yours or theirs.” He pointed north, toward the tree line. “Beyond those hills, on the road to Tillara City, roam packs of Bloomfangs—one of the more common Ferals in our kingdom. Best avoid them, unless you want to end up as their snack.” A short boy raised his hand and blurted out before he could stop himself. “What do they look like? How strong are they?” The Elder gave him a withering look. “Let me finish, kid.” “Bloomfangs are tall, wolf-like creatures with dark green fur and vines sprouting from their backs. They blend into the forest so well, you won’t notice them until it’s already too late. They’re fast, strong, can leap high—and if you’re close enough to see all that, you’re probably halfway eaten.” The same girl from before raised her hand again. Still ignored. “Now…” Eldon’s voice dropped lower. “The Ascendants.” He stopped pacing, his tone suddenly colder. “I truly hope none of you ever encounter one. Ascendants aren’t mindless. They’re evolved—intelligent. Some can even speak, strategize, and lead entire swarms of Wildborn in coordinated attacks. What makes them truly dangerous, though, are their special abilities.” He took a step forward, cane tapping the ground softly. “So how do humans survive against that?” More hands rose. Still ignored. “We use weapons. But beyond that,” he said, stopping mid-step, “we have Echo Abilities.” Eldon reached into his sling bag and drew out a strange tome. Its cover was fashioned from dark bark, etched with faint symbols in glowing ink that pulsed like a heartbeat. Every teen in the plaza stared, wide-eyed. “This is no ordinary book,” the Elder said softly. “This is a Verdant Echo—born from the earth’s breath, bound by the memory of nature. You do not read it. You accept it. It burns itself into you. And once it does, it’s gone.” He held it up high, letting the light catch the strange, shifting patterns on its surface. “There are two types of Echoes: Root and Rare,” he said. Ren’s hand shot up instantly. “Where do we get one?” The Elder gave him a look but answered anyway. “Mysteriously, they appear when you defeat Wildborn. Or, if you’re lucky, you might find them in nature—buried in the earth, sealed in stone, hidden in trees. But be warned, most people can only absorb two to three Echoes, depending on their body’s strength and compatibility.” A girl spoke up, hesitant but curious. “How rare are… Rare Echoes?” Eldon frowned slightly. “They’re called Rare for a reason. How should I know? But don’t underestimate Root Echoes just because they’re common.” He lowered the tome, his voice rising. “No, my dear students. Echoes grow with the user. A weak Echo of Fire may only light a candle at first, but in the hands of a master, it can ignite storms or let you swim through lava unharmed. Some Echoes even enhance each other when used together. It all depends on you.” His tone hardened. “It’s not just about wanting power, children. The Echo chooses too. If your mind is clouded, your body broken, or your spirit weak, don’t blame the Tome when you explode into bark dust.” The plaza fell silent. He let silence hang for a moment. Later that day. After class, Gian and Ren walked down the shaded path that cut through the edges of town. “So…” Ren kicked a stone ahead lazily. “What do you think about all that beasts and Echo stuff?” Gian shrugged. “Still processing. It’s terrifying… but kind of amazing and exciting at the same time.” Ren grinned. “I want a Rare Echo. Something awesome—mind control, teleportation… ooh, time-stopping. That’d be perfect.” “I don’t think there’s such a thing as time-stopping,” Gian replied dryly. “Don’t rush it. You’ll get an Echo someday.” “Easy for you to say. You already have one.” Gian gave a tired smile. “Yeah. But it’s useless. I don’t even know if it works.” His thoughts drifted to the small hill north of town, sun-drenched and wild. He had been four, chasing crickets with his nine-year-old brother. They had been darting through the tall grass when Gian’s foot snagged on a root. He tumbled down the slope, rolling painfully until he hit the bottom. “Gian!” Gideon shouted, panic written across his face as he scrambled down after him. Dazed, Gian lay still. That was when he noticed it—a brilliant blue flower swaying alone in the grass. Despite the throbbing in his leg, he crawled closer and plucked it. To his surprise, the ground shifted. Roots tore free, and something hidden came loose. A tome—ancient and worn. Its cover was cracked, wrapped in brittle blue petals and weathered dark bark. At its center pulsed a white glow, forming a great four-pointed star, flanked by two smaller ones: one gleaming bright at the upper right, the other shrouded in shadow at the lower left. Eyes wide, small hands trembling, he opened it. A faint glow seeped into his skin.
