Chapter 24: The Full Moon
By noon the following day, the group had stopped to rest beside the dirt path. The sun blazed overhead, its light filtered through the sparse canopy of trees that offered only meager shade. A faint breeze stirred the tall grass at the roadside, carrying with it the earthy scent of soil and leaves.
“I can help with patrol, sir,” Kel offered, tightening the strap around his wound as if to prove his point. 
“There’s no need, Kel. Just rest,” Lord Henry replied with a glance, his tone firm but not unkind. Turning toward Torren, who leaned casually against the trunk of a tree with arms crossed, he asked, “How many days away are we from the city, Torren?”
“Since we’re on foot, two—three at most, my lord,” Torren said. 
“I see. At last, we’re almost there.” Lord Henry nodded, his eyes softening. “Make sure you arrange matters for the families of the fallen guards once we reach Tillara.”
“Yes, my lord. We’ll send word as soon as possible,” Torren replied.
Lady Evangeline, seated on a flat stone, folded her hands in her lap. Her eyes lowered with quiet sorrow as she asked, “What about the families of the mercenaries?”
Thomas shook his head, flexing the bandaged stump of his hand. “We don’t have their records, my lady. They took the job, gave us only their names and skills. Nothing more.”
“So much for your first escort mission, huh?” Baldwin said with a chuckle, grinning at Kel.
“Once we reach the city, I’ll increase your payment,” Lord Henry said, offering Kel a faint smile.
“You don’t have to do that, my lord. We’re just doing our job,” Kel answered quickly.
“Don’t speak for all of us,” Baldwin cut in, hand raised. “I’ll gladly take it, my lord.”
“You deserve it, Kel,” Lady Evangeline added warmly.
Torren let out a low sigh, his gaze drifting toward the trees. “I don’t know if it’s bad luck or fate… normally this road is safe. If not for that Ascendant…”
“Speaking of luck,” Thomas added, grumbling, “how is it that Gian keeps finding animals to hunt? I can’t even spot one.”
“Just don’t let him cook it, sir,” Kel said under his breath, a shadow of old trauma flickering across his face.

Some time passed as the group rested, rotating through patrol shifts. The forest was calm now, only the faint chirp of insects and rustle of leaves breaking the silence. 
“Nothing to report, sir. Maybe the Blinkclaw really won’t pursue us anymore,” Edmund said as he and Gian returned from their watch. 
“I see. That’s good. You two can rest now. Thomas, Michael, you’re next,” Torren ordered, assigning the next patrol shift.
Gian lowered himself beside Kel and Baldwin. “How are your injuries holding up?” he asked quietly. 
“It’s nothing. Honestly, I could still fight as if nothing happened, but Lady Evangeline insists I rest. Since we’re close to the city now, I’ll listen to her,” Kel replied with a faint smile. 
Baldwin lifted the stump of his bandaged arm with a wry grin. “As for me… well, you can see it. It’ll take a while to adjust. Not exactly comfortable yet.” 
“I’m sorry you lost your arm,” Gian said, guilt flickering in his tone.
“Haha, don’t worry, kiddo. These things happen. Just bad luck, that’s all,” Baldwin chuckled, his voice carrying more grit than sorrow.
Gian managed a small smile in return, but his attention drifted to a nearby argument. Lady Evangeline was holding out a rolled parchment-colored deep blue.
“Clara, just take this,” she urged.
“But what about you, Mom? You need that,” Clara protested, shaking her head.
“I’ll feel better knowing you have it. With your Echo of Water, you can use this to strengthen your materialization. I’m not taking no for an answer,” Lady Evangeline insisted firmly.
“I want one! I want one!” Cedric piped up eagerly, eyes sparkling.
“You’re too young, Ced. You wouldn’t even be able to control it if you unscrolled it,” Clara scolded him, clutching the parchment closer.
As the two siblings argued, Baldwin noticed Gian watching them.
“Great stuff, huh?” Baldwin said, nodding toward the parchment. “Having one of those relics…”
“That’s a relic?” Gian asked, curious.
“Yep. Whispering Scroll,” Baldwin said. “One-use, consumable relic. Unleashes a single, powerful effect drawn from the essence of Ascendant Cores.” 
“I heard they’re pretty pricey,” Kel added, leaning closer.
“‘Pricey’? You can’t even dream of getting one just because you have the money,” Baldwin laughed. “It’s rare, kid. You need connections. Even if one appears on the market, the demand’s so high that the price shoots through the roof.”
Gian studied the parchment. “What’s it even made of?”
Baldwin tapped his chin. “Blood and skin of a Wildborn, along with Ascendant cores specific to the desired effect, are needed. Then, someone with the Echo of Shape crafts it. There are all sorts of Whispering Scrolls—Fire, Water, Earth, Air, Lightning …” 
“I’ve heard of Light and Dark types too,” Kel said, miming a casting motion with his hand. 
“Exactly. But they’re hard to make in numbers,” Baldwin continued. “At best, one core can yield a few scrolls of the same type.”
As Baldwin carried on about the myths and origins of Whispering Scrolls, Gian quietly chewed on his rations, his thoughts drifting far from the fire’s circle.

“Sir, up ahead there’s an open field,” Thomas reported after returning from patrol. “We’re close to the city, but I counted about eleven Bloomfangs resting there. I think it’d be safer if we ambushed a few tonight. That way, the path will be easier to cross tomorrow morning.”
Lord Henry’s gaze shifted to Torren. “What do you think?” he asked his most trusted guard.
Torren lifted his eyes to the full moon, its pale glow casting silver light across the camp. After a moment, he nodded. “I agree, my lord. We’re short on men—it’ll be dangerous to face them in daylight while protecting your family and the injured.”
“You’re right,” Lord Henry said with a slow nod. “We don’t need to wipe them all out. Kill as many as you can from a distance, then retreat. But we can’t leave the camp undefended.”
“Understood. Three should be enough for the ambush,” Torren suggested.
“Thomas, take Gian and Edmund with you,” Lord Henry ordered. “Move quickly, then return at once.”
Edmund hesitated. “Sir, wouldn’t Michael be a better choice than me?”
“Yes, Michael is stronger with his Echo of the Bow,” Lord Henry admitted, “but we need him here in case the camp is attacked. You’ll serve as guide if something goes wrong. Gian can learn from this—it’ll be good experience.”
He glanced over at the young man sitting near the campfire, polishing a short sword beside Lady Clara, Cedric, and Kel. 
“Understood, sir,” Thomas and Edmund replied, bowing slightly before moving toward Gian.
Torren watched them leave, then glanced back at his lord. “You see something in him, my lord?”
Lord Henry gave a soft chuckle. “It’s just a feeling. Both he and Kel… I believe they have the potential to grow into something greater.”
“Do you wish me to recruit them officially?” Torren asked.
“Not yet,” Lord Henry replied with an amused smile. “Let’s first reach the city. If they wish to join us, they will. Who are we to stand in the way of a young man’s adventure?”
“As you say, my lord.” Torren bowed his head, then turned, his eyes following Gian as he walked away with Thomas and Edmund, disappearing into the moonlit night.

Later that night, beneath the pale glow of the full moon…
“They’re there,” Thomas whispered, pointing across the field as he, Edmund, and Gian crouched low in the brush.
“Do you hear anything?” Thomas asked.
“Just like you said. Eleven Bloomfangs,” Edmund replied, his longbow already drawn. Gian mirrored him, steadying his breath as he notched an arrow.
Clouds shifted above, letting moonlight spill across the clearing, illuminating the pack of beasts.
“Now!” Thomas commanded.
A fierce gust burst from his hand as Gian and Edmund loosed their shots. Two Bloomfangs dropped instantly, arrows lodged deep in their skulls, while another was hurled back, injured by the gale.
Snarls ripped through the air. The rest of the pack sprang up, hackles raised, charging toward the disturbance.
“Second volley!” Thomas barked.
Edmund’s arrow buried itself in a beast’s throat, dropping it. Gian’s shot struck the leg of another, slowing it down but not stopping it. 
“They're still at a distance. You can each get off two more shots. Don’t worry, I’ll cover you,” Thomas said, stepping forward.
They fired again—one arrow struck true, crippling another beast, while Gian’s missed wide. 
“Don’t worry about it, Gian. Just focus,” Thomas encouraged.
Gian took a deep breath, steadied his aim, and let loose another arrow. This time, it hit a Bloomfang in the shoulder, halting its advance. 
“Good! I’ll blow them back. Aim for the nearest one—”
But Edmund suddenly froze, his head snapping to the side. His face drained of color.
“This is bad,” he muttered.
Thomas and Gian turned, startled by his tone.
“I hear footsteps—eleven of them. Not beasts. People. They’re heading straight for the camp. It might be bandits!” Edmund hissed, urgency sharpening his words.
The sudden news broke their concentration, and the Bloomfangs gained ground closing the distance.
“Damn it! Edmund, go warn them! Gian, stay with me—hold them here!” Thomas roared, blasting another Bloomfang back with a surge of wind. 
“No—Gian’s faster than me. He should go! I’ll hold them off with you!” Edmund insisted.
Thomas hesitated for only a heartbeat before nodding.
Gian stored his longbow into the Void Ring, turned, and sprinted back toward the camp under the full moon’s watchful gaze, his footsteps pounding the earth as snarls and the clash of battle faded behind him.
