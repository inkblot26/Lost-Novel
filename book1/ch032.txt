Chapter 32: When Quiet Hands Shake
So this is Lady Rose… Torren thought as he stood quietly by the door, observing her with silent awe. The enigmatic sister of the Highlord.
She moved with quiet dignity, graceful but never showy. Long ashen-gray hair flowed freely down her back, untouched by pins or jewels. Though noble by birth, Rose dressed in simple, well-worn cloaks and muted tunics, choosing function over fashion.
She rose and walked toward Henry, offering her hand in greeting.
Henry accepted it, his eyes meeting hers. Her storm-blue gaze, soft yet piercing, seemed to look through him rather than at him, thoughtful and unreadable. 
“Please,” she said, gesturing to a chair near the window.
They both sat.
A brief silence settled before she spoke again.
“What do you know of this city, Sir Henry? And please… be honest.”
Henry didn’t hesitate. “Forgive my bluntness, but I think this is the worst city in the kingdom. Compared to the serene beauty of the capital, Liora, or the rising strength of Salrima… this place is a stain.”
She nodded slowly. “You’re right. This city has a disease. Killings in broad daylight. Crushing taxes. Corrupt nobles who feast while the people starve.” Her voice turned colder. “But it wasn’t always like this.”
Her gaze dropped to the floor as her fingers touched the single ornament she wore, a faded ring once her mother’s. A quiet memory of better days. 
“It was different a decade ago, when my father ruled,” she said softly.
“He was a good man,” Henry replied. “Beloved by the people.”
“Yes,” she nodded. “Both my father and mother were kind, just leaders. But after his death, chaos erupted over who would inherit the title. Our main family splintered. My uncle pushed for my older brother, who was twenty-five at the time, claiming his bloodline gave him the right.” 
“I was twenty-three,” she added. “I hated politics. I preferred a quiet, simple life. And Ronald, our youngest brother, had just turned seventeen, a year into adulthood. My father, burdened by guilt after our mother died giving birth to him, spoiled him beyond repair. He grew up twisted.”
Her tone darkened.
“When my brother and uncle took control, they made sure no one could oppose them. They purged anyone in the family who might challenge their rule.” 
Henry leaned forward. “Forgive my question, but… why were you spared, my lady?”
She gave a faint smile. “Simple. They never saw me as a threat. I was just a woman who kept to herself, never involved in politics. To them, I was harmless.”
Her gaze sharpened.
“And Ronald became their pet. Parties, corruption, drugs, slaves—he did as he pleased, and in return, a cut of everything went to my dear Highlord brother.”
Henry’s jaw tightened. “And now?”
She looked at him, eyes like thunderclouds.
“Now I can’t stay silent anymore. I won’t let my people suffer while those in power gorge themselves.”
She leaned forward, her voice low and steady.
“I need your help, Sir Henry. This city has been rotting for years, Sir Henry. If nothing is done... there will soon be nothing left to save.”
“You want to kill your brother,” Henry said quietly.
“I’ve been preparing for years,” she admitted. “But I need more. More funds to arm my people. That’s why I’ve reached out to you. I know you're a man of principle.”
She held his gaze.
“Together, we can make this city a better place.”
Lord Henry remained silent for a moment, his gaze drifting to the window. Lady Rose didn’t press him, waiting patiently as he watched the quiet streets beyond the glass.
Finally, he spoke.
“Let’s say I provide the funds you need, enough to arm your people and prepare them. Even then, how do you plan to defeat the city’s military? You’d be going up against hundreds of trained soldiers.”
Rose nodded, prepared for the question. “The city of Tillara has around three thousand soldiers in total. But not all of them are stationed here. Around a thousand are deployed near the Verdant Ring on permanent rotational duty.”
“And the city’s internal defense?” Henry asked.
“It requires roughly five hundred active soldiers at all times.”
Henry raised an eyebrow. “That still leaves around fifteen hundred soldiers—fully armed, well-trained, and close enough to respond quickly from nearby towns and outposts.”
“Yes,” she admitted.
“So how many people do you have?” he asked, eyes narrowing.
“Thirty to forty, including my personal guards and a few trusted allies—survivors, victims of this city’s cruelty. With the right resources and timing, we can recruit a dozen more—mercenaries, deserters, desperate folk willing to fight for the right cause… if the price is right.”
Henry frowned. “Even with that, my lady… that’s nowhere near enough.”
Torren, who had been silent until now, stepped forward. “He’s right. That would be suicide.”
“Torren,” Henry said gently, raising a hand to stop him.
Lady Rose turned to Torren with a soft smile. “It’s alright. I know who you are, Sir Torren, and I admire what you’ve done. Your loyalty and bravery are well-known.”
She looked back to Henry, eyes calm but resolute.
“I understand your doubts. But I promise you, we do have a chance. Because numbers aren’t everything. Timing, knowledge of the terrain, the element of surprise, the people’s anger… all of these are on our side.”
"And if what I've gathered is correct… an opportunity will present itself." She smiled.
Suddenly, the back door creaked open, the sound sharp in the stillness. Torren instinctively reached for his weapon, but Lady Rose raised a hand calmly.
“It’s alright. He’s with me,” she said.
A man stepped into the room and knelt before her.
“It’s confirmed, Lady Rose,” he said. “My source told me the army will be summoned and will depart within three days.”
“How many?” she asked, her voice steady.
“Around fifteen hundred soldiers, my lady. Preparations are already underway,” the man replied.
“What about the city’s defenses?” she asked, anticipation flickering in her eyes.
“Of the original five hundred stationed here, two hundred will be redeployed to neighboring towns and emergency camps. That leaves only three hundred soldiers to defend the city,” he reported.
Lady Rose nodded.
“You’ve done well.”
“I’ll take my leave now, my lady,” the man said with a respectful bow, the scar on his forehead catching the light as he turned.
“Thank you,” she said softly, watching as he disappeared through the door.
Noticing the confusion on Lord Henry and Torren’s faces, Lady Rose offered an explanation.
"You might not know this, since you’ve been traveling for a few weeks, but further south, beyond our capital Liora, at the Gobe desert, a horde of Wildborn has been spotted. Scouts estimate they’ll reach the borders in two or three months."
"What?" Lord Henry and Torren exclaimed in unison, stunned.
"Don’t worry," Rose added with a slight smile. "Even though they’re calling it a horde, I’ve heard it’s only slightly larger than the usual Wildborn attacks.
She leaned slightly forward, her voice calm but firm.
"And as you may know, while Liora is calm and beautiful, its king is selfish and unwilling to risk his own army. So he ordered reinforcements from Tillara and Salrima to bolster and intercept the Wildborn before they cross into settled land.
“What? But the main army of the kingdom is in the capital city. Why wait? The settlements along the border will be the first to suffer.” Torren said angry.
“You're right. The king just wants to play it safe and reduce casualties among his own men while bleeding the other two cities of both manpower and resources.” Rose said.
She paused, her expression shifting.
"But this is an opportunity for us. With the majority of Tillara’s forces gone, including the Verdant Ring’s stationed troops, and the remaining city guards reassigned to reinforce neighboring towns, the city will be vulnerable."
Her gaze hardened. "We can strike my dear brother swiftly, before he has time to react."
"I see," Lord Henry said quietly, the pieces coming together in his mind.
"I’m not here to pressure you, Sir Henry," Rose said gently. "I’m not like my brother. With or without your help, this plan will go forward. But if you decide to stand with us, let us know. You have time to think it through."
Lord Henry stood, nodding with respect. "I will, Lady Rose. Thank you."
With a bow, he and Torren took their leave.
Rose stepped to the window, looking out at the city bathed in steady midday light, rooftops glinting faintly as if nothing beyond the horizon threatened them.
"I suppose… luck might finally be on our side."
