Chapter 23: Held Back by Blood
“Everyone, protect Lord Henry!” Michael shouted, loosing arrow after arrow in rapid succession. 
“I need to time it properly. Hold it off as long as you can,” Lord Henry said, gripping the red parchment tightly in one hand.
The Blinkclaw darted through every attack with taunting precision, weaving around the arrows as it sprinted toward them.
“Gian, same as before—I’ll open it up for you!” Kel roared, swinging his Crimson Blood Axe in a wide arc.
Gian crouched low behind him, sword ready, eyes fixed on the creature’s movements. If needed, I’ll summon the short sword, he thought, no longer bothering to conceal his Void Ring.
The Blinkclaw slipped past Kel’s strike and lashed out in return. Its claws tore into Kel’s side, blood spraying as he staggered back with a grunt of pain.
But Gian was already moving.
From the creature’s blind spot, he slashed downward with his right hand—but missed. Anticipating the dodge, he feinted and summoned a short sword mid-motion. In one fluid sweep, he caught it with his left hand and slashed across, grazing the Blinkclaw’s flank.
The shallow wound drew its attention instantly. With a guttural snarl, it whirled and lunged at Gian, its yellow eyes blazing with murderous focus.
Lord Henry didn’t hesitate.
He unrolled the Whispering Scroll, and at once the air ignited, heat bursting outward as a colossal wave of fire roared forth, racing toward the airborne beast.
Too fast, Lord Henry thought grimly. I won’t make it in time.
But then, from the corner of Gian’s eye, a red streak shot across the air, swift and liquid-like, striking the Blinkclaw mid-leap from Kel’s direction.
The blow staggered the creature for the briefest moment.
That moment was enough.
The flame swallowed the Blinkclaw whole, incinerating it in midair. The shockwave of heat ripped through the clearing, scorching earth and setting trees ablaze.
Gian threw himself to the ground, shielding his face from the searing gust. When the blaze finally died down, he looked up through the smoke.
The Blinkclaw lay writhing, burning—then stilled. Dead.
Flames devoured the nearby trees, scorching the forest in a wide radius. Smoke rolled low across the clearing, thick and bitter, stinging eyes and throats. The forest that had once loomed in shadow now burned in violent orange, night held back only by fire. But amid the chaos, Gian realized something.
The center of the blast… it wasn’t aimed at me. He deliberately used the left side of the scroll’s flame to avoid hitting me. He controlled it that precisely. His’s gaze shifted to Lord Henry, a new level of respect forming in his eyes.
“You okay?” Kel asked through clenched teeth, one hand pressed hard against his bleeding side.
Gian rose, dusting off ash. “I should be asking you that.”
His eyes fell to his swords. The old blade trembled faintly in his grip, a thin crack splitting along its steel. With a quiet sigh, he dismissed it back into his Void Ring.
Lord Henry stepped toward the charred clearing, his staff in hand, eyes fixed on the Blinkclaw’s smoldering corpse. He lingered there in silence, ensuring it did not rise again. 
“Good thing you finished it off, my lord,” Michael said, lowering his bow as he joined him. “For a second, I thought it might escape again with how fast it was, but I think Gian’s strike slowed it down enough.”
At that, Gian flicked a glance toward Kel but held his tongue.
Michael’s gaze shifted back. “I didn’t know you had a relic, Gian… and a storage one, no less.”
Lord Henry turned away from the ashes, his voice steady. “We don’t have time to rest. Michael, you’re with me—we need to find Torren and check on their fight.”
He looked back toward the two young men. “Can you hold things here, Gian?”
“Yes, sir,” Gian replied, summoning a longbow of deep green light into his hand.
“Good. Kel, stop the bleeding and get some rest,” Lord Henry said with a faint smile before he and Michael dashed off, following the distant crash of falling trees.

As Gian and Kel approached Edmund and the others, Lady Clara rushed forward, her eyes brimming with guilt. Without hesitation, she knelt at Kel’s side, hands trembling as she wrapped a cloth around his wound. 
“I’m sorry... I was too weak. I couldn’t help you, and I hid the fact that I have an Echo of Water,” she whispered, her voice breaking.
“There’s nothing to apologize for, Lady Clara,” Gian said gently as he moved toward Baldwin, who sat pale and grim, clutching his bleeding arm. “It’s our job to protect you from the start.” 
“Besides, you’re not weak,” Edmund added firmly. “You’ve got great control over water. Summoning is only part of it. You’ll get stronger.” 
Kel forced a smile, trying to lighten her burden. “He’s right, my lady. Everyone has their own circumstances. Don’t beat yourself up.” 
“How’s your arm?” Gian asked, crouching beside Baldwin. 
“We managed to wrap it… but it hurts like hell. It’s still bleeding pretty bad.” Baldwin growled through clenched teeth. “Got anything in that magical storage of yours?” 
“Unfortunately, no. Just water jugs. Supplies are in the pack over there,” Gian said, pointing toward the bag resting nearby. 
Baldwin’s jaw tightened. His eyes hardened with resolve. “It’s no good. Best thing is to cut it clean—and burn it shut.”
Lady Evangeline paled, stepping forward. “Are you certain?” 
“I agree,” Edmund said grimly. “It might be the only way to stop the bleeding.” 
“Do it, Gian,” Baldwin said, meeting his eyes without wavering.
“You can use my axe,” Kel said quietly, setting the Crimson Blood Axe at Gian’s feet. 
Gian picked it up, heavy with the weight of what he was about to do. “Alright.” He turned toward the fire, pressing the blade into the flames until it glowed red-hot. Sparks hissed in the air.
Meanwhile, Lady Evangeline asked in a low voice, “What do you hear from the others, Edmund?”
Edmund tilted his head, listening. “I can’t say for sure… but the trees keep falling. I think Lord Henry has reached them.”
When Gian returned with the glowing axe, Baldwin braced himself, his breathing steady despite the sweat pouring down his face.
“Go,” he said, voice rough.
With one swift motion, Gian swung. The axe cut clean through. Baldwin’s muffled scream tore from his throat as Gian immediately pressed the searing blade to the wound. Flesh sizzled. The smell of burning filled the camp. Baldwin’s entire body shook violently before, at last, he went limp, unconscious.
The bleeding had stopped. In silence, Gian and Clara wrapped the stump in clean cloth, working quickly, their hands steady despite the heaviness in the air.

It was several long minutes before Lord Henry and the others emerged from the treeline. Thomas staggered beside Torren, his left hand wrapped hastily in cloth—his pinky gone. 
“How is it?” Edmund asked, rushing to meet them.
“Damn beast…” Thomas growled, teeth gritted. “I’ll kill it next time.”
“We managed to injure it, but it slipped away,” Torren said evenly. His tone was calm, his stance unshaken—his body showing no visible injuries, as if the clash had barely touched him.
“How are things here?” Lord Henry asked, striding toward his family.
Edmund gave a detailed report of what had happened in their absence. 
So it still managed to get away… Gian thought, eyes drifting toward the forest.
“What are we going to do now, sir?” Edmund asked after finishing his report.
“I doubt the Ascendant will come after us again,” Torren replied, glancing at the few trees still smoldering nearby. “Even if it does, it’s injured and alone. It can’t do much. . We’ll put some distance between us and the fire, then rest.”
“You’re right. The smoke could draw more Wildborn,” Thomas muttered in agreement as the smoke column rose thick into the night sky.
“Edmund, scout ahead. Michael, cover the rear,” Torren ordered. 
“Yes, sir,” both men answered, immediately moving to their posts, Edmund slipping into the shadows up front, Michael nocking an arrow at the back. 
“I’ll take point,” Torren continued. “Gian, Thomas, you guard the flanks. The family and wounded stay in the center.” 
“Yes, sir,” they answered together. Gian readied his bow, Thomas adjusting his bandaged hand before falling into position. 
Torren bent, hefting Baldwin onto his back as though the weight meant nothing.
“We’re ready, sir,” he said.
Lord Henry gave one last glance at the charred battlefield behind them, then nodded. “Move out.”
And so, the eleven of them set forward once more, leaving behind the scorched ruins of the fight as they pressed on along the dirt path toward Tillara City.
