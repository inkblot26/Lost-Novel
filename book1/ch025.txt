Chapter 25: A Moment Seized
“Are we there yet?” Walter asked, voice low and flat. The bald bandit leader scanned the dark line of trees ahead, shadowed by moonlight, then looked back to the ragged band gathered behind him.
“Almost, sir,” one of the men with the Echo of Pulse answered, fingertip pressed to ground as if listening for its heartbeat. “But one of the fighters who ambushed the Bloomfangs is sprinting toward their camp now. Looks like he’s trying to warn them.”
Walter’s mouth twisted. He studied his crew—scraps of leather and mismatched mail, a scatter of bows and short blades glinting under the moon—and fixed on the shortest figure among them, a new recruit who kept shifting from foot to foot.
“All right, Minta, stall him,” Walter ordered, voice sharpened. “He might be running to warn the camp. We need surprise. Don’t let him reach them.”
Minta swallowed, eyes darting to the others. “Alone, sir?” he asked, the question thin with panic.
“Yes. Alone, you idiot.” Walter’s glare cut through the trees. “Ambush him. Don’t come back if you’re a coward. I don’t have room for weaklings.”
“Y-yes, sir,” Minta stammered, fingers tight around the hilt of his sword as he slunk off into the underbrush.
Walter turned to the rest of the band, voice cold and businesslike. “When we reach the clearing, take positions and flank the camp. On my signal, loose your arrows—no mercy. Kill everyone who resists.”
“Aye, sir!” the group chorused, slipping forward like shadows, the crack of bowstrings and soft scuffs of boots swallowed by the night as they moved.

I have to warn them, Gian thought, his breath ragged as he sprinted through the trees. Branches whipped at his face, roots clawing at his boots, but he pushed on.
From the shadows of an oak, steel flashed. A blade hissed toward him. Gian barely managed to raise his short sword in time, the clash ringing sharp in his ears. The force sent him staggering. His foot caught on a rock, and he tumbled hard onto the dirt.
The bandit pounced, sword descending. Gian twisted, catching the strike as steel scraped steel. Desperation fueled him—he lashed out with a kick, slamming the man’s knee. The bandit crumpled, and they both hit the ground, rolling in a blur of fists and snarls.
Their blades slipped from their grasp, lost in the grass. Now it was raw struggle—knuckles cracking against bone, breath heaving. Gian’s vision blurred as rough hands clamped around his throat.
“Die! Die!” the bandit—Minta—screamed, his eyes wild, foam flecking at his mouth. The strength in his grip was savage, frantic, choking the life from Gian.
Gian’s hand scraped through the dirt until it struck something cold and solid—an arrow, freshly conjured by the Void Ring.
With the last of his strength, Gian seized it and drove it upward. The arrowhead buried deep into Minta’s neck.
The bandit froze, eyes wide in disbelief, then collapsed heavily on top of him. The choking grip loosened.
Gian gasped, dragging in air like a drowning man. He shoved the lifeless body aside. For a long moment, he sat there trembling, his gaze fixed on Minta’s vacant eyes—still wide, still shocked.
The first man he had ever killed.
Silence pressed in. His chest rose and fell, each breath sharp and uneven. Then, like embers catching flame, resolve surged through him. Gian clenched his sword, rose to his feet, and tore back into the trees, running faster than ever.

Michael perched high in the branches, the rough bark digging into his palms as his eyes scanned the darkened forest. Then—movement. Shadows slipped between the trees, figures fanning out in silence, their pattern too deliberate to be chance. Bandits. They were spreading, closing the circle.
He didn’t hesitate. A longbow shimmered into being at his side. Moonlight cut through the canopy, glinting off a bandit’s cheek. Michael drew, loosed. The arrow flew true—piercing the man’s eye. He dropped without a sound.
“Bandits at the front!” Michael’s voice rang down to the camp below.
Chaos broke the stillness. 
“Damn it, they saw us!” Walter snarled, rounding on his scout.
“I—I’m sorry, sir! I didn’t think anyone would be in the trees. I only used my Echo of Pulse on the ground,” the scout stammered, pale with fear.
“What now, boss?” another asked.
“What do you think, idiot? Attack! Look at them—half of them aren’t even abled fighters!”
On the ground, Torren and Lord Henry rose as one.
“Torren, don’t let a single one through!” Lord Henry barked, charging forward.
“Kel! Baldwin! Stay close to them!” Torren added, pointing to Lady Evangeline, Clara, and Cedric.
Two archers spotted Michael and fired. He leapt from the tree, twisting in midair as the arrows hissed past where he’d been.
Walter summoned a longsword with his Echo of Sword and lunged at Torren. But Torren was ready—Echo of Strength coursing through his veins, his body gleaming silver under the Echo of Metal Skin. Their weapons clashed in a shower of sparks—then Torren’s warhammer fell like a thunderbolt, smashing Walter against a tree with a sickening crack.
“Surround him!” a bandit shouted, and four rushed in, blades flashing.
On the other side, a bandit’s Echo of Bloom burst forth—thorny roots lashing like spears toward Lord Henry. He twisted aside just in time, then countered with a blazing lance of magma that lit up the night and nearly skewered his foe.
“They’re too strong… we picked the wrong people to rob!” the Pulse Echo scout gasped—just before an arrow buried itself in his arm. Gian had arrived.
“Kill them all!” Walter roared, dragging himself upright, face twisted with rage and humiliation.
Lady Evangeline, Clara, and Cedric stayed at the rear, Kel and Baldwin bracing in front of them to block the hail of arrows. But then—a blur of shadow slipped past their defense. In an instant, claws raked across Evangeline’s back.
Time froze.
Lord Henry spun just in time to see his wife crumple, arms still wrapped protectively around Cedric.
“EVA!!!” His scream ripped through the night.
He charged toward her—but an arrow buried itself in his back, wrenching a cry from his lips. The Bloom Echo bandit conjured a spear of thorns, raising it for the killing blow—only for Gian’s arrow to whistle through the night and pierce his skull, cutting him down in an instant.
Michael switched to his shortbow, loosing arrow after arrow at the Blinkclaw as it darted into the trees.
“Mother!” Clara cried, falling to her knees beside Lady Evangeline.
“Baldwin, guard them!” Kel roared. Blood seeping from his wounded arm, he gripped the Crimson Blood Axe and charged into the forest after the beast.
Torren’s warhammer smashed into a bandit’s chest with thunderous force, hurling the corpse aside like a ragdoll.
“Protect the Lord!” he bellowed, barreling toward Henry. Blades scraped harmlessly against his silver-hardened skin, sparks flashing with every strike.
“Where do you think you’re going?” Walter snarled, directing his summoned sword to lash at Torren’s legs.
I can withstand ordinary blades, but summoned weapons are another story, Torren thought, twisting aside. The blade still grazed him, cutting shallow.
His eyes flicked across the battlefield. Kel can’t handle that Blinkclaw alone… but I can’t turn my back on this Sword Echo user either.
“Gian!” he roared. “Go after Kel! We’ll hold here!”
Gian had already realized the same. With a sharp nod, he bolted into the trees after Kel.
Meanwhile, Baldwin, Clara, and Cedric scrambled to Lord Henry’s side as he struggled to rise, blood darkening his back.

Deep in the forest, Kel thoughts raced, It’s not as fast as before. Maybe because of its previous injuries. And we’re alone... I can use my Echo here.
He raised his hand, and droplets of blood formed in the air before hardening into crimson spikes. They shot toward the Blinkclaw, but the beast twisted away with frightening speed and slashed back with a sweeping claw. Kel caught the strike with the Crimson Blood Axe, the impact jolting his weakened arm.
Gritting his teeth, he forced his injured right arm up again, summoning more blood. The droplets solidified midair, darting forward like deadly needles. Several pierced the Blinkclaw’s compact frame, drawing a hiss of pain. The creature leapt into the shadows, vanishing
Movement rustled behind him. Kel spun, swinging his axe, only for his strike to be caught on a short sword.
“Kel, it’s me!” Gian’s voice cut through the clash.
“My bad,” Kel muttered with a strained smile, his exhaustion clear from the blood loss.
“Where is it?” Gian asked, scanning the dark.
“Not sure. But it’s close. Watching us. Waiting,” Kel replied, tightening his grip.
“Hey, Gian… about this—”
“You can explain later if you want,” Gian cut him off, shifting so their backs touched. “For now, focus.”
The forest answered with violence. A claw burst from the black, slashing across Gian’s chest—chainmail useless against its speed—before the beast melted away again.
Then the pattern began: strike, vanish, stalk. In moments, both Gian and Kel were bleeding from shallow cuts, driven onto the defensive by the Blinkclaw’s relentless ambushes.
“Damn it, it’s too dark! We can’t see!” Kel growled.
As if in answer, an orb of light flared high into the sky from the camp. Soft brilliance spilled outward, banishing the shadows and turning night into pale day.
The Blinkclaw was already mid-lunge at Kel when the light caught it. Snarling, Kel raised his axe in time, deflecting the strike with a desperate swing.
The beast landed lightly on the ground, yellow eyes glowing. For a moment, it only circled them, silent and calculating, its body taut with the promise of another strike.
“Not to worry you,” Kel muttered with a faint grin, “but I’m almost done.”
His right arm dropped limp, all strength drained from it.
“Just hold on a little longer. We can do this,” Gian said, eyes locked on the wildborn, tracking its movements more clearly now under the light.
If only we could stop it from moving… he muttered desperately.
“I might be able to hold it—just for a while,” Kel rasped, vision swimming from blood loss. “But not at this speed. Even if I can see it now, I can’t catch it.”
A blur cut across their vision—another slash tore open Kel’s knee before the beast vanished again into the shadows.
“Damn… it’s killing me slow,” Kel hissed, grinning through the agony.
The Blinkclaw darted back almost instantly, its claws streaking toward Kel’s throat, only for Gian’s short sword to intercept the strike in a flash of steel. Sparks spat as the blow deflected inches from Kel’s face. 
“Thanks,” Kel panted, forcing himself upright.
I can keep pace—for now. But Kel’s fading, and the Blinkclaw knows it. That’s why it keeps pressing him. It wants him to bleed out, Gian thought, jaw tight with frustration.
Then the forest exploded.
A crushing wall of water tore through the trees, uprooting roots, snapping branches, and swallowing everything in its path. Gian’s breath caught—yet somehow the torrent curved around them, enclosing the two in a protective sphere. The Blinkclaw wasn’t so fortunate. It was caught mid-leap, hurled into the ground with bone-cracking force.
Drenched and stunned, the wildborn struggled weakly to rise.
Without hesitation, Kel gathered the blood spilling from his wounds, shaping it into a crimson hand that slammed down and pinned the beast in place.
"Now!" he shouted.
Gian didn’t hesitate. He lunged forward, both hands gripping his short sword, and drove it through the creature’s chest. The blade burst out the other side in a spray of black ichor.
The Blinkclaw gave one last twitch—then went still.
The Ascendant was finally dead.
