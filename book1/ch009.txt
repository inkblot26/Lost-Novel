Chapter 9: In That One Moment
The plaza had become a war zone.
Broken stone littered the ground. Flames devoured the shattered remnants of buildings, and dust hung heavy in the air. Soldiers and patrolmen lay scattered across the plaza—some groaning in pain, others eerily still. The few still standing were bloodied, panting, and barely able to hold their weapons.
Gideon knelt at the edge of the carnage, clutching a bleeding wound. Ragged breaths tore from him, eyes wide as they swept the chaos, hands trembling—not from fear, but helplessness.
In his unit, only Willy was barely alive, clutching his wounded stomach.
“Gideon… run! Save yourself!”
“Stop talking nonsense,” Gideon snapped, surveying the situation. “I’m not leaving you or the others here. We have a duty to protect our town.”

A short distance away, the mayor faced a lone enemy unlike any other. Every movement sent shockwaves rippling through the ground—small quakes that shattered cobblestones and hurled debris into the air. His fists pulsed with concussive force, each strike tearing through the air like a violent blast.
The mayor countered with bursts of sharp, jagged ice. His Echo flared bright, casting a blue shimmer across his frost-coated arms. A crystal wall erupted in front of him, intercepting a devastating blast. He retaliated with a flurry of ice lances, each hissing through the air.
But his movements were slowing. His breathing grew ragged, and the delay between each summon lengthened. The relentless shockwaves were wearing him down.
On one side, the soldiers’ captain fought Skareth with raw, brutal force. His summoned weapon—a glowing, translucent sword—hovered around him like a vigilant guardian, deflecting the metallic feathers hurled by the colossal winged creature.
The Skareth swooped low, its monstrous head a terrifying blend of raptor and wyvern, with fiery red eyes. It let out a piercing, sonic screech that rattled the air. The captain endured it, muscles straining as his Echo of Strength amplified his raw power to monstrous levels. But even so, he was slowly being pushed back.
A sharp, metallic feather suddenly struck his arm, drawing blood. Gritting his teeth, the captain directed his summoned sword. It shot forward in a blur of light, slashing across Skareth’s side. The creature reeled, injured—but far from defeated.
The captain didn’t pursue, as another massive Skareth swooped overhead, its golden eyes fixed, waiting for the right moment to strike while raining metallic feather blades down on the fighters below.
Aside from the two Wildborn, dozens of shadowy kangaroos—creatures formed through the Echo of Pack—fought against the soldiers and patrolmen.
At the center of the chaos stood another masked figure, cloaked in dark violet robes. Unlike the others, he wielded neither flame, ice, nor blade—but ink and paper.
Two monstrous ape-like creatures, crafted from hardened ink, rampaged through the plaza. Though born of paper and fluid, their bodies had solidified, thick, glossy, and unnaturally strong. Every movement sent tremors through the ground as they hurled soldiers aside with terrifying force. Blades and arrows struck them, leaving marks but not stopping their advance.
Finally, one of the beasts took a direct hit from a lightning echo. The blast surged through its body, charring the inky surface and causing it to stagger. A second blow followed—this time a spear charged with kinetic force—piercing deep. With a warped, guttural shriek, the creature collapsed. Its form shuddered violently before liquefying into a dark pool of ink that bubbled and steamed as it spread across the ground.
The masked man didn’t flinch. Calmly, he pulled out another sheet of parchment and, with practiced precision, sketched the outline of a massive serpent. In one motion, he slapped the paper to the ground. Dark ink bled from the page, slithering outward as the form of a colossal snake began to materialize.
Spotting an opening, Gideon nocked an arrow, channeling his Echo of Fire. Flames streaked behind the shaft as it arced toward the masked man. But before it could land, a flock of inky crows intercepted the arrow mid-flight. The collision crackled in the air, both arrow and crows dissolving into black mist on contact.
He hadn’t noticed them before, hidden by darkness and chaos, but now, glancing upward, Gideon saw them clearly—dozens of ink-like crows circling above and around their master. Some glided high, others swooped low, striking at unsuspecting targets like silent knives. A few stayed close, fluttering erratically, forming a living barrier—watchful sentries made of shadow and ink.
The masked man remained motionless, his presence alone commanding chaos as his creations tore through the crowd.
And yet, as all this unfolded, two other masked figures stood apart from the chaos. They watched silently from a crumbled rooftop, arms crossed, not lifting a finger. They were only observing, judging, waiting.
Then, from other corners of the plaza, more figures in dark violet robes began to appear—two at a time.
Gideon’s heart pounded. Every second felt heavier. He knew now this wasn’t a battle anymore.
It was an execution.

As the invisible knives were about to strike Gian, a wall of ice erupted in front of him, blocking the attack. The spear-wielding patrolwoman charged toward No. 8, her spear aimed in a piercing thrust. Despite the difficulty, her other arm still in a sling, she pressed forward with determination.
No.8 turned to her with a smile, effortlessly deflecting the unstable attack using a now-visible kukri knife.
Unnoticed in the chaos, Gian summoned a wooden sword from his Void Ring. Gritting his teeth, he sprinted toward No.8, hoping to join the fight.
Panting, the spearwoman unleashed a volley of ice shards, but No.8 dodged easily, closing the distance. Her kukri slashed toward the patrolwoman, who barely parried with her spear. 
“Ahhh!” Gian shouted, now close enough to strike as he brought his wooden sword down in a heavy slash.
No.8 easily sidestepped the attack.
“Where did that thing come from?” she muttered, surprised by the wooden weapon.

The smoke curled and twisted violently around Elder Eldon, dancing to the rhythm of his footwork. His cane sword flashed through the haze with every strike, obscuring vision and buying time.
With a roar, fire pulsed in bursts around No.7, searing through Eldon’s cover.
“You're slowing, old man,” No.7 growled as he lunged.
Eldon didn’t reply. Instead, he twisted away, conjuring a wall of smoke to mask his retreat—only to pivot back in for a surprise slash.
Then—it happened.
A faint shimmer beneath his feet—too late. Ice. His boots locked to the cracked stone floor, solid and binding. Just a single second of delay.
But enough.
No.7’s flaming blade came down in a brutal vertical slash.
Eldon tried to lift his sword, but the strike came faster.
The blade tore down his chest in a searing arc of pain. He staggered, gasped, and crumpled to the ground, blood spreading beneath him.
No.7 stood over him, smiling behind the mask.
At that exact moment, Gian turned just in time to witness the fatal thrust pierce Eldon’s heart.
His eyes widened. Tears streamed down his face, his body trembling with rage and helpless frustration.
“No!!” he screamed.
Crying out in fury, Gian lunged at No.8 with his wooden sword, his grip tightening painfully as he swung with all his strength and desperation. But just as he closed the distance, a sharp force slammed into his stomach—an invisible knife.
His breath caught.
The wooden sword slipped from his hands as he collapsed to the ground, clutching his side. Blood began to pool beneath him, his vision dimming.
No. 8’s gaze flicked to the fallen wooden sword, a brief furrow crossing her brow. Did the boy grab it in the chaos? she wondered.
Seizing the opening, the spear woman swung her weapon in a sweeping arc toward No. 8’s face, but No.8 leaned back with ease, narrowly avoiding the strike. Without missing a beat, she flicked her wrist and hurled her kukri knife in a sharp, whistling arc toward her attacker.
With the last of her strength, the spear woman summoned a thin shard of ice wall in front of her. The knife struck it, embedding deep, but it stopped.
But then… something else hit her.
She gasped, eyes widening. Looking down, she saw a different blade—smaller, sharper—buried in her chest.
“H-How…?” she whispered, her voice trembling with confusion. Her gaze flicked back to the block of ice, the kukri knife was still there.
No.8 stepped closer, smiling beneath her mask.
“Just a simple trick, darling,” she said softly.
“Two knives—one to distract, one to finish the job.”
The spear woman fell to her knees, eyes dimming as the last breath escaped her lips.
