Chapter 58: Words Carried by a Bird
There was silence in the room after Granny Emma finished her story.
Gian’s gaze shifted to Lapin, who quietly ate with his head lowered. So much pain… and he’s still just a child.
“That day was devastating for all of us,” Granny Emma said slowly, her frail voice trembling. “Many sacrificed their lives, Chief Dominic, and Lawrence… who wasn’t even given a clean death.” Tears slid down her wrinkled cheeks, her old body shaking under the weight of the memory. 
“He wasn’t even born here,” she continued softly. “When he was a young mercenary, he stumbled upon our little village. Back then, he stayed for only a short while, but in that time, he built the stone walls you see today—he wanted us to have a better chance at surviving the dangers of the Doom Forest. Then, years later, when he finally retired from the mercenary life, he returned… this time to stay. Having an Echo Master who protects us has given us peace and security, making us one of the few lesser villages that are safe from random Wildborn attacks. He became one of us.”
Kel walked over and gently patted Granny Emma’s back, handing her a handkerchief.
Gian clenched his fists, his heart twisting with emotions he couldn’t untangle—pain, pity, anger, and a hatred he didn’t know how to place. “Do you have any idea where the bandits are hiding?” he asked quietly.
Oldman sensed Gian’s intent but remained silent.
“No,” Granny Emma replied with a weary shake of her head. “We’re still trying to rebuild what little we have. Most of our able men are injured, and almost all of the hunters are dead. We can’t even venture into the forest.”
“The attack happened over a month ago, right?” Kel asked. “What are you going to do when they come for the tribute?”
“Yes,” Granny Emma said. “We’re doing what we can to harvest what crops remain in the fields outside the village. But…”
“…but you can’t give all that food away,” Gian finished for her.
She nodded sadly. “We’re secretly planning to bury some, to keep for ourselves.”
“What about water?” Oldman interjected.
“The well was destroyed during Diego’s raid,” Granny Emma said with a sigh. “We managed to patch it, but it’s barely usable. We’re short on water… but somehow we’re still managing.”
Without a word, Gian summoned the jugs of water they had stored in his Void Ring, placing them gently on the wooden table. Emma gasped, and even Lapin’s mouth fell open in disbelief.
“H-How did you do that?” the boy asked, eyes wide.
Oldman stood, Tata hopping onto his shoulder. “It’s a special magic, kid. Don’t worry about the details.” He smiled faintly. “Listen, we can’t supply water for the entire village, but if you can notify your neighbors, we’ll share what we have right now.”
“Really?” Lapin’s eyes sparkled as they remained fixed on the water jugs.
“Yes. Now go,” Oldman chuckled as he began carrying the jugs outside one by one.
Emma bowed deeply, tears streaming again. “Thank you... truly.”
“Don’t worry,” Gian said gently. “If needed, we can always go back to the Baobao Tree. It’s just a day’s travel from here.”
“You know where it is?” Granny Emma’s eyes widened. “We’ve thought about it, but all our hunters are gone, and those left are too injured or too weak. I couldn’t risk sending anyone.”
“Yes,” Oldman said as he returned inside to get another jug of water. “He found it just yesterday. If you can gather a few men strong enough to carry water, we can protect you from any Wildborn along the way.”
“Thank you… thank you so much,” Granny Emma whispered, her voice filled with deep gratitude.
After gathering a few able-bodied villagers to help carry the loads, Oldman, Gian, and Tata led and protected the group on several trips to the Baobao Tree to collect water. Kel stayed behind in the village, keeping watch in case any wildborn lurked nearby.

Meanwhile, back at Outpost Two, Thompson’s squad finally returned to the base. As soon as they arrived, Thompson went straight to Captain Kirk’s office to deliver his report while the rest of the unit stayed behind to rest.
After listening carefully, Captain Kirk gave a slow nod. “I see. It’s a good thing A-26 found you and lent a hand.”
“So he really was in the Doom Forest?” Thompson muttered without thinking. The words slipped out before he realized it, and his back stiffened. “Ah—my apologies, Captain.”
“It’s fine,” Kirk said with a faint smile. “You’ve already pieced it together yourself. But don’t speak of it to anyone else. Make sure your unit is reminded of that.”
“Yes, sir.” Thompson stood at attention.
Kirk leaned back in his chair, his expression clouding. “I just hope Gian and the others received my letters. I sent several to Graylatch Village, but simple bird messengers might not have been enough. With all the wildborn roaming the forest… I had no choice. My best couriers are still deployed at Outpost One, and the remaining few must stay here in case of an emergency.”
Thompson hesitated, his face tightening as if he wanted to say something. Kirk noticed immediately.
“What is it, Sergeant?”
With a respectful tone, Thompson answered, “Sir, we only just returned, so we’ve only now heard what happened at Outpost One. But… I would like to request that my unit be sent there as part of the reinforcements, if possible.”
Kirk’s eyes sharpened slightly. “Are you certain? Your men just returned from a dangerous mission—they’ll need time to rest. And you’re still missing members to fill your squad properly.” 
“My unit has already discussed it, Captain,” Thompson said firmly. “We’ve agreed. As for replacements, we’ll take whoever is assigned to us.”
Kirk studied him for a moment, then smiled faintly. “I see… Just like your father and grandfather, you carry the same sense of duty. Very well. I’ll approve your request. In a few days reinforcements from Outposts Three and Four will arrive here. When they do, your squad will join the reinforcement force heading to Outpost One.”
“Thank you, Captain!”
“Good. Now you’re dismissed. Go rest while you still can.”
Thompson saluted crisply, then turned and left the office.

After a few days and several trips, they finally managed to fill most of the village’s containers with fresh water.
When Gian and Oldman stepped  back into Granny Emma’s house, with Tata perched on Oldman’s shoulder, Kel immediately walked toward them, holding a letter, his expression serious. 
“Guys,” Kel began, “just after you left, Graylatch received a letter from Captain Kirk of Outpost Two.”
Oldman’s eyes narrowed. “What did he say?”
“It contained two important things,” Kel explained. “First, it’s about the situation just north of here, concerning Outpost One of Tillara City and Outpost Three of the southern wall city, Ravenscar.”
Oldman’s brows furrowed in surprise. “Wait… those two outposts are adjacent, connecting the northern part of our kingdom to the southern reach of Aethelgard Kingdom. What could have happened that would require both outposts’ attention?”
“That’s the problem,” Kel said gravely. “A massive horde attacked both outposts. At first, they held their ground with minimal damage, but the attacks didn’t stop. Day after day, the wildborn kept coming.”
Oldman shook his head, disbelief etched on his face. “That’s impossible. What you’re implying… it sounds like a large-scale, organized wildborn assault.”
Kel nodded. “Exactly. It’s not confirmed, but Captain Kirk suspects a powerful Ascendant might be leading the attacks. Of course, even if it’s on a large scale, the kingdoms could still crush it if they got serious, especially Aethelgard, the strongest of them all. But for now, both kingdoms are content to let their outposts handle it, preserving their main armies.”
Oldman’s expression darkened as he thought it through. “But still… consecutive horde attacks? That’s nothing to ignore.”
“Right. That’s also why Captain Kirk sent more reinforcements to Outpost One after we left, because the assaults haven’t stopped. That’s also why he couldn’t send a better messenger. This letter was carried by an ordinary messenger bird.” 
“And what about the second message?” Gian finally asked after listening quietly.
“It’s about the bandits,” Kel said as they all moved to sit around the dining table. Granny Emma sat quietly nearby, listening intently.
“The thing is…” Kel’s tone grew heavier. “The same bandits who raided this village were also the ones who ambushed the soldiers carrying the relief supplies.”
“Of course, we assumed that much,” Gian said, frowning. “But how can the military be so certain?”
Kel leaned forward slightly, his voice dropping. “Because of him.”
“Him?” Gian asked, puzzled.
Kel continued, his tone mysterious. “Pathfinder A-26. While conducting his own mission here in the Doom Forest, he uncovered information about the attacks on several lesser villages and the ambush on the soldiers.”
Oldman’s brows furrowed. “Wait, so you’re saying it wasn’t just Graylatch? Other nearby villages were attacked too?”
“Yes,” Kel replied grimly. “According to his report, at least two more villages were hit. And that was weeks ago. Who knows what’s happened since?”
Oldman shook his head in disbelief. “That makes no sense. Bandits don’t raid multiple villages consecutively. Every raid costs them men. They can’t sustain that.”
“And that’s exactly the problem,” Kel said, pausing for a moment before adding, “Diego, the so-called bandit leader, doesn’t care what happens to his men. Because…”
He hesitated, his eyes shifting between Gian, Oldman, and even Tata, who tilted his head curiously.
“Because what?” Gian pressed.
“Because Diego isn’t actually a man.”
The room fell silent. Even Granny Emma stopped breathing for a moment.
Gian’s expression hardened. “What do you mean… not a man?”
Kel swallowed. “According to A-26’s report, ‘Diego’ is a Mimivane, an Ascendant wildborn. A chameleon-like creature that can alter its shape, size, even facial features. It can imitate humans, or any creature, with terrifying precision. The report suggests it probably killed the original bandit leader and then assumed his identity, taking over the group in disguise.”
Oldman stood abruptly, pacing around the table. “Damn it… an Ascendant that can hide among humans. That’s worse than any normal raiding bandit.”
“It’s horrifying,” Kel agreed. “Just thinking about a wildborn that can slip into a village, or a city, pretending to be someone else… it makes you wonder how many more are out there.”
Gian clenched his fist. “But A-26… he’s a legendary Pathfinder. If he discovered this, why didn’t he do anything? Why let it go on?”
Kel sighed. “This was just a quick investigation he did while resting. Captain Kirk said A-26 is focused on something even more dangerous right now—trying to uncover the truth behind the relentless horde attacks, and whether there’s a powerful wildborn commander hiding in the shadows.”
“I see…” Gian muttered, weighing the situation. Compared to the constant horde assault, this bandit problem, though terrible, was secondary. 
“But…” Kel hesitated again.
Oldman stopped pacing. “But what, kid?”
Kel looked up at both of them, his expression grim. “But A-26 did pinpoint the bandits’ headquarters.”
