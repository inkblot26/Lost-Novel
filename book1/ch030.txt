Chapter 30: What Was Taken
It was a blistering summer day, a year ago, in the modest village of Dimuara, perched on the edge of the Unknown, southeast of Tillara City. The air shimmered above the dusty paths, cicadas droning from the trees that bordered the forest’s dark edge. 
“Althea, stay here in the village, alright? I’ll be back by afternoon. I need to head into the forest because someone needs my help,” her father said, adjusting the satchel across his shoulder. 
“Okay, Daddy! I’ll just play with the others,” Althea beamed, her smile bright as sunlight. She skipped off toward her friends.
“Look what I got for my birthday last week!” she announced, proudly holding up a small bottle of perfume.
“Don’t worry. I’ll keep an eye on her,” one of the village elders said with a nod.
“Thank you, Elder,” the man replied.
“It is we who should thank you,” the elder said with quiet reverence. “Your Echo of Healing has eased so many of our pains.”
The man smiled, gave Althea one last lingering look, then turned toward the forest path.
But when he returned that afternoon, the village was unrecognizable.
Smoke curled from toppled cookfires, carrying the bitter scent of charred grain. Cries of grief carried through the air. Belongings lay scattered like the aftermath of a storm, trampled into the dirt. 
“What happened?!” he shouted, pushing through the chaos.
“They… they took them…” a woman sobbed on her knees.
“Took who?!”
“All the girls… all the twelve-year-olds. They took my angel! They took her!” She clutched her chest as if her heart had been ripped away.
His pulse thundered in his ears. He ran straight for the elder’s hut, hope and dread clashing inside him.
“Elder! Where’s Althea? What happened?!”
The elder sat silent. Even with the wailing outside, his silence was heavier. At last, his voice broke through, low and burdened. 
“A squad of soldiers from the city came. They demanded every twelve-year-old girl, saying it was for… military preparation once they turn thirteen.”
The man’s eyes darkened. “That’s a lie. There’s no such law in Tillara.”
“I know.” The elder’s voice shook. “We tried to resist. Some were cut down. Others imprisoned. I’m sorry… Althea was taken. We could not stop them.”
The world tilted. His breath came ragged. “No… not my Althea.” He bolted outside, fury propelling him.
“Tata! Track her perfume scent!” he commanded.
From his shoulder, a small creature leapt—a Driftmouse wildborn. Barely the size of a fist, it was a mutated rodent with dark brown fur, an elongated body, flexible limbs, and sharp, clawed toes built for stealth. It pressed its nose to the dirt, whiskers twitching, then darted ahead, relying on scent and vibrations to lead the way.
If they came from Tillara, they’ll have taken the main road. He sprinted after his companion, heart burning with a father’s desperate resolve.
After a long, relentless pursuit, the man reached the eastern gate of Tillara, the one that opened into the noble district. 
“Where are they taking them…?” His voice was barely a breath, dread tightening in his chest.
Up ahead, Tata halted, whiskers twitching. Through their bond came a single message. A mansion. 
The man’s jaw tightened. That direction… it can only be one place. Lord Ronald of Rosendale.
He slipped into the shadows cast by manicured hedges and marble statues, his movements careful, every breath measured. In this district, a commoner could lose his life for the crime of being seen.
“Tata,” he whispered. “What’s the situation?”
The Driftmouse crouched low in the grass near the mansion’s perimeter, relaying what it had witnessed.
“Four masked men… in violet robes,” the man muttered, confused. “They left the front door… then walk to the forest?” His brow furrowed. Who are they?
Before he could dwell further, the mansion’s door burst open.
Ronald stumbled out, bloated, red-faced, gray hair in disarray. His expensive clothes were thrown on in haste, one slipper missing. He didn’t look back. He only fled, wild-eyed, into the darkening streets, his panic louder than any confession.
The man’s fists clenched. His prey was finally within reach.
Without hesitation, he crossed into the mansion’s threshold.
Night was falling.
The once-grand estate had become a ruin. Broken glass crunched under his boots. Vases lay smashed along the walls, their porcelain shards glittering faintly in the dim light. Deep gashes scarred the polished floorboards, echoes of a violent struggle.
Bodies littered the hall. Men lay twisted and still, white-fletched arrows jutting from skulls, blood soaking into the carpets in wide, dark stains.
Knife in hand, he climbed the staircase. Tata followed silently at his heel, eyes darting back, claws flexing.
At the top, another corpse slumped against the wall. Blood dripped steadily from the two precise sword wounds that split its chest.
On the second floor, the reception hall was carnage. Bookshelves had toppled, spilling their contents like broken bones. Fragments of teacups lay scattered across the floor, sharp against the firelight that still flickered faintly in the hearth. Before it, a man’s body sprawled face-down, a single stab wound marking his heart.
The man pressed on, reaching a door slightly ajar at the side. From it seeped a bitter stench—chemical, sharp, wrong.
He pushed it open.
The air hit him like a wall. Bittersweet poison  clung to the floor in curling greenish vapors. Several young girls lay scattered across the room, limbs twisted unnaturally, lips tinged blue.
Then his gaze found her.
Althea.
Near the far wall. Eyes closed. Skin pale.
The knife slipped from his hand, clattering to the floor.
“No… no, no, no…” His knees gave way. He gathered her in his arms, desperate, trembling. Her body was cold against his chest.
A sound tore from his throat, not a word, not even a cry, just a raw, broken scream as he held her close, his grief echoing through the shattered mansion.

Kel met his gaze. “Thank you for the warning,” he said calmly. “But we have even more reason to stay. Right, Gian?”
Gian gave a small, wordless nod.
Oldman frowned. “Why? What could possibly be worth risking your lives?”
Kel didn’t answer immediately. He asked instead, quietly, “Have you heard of the Eclipse of Tala?”
Oldman’s eyes narrowed. “Yes. Four years ago. It made headlines. Wait, you said you were from a nearby town earlier… you didn’t mean Tala, did you?”
Kel nodded. “We did.” He kept his voice flat. “Hundreds died that night—mayor, soldiers, patrols, civilians. The north and west districts were wiped out.”
Gian’s voice cut in, cold and measured, each word deliberate. “My mentor… my best friend… my mother… my brother. All gone that night.”
“And no one ever found out why,” Kel added. “The King of Lanthea didn’t even send investigators. He just appointed a new mayor like nothing happened.”
Oldman’s face darkened. “You saw them? The ones who did it?”
Gian met his gaze and did not flinch. “Yes. A group of masked men in dark violet robes.”
A heavy silence sat between them. Kel leaned back, a slow, unreadable smile forming at the corner of his mouth.
“Well… I guess it’s my turn to tell a story.”
But the smile didn’t reach his eyes. Something darker flickered there—an ember of something he’d been keeping carefully hidden.
“I told you I grew up in a restaurant,” he began, voice low. “My parents ran the place. We lived a simple, honest life. My father wasn’t good at much except cooking. And my mother, strict as she was, had a talent for managing everything. They balanced each other perfectly.” He let a faint, sad smile ghost across his face and then vanish.
“That night we were prepping for the dinner rush. Then we heard it—a monstrous screech, and the ground felt like it was breaking. Chaos exploded everywhere: fire, shouting, people running.”
Gian listened in silence.
“People ran screaming in every direction. Chaos. Fire. Screams. My father grabbed the axe hanging on the wall of the restaurant, and we rushed back toward our home, near the north gate.” 
Kel’s eyes narrowed, jaw clenched.
“But then we saw it—the battle. The road was blocked by a thick wall of mud. There, in the middle of it all, I saw a patrolman glowing with silver light, shielding a mother and her child from a rain of arrows using his Echo of Metal Skin. On a rooftop above them… a masked man.”

“I was sixteen,” Kel continued, his voice shaking slightly. “Just a few months away from finishing military training. I was arrogant. I thought I was strong. I thought this is what I was trained to do.”
He gave a bitter laugh.
“I ran toward them, thinking I could protect the mother and her child. My mother screamed for me to come back. My father chased after me.” Kel stared at the ground, voice hollow. 
“Before I could reach them, another masked man appeared behind the patrolman. His spear blazed with a radiant yellow halo. In a blink, he drove it through the patrolman’s back. And when others tried to stop him… every weapon that clashed with that spear shattered like paper. Nothing could stop it.”
His fist clenched. “An arrow struck me, and I fell. The spear-user came straight at me. But my father… he caught up.”
Kel’s voice broke. “They fought—my father’s Crimson Blood Axe against that spear. Somehow the axe held. The spear-user took an interest in it. They fought. My mother tried to drag me away.”
Kel paused, struggling to hold it together.
“Then… it happened. An arrow struck my mother in the head.”
His voice cracked. Silence followed, heavy and suffocating.
“My father fought with everything he had to protect me. I was useless, no weapon, no strength, just lying there,” Kel said, his voice trembling. 
“Then an arrow hit him in the side. The bow-user… he walked toward us calmly, a sword sheathed at his side. My father stumbled. He looked at me, and with his last breath, he shouted—run.”
Tears welled in Kel’s eyes.
“I couldn’t move. I couldn’t do anything. And then… the spear pierced my father’s stomach. Right in front of me.”
Gian said nothing. He simply placed a firm, steady hand on Kel’s back.
Kel wiped his eyes and continued through clenched teeth.
“The bow-user picked up my father's axe. The spear-user impaled the mother and child with mud spikes. Then… they argued over who would get to keep the axe.” His voice rose with fury.
“They tossed a coin to decide. Right in front of me.” He shook with rage.
“I thought I’d die that night. I wanted to die. But I didn’t. The bowman won the axe. As they left, he told the spearman to let me live. Said if I wanted revenge, I was welcome to try. He laughed. The spearman said it went against the ‘code’ to let me live. But the bowman didn’t care.”
Kel’s voice quieted, low and dangerous.
“That night… I swore I’d hunt them. Every last one of them.”
He looked down at the axe resting at his side and placed a hand on it, his grip firm and sure.
“When I returned home, I went to my parents room. There, untouched, was the other relic Crimson Blood Axe, its twin. Beside it, a Verdant Echo, both passed down for generations from our ancestors.” 
“A rare Echo,” Gian said softly.
Kel nodded. “The Echo of Blood.”
The candle burned low, wax pooling at its base. Beyond the window, the city carried on as if nothing had happened. In the silence, the vow lingered—three men bound by loss, by rage, by vengeance.
