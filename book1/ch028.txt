Chapter 28: Kingsfall
Inside, the Dice Den was a low-ceilinged furnace of smoke and noise. Pipe fumes curled thick beneath blackened rafters, trapped in the stale air like a second roof. The smell was layered—cheap tobacco, spilled ale soaked deep into warped floorboards, sweat that had nowhere to escape.
Laughter rose too loud. Curses cracked like whips. Somewhere near the back, a man shouted in triumph, only to be drowned out by the violent slam of a mug.
The plastered walls bowed inward with age, stained amber and gray. Old knife gouges scarred the wood beams—tallies of past disagreements settled without mercy. Round tables filled the hall, tight enough that chairs scraped constantly against one another. Each table was its own battlefield—some for quiet calculation, others for spectacular ruin.
Gian and Kel edged toward a table already in play. Several heads turned.
“Looks like another pup who can’t tell heaven from hell,” one man sneered, teeth stained dark.
“Let ’em sit,” another chuckled. “They’ll cry soon enough.”
The dealer stood at the center of it all like a conductor. Hair slicked flat. Rings glinting on every finger. His smile was immaculate—too immaculate.
“Gentlemen,” he purred smoothly, voice cutting through the din without effort. “Care to test fate?”
The table they approached felt different from the others—quieter, heavier. The three players here didn’t shout. They watched.
The brute with pipe smoke rolling from his nostrils barely looked at them. “This is the big leagues, kids. Piss off.” 
“Now, now,” murmured the slender old woman, half-hidden behind an embroidered fan. Her laugh was soft, but her eyes glittered like polished glass. “Let the lambs approach the altar.”
“The stake is ten gold fangnotes,” added a rotund nobleman, cheeks flushed and eyes small as coins. His grin stretched wide, fake as painted porcelain.
Kel froze. “Ten gold? That’s steep.”
“Let’s find another table,” Gian muttered, already pulling away.
“Wait,” Kel said, flashing a grin and slapping down a fangnote. “One round won’t kill us.”
“Kel, we each got forty extra gold from Lord Henry. That's too much to risk on one game,” Gian said flatly. 
“Just one game, please. I've got a good feeling about this. I promise—after this, you can manage all our savings,” Kel begged. 
“Fine,” Gian agreed. 
The nobleman’s grin widened like a trap snapping shut. “Now we’re talking.” He motioned to two empty chairs.
With a long sigh, Gian sat beside Kel and laid down his own note.
“Don’t come crying to your mama when you lose that, kid,” the brute scoffed through a cloud of smoke.
“So what’s the game?” Kel asked, leaning forward with the enthusiasm of someone who had no idea what he was walking into.
“This table’s for Kingsfall,” the dealer said smoothly, sweeping up the bets and passing two dice to each player.
“Kel… what’s Kingsfall?” Gian whispered.
The table erupted. Someone nearly spat out their drink, another slammed the table in wheezing laughter. Even the nobleman almost tumbled from his chair.
“My dear boy,” he guffawed, wiping tears from his cheeks. “Why are you even here?”
“Let’s not be rude,” the old woman cut in, her fan fluttering as she gave Gian a smile sharp enough to cut. “Dealer, be a gentleman. Explain the rules for our… newcomers.”
The muscular man groaned. “This is a waste of time.”
“We’re gambling because we’ve got nothing better to do, young man,” the old woman replied with a sly wink.
The dealer straightened, his smile practiced. “Kingsfall is played with two six-sided dice. Each player rolls both dice at once. Land exactly seven, and that’s a true Kingsfall—you win the round instantly.”
He tapped the ringed box with a knuckle. “If no one crowns, the round goes to whoever lands the closest number above seven. Anyone below seven is a Fallen—unworthy of the crown. Out. And if all fall? The house takes the pot.” 
“The house also takes one gold per winner, per round,” he added smoothly. “That’s the law in the Dice Den.”
“I see,” Gian said. “What if more than one hits a Kingsfall, or ties for closest?”
“In that case, the tied players can either split the pot or roll again to determine a final winner,” the dealer explained.
“Alright,” Gian murmured, gripping the dice.
“Ha! Good luck, kid,” the chubby nobleman snorted, lifting his drink in mock salute before downing half of it.
Dice tumbled into the shallow ring, rattling against scarred wood. The sharp clatter cut clean through the haze of smoke and drunken chatter.
Gian held his breath. His dice spun, tumbled, then stilled. Three and four.
Kingsfall.
He blinked. For a moment, he thought he misread them.
Then Kel's groan reached him. “I'm Fallen.”
“Double Kingsfall!” the dealer declared. Gian glanced around. The noble’s dice showed a two and five.
“Wow… how rare is that?” someone murmured nearby.
“No way! You hit Kingsfall on your first try?” Kel blurted, eyes wide.
The noble smirked. “Well then, lucky boy, split the pot, or shall we roll for the crown?” 
“Roll,” Gian said without hesitation.
The slender old woman laughed softly behind her fan. “I like his spirit.”
The dealer cleared his throat. “Fall!” Dice were passed, and the two rolled again. The crowd leaned in, a hush falling. 
The dice tumbled, bounced, then landed. Gian—six and one. Noble—two and four.
The noble cursed under his breath. “Fallen. Damn it.”
“Gian, you got Double Crown!” Kel exclaimed.
“What’s that?” Gian asked, still catching up.
The noble forced a smile. “Means you've rolled Kingsfall twice in a row. Enjoy it while it lasts, boy.” 
The dealer slid a neat stack of fangnotes toward Gian. “House cut taken.”
“Again,” the muscular man barked, already tossing in another note.
“Okay. Let's go,” Gian said, rising from his chair. 
“Hey, how can you just leave after one round when you just won?” the muscular man demanded, glaring at Gian. 
“Um... he's right, Gian. You just won your very first round.It's not a good idea to leave now. I can stop, but you should keep playing,” Kel whispered, noticing the way everyone at the table was staring at them. 
“Fine. We'll stop once we lose,” Gian said, sitting back down as Kel stepped behind him. 
“That's the way, boy,” the nobleman said, flashing a broad grin. 
The call went up. Dice rolled. Gian looked down. Four and three.
“Kingsfall!” the dealer announced.
Groans rippled around the table. Another round. Another roll. Another seven.
The noble ran a hand through his thinning hair, glaring. “Golden boy,” he muttered.
The old woman folded her fan. “I think I’ll retire my purse for a moment before it’s emptied.”
The muscular man shoved back his chair. “I’ll be at the bar. Better use for my gold than feeding this brat’s luck.”
The laughter around the table died. Even the surrounding gamblers began drifting closer, their conversations fading into uneasy murmurs. 
"Three in a row..." someone whispered.
"That's not luck."
Kel couldn't stop smiling. “Wow! You just won three straight rounds! We're gonna be rich!” 
The chubby noble leaned back in his chair, grin sliding across his face. “Looks like it’s just the two of us, boy. What do you say we raise the stakes? Thirty fangnotes this round?”
Gian’s reply was flat, unamused. “Ten is fine.”
The noble’s sneer deepened. “You’re no fun.”
Before the dealer could call the round, a voice cracked through the haze of smoke and laughter.
“Patrick! Bob!”
Heads turned. A man strode toward the table, boots heavy against the warped planks. A wide-brimmed gambler’s hat shadowed most of his face, but his voice carried sharp authority.
“There you two are,” he barked, eyes flicking to Gian and Kel. “Gambling away the master’s coin?”
He jabbed a finger toward them. “Up. Finish this round, then we’re leaving.”
Gian froze, his heart tightening. I've been careless. Rolling exactly seven every round was bound to attract the wrong kind of attention.
Gian's eyes narrowed. Whoever this man was, he'd just given them the perfect excuse to leave. 
“Yes, sir,” he said quickly, masking his unease. He shoved the dice into Kel’s hand with a sharp elbow. “Roll it. I told you the master wouldn’t wait forever.”
“Fall!” the dealer called.
Kel tossed the dice. They tumbled, bounced, and stopped—five and four. The nobleman rolled a pair of fours.
“Winner!” the dealer barked, sliding the pot across.
“Bad luck,” Gian muttered, tugging Kel along. “We need to go before the master has both our hides.”
The nobleman’s smile faltered, his gaze sharpening. “Leaving so soon?”
The stranger nodded smoothly. “They’re being summoned by our master. That call can’t be ignored.” He didn’t wait for protest, just turned on his heel and pushed through the haze. Gian and Kel followed, their shadows trailing as the Dice Den closed behind them.

When they stepped back into the street, the noise of the city hit like surf.
Wagon wheels groaned under weight. Vendors shouted about river fish and cheap linens. A pair of city guards argued over patrol routes near a bakery stall. Gian and Kel were swept along the crowded street, forced into a brisk pace. 
“Hey, what’s going on? Who is this guy?” Kel whispered, his confusion written across his face. 
“Shut your mouth and keep walking,” the stranger snapped, his voice sharp, carrying an authority that cut through the noise.
Gian’s eyes narrowed, calm but alert. “We’re being followed,” he muttered under his breath.
Kel blinked. “Huh?” Then, stealing a glance around, his eyes widened. “Oh…” 
“I know. Just act natural,” the stranger said without slowing. He led them through the press of people, weaving with practiced ease.
At the corner, he veered sharply into a narrow alley, shadows swallowing them whole. Another turn, then another, darting through crooked side lanes, slipping past shuttered shops and laundry lines swaying overhead.
They merged briefly with a crowd at a fountain plaza, the din of splashing water and chattering townsfolk masking their trail. Gian risked a glance back—nothing. Whoever had been tailing them was gone.
Only after several more detours did the stranger finally ease his pace. At the city’s edge, beneath the shade of an old oak, he stopped and exhaled. “Alright. We’ve shaken them.”
He pulled off his brown leather gambler’s hat, wiping sweat from his brow. In the daylight, his face was plain but weathered, marked by a long scar across his forehead.
“Do you have any idea how close you came to getting killed?” His tone was harsh, like a reprimand. “Cheating in a den is one thing, but even if you weren’t, winning that much is like throwing blood into the water.” 
“But we didn’t cheat,” Kel protested.
“Doesn’t matter,” the man said bluntly. “The crowd thinks you did. No explanation changes that. They’d slit your throats the moment you stepped outside.”
“My bad. I didn’t know we’d keep on winning,” Kel muttered.
“I’m the one at fault,” Gian admitted. “I should’ve realized sooner.”
The stranger sat down with a sigh. “What’s done is done. I just want to remind you to be careful.”
“Thanks,” Gian said, meaning it. 
“First time gambling?” the man asked, eyeing them.
“Yes,” Gian admitted, drinking from his waterskin.
“Well… not really,” Kel said with a sheepish grin. “First time winning though. And first time in the city!” 
“You’re not even the one who won,” the man scoffed. Then he paused, brow furrowing. “Wait. Your first day in Tillara?”
Kel nodded proudly. “Yep.”
“Which I’ve been reminding you about. We still don’t have an inn to stay in,” Gian said, sounding serious.
“Where’d you come from?” the stranger asked. 
“Nearby town,” Kel said. “Finished an escort job.” 
The man’s eyes hardened. “Then listen. Don’t head back into the main street. Ronald will have his men searching already.”
Kel’s smile faded. “So… what do we do?”
“If you’ve got nowhere else, you can stay with me. I live outside the gates,” the stranger offered, tone guarded. Then, after a beat, he added, “That is… if you trust me.”
Kel glanced at Gian.
“Fine. Lead the way,” Gian said flatly.
The man slid his hat back on. “Another lesson—don’t trust strangers so easily. Not in this city.”
Kel grinned. “Well, my name’s Kel. And this gloomy, serious guy who’s shorter than me is Gian. Nice to meet you.”
Gian raised his hand for a shake. The man looked at it, sighed, and finally clasped it.
“Call me Oldman. That’s all the name I’ve got left.”
He chuckled once, shaking his head. “You’re too trusting… or too confident. Either way, follow me.”
With that, he turned and set off down the dirt road beyond the city gates, the two younger men trailing close behind.
