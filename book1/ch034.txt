Chapter 34: Old Bonds, New Ties
Mist began to pour from one of the soldiers, thick and fast, swallowing the moonlit road and turning the open fields into a choking blue haze. Sergeant Claren summoned a swirling sphere of water before him as five soldiers charged the carriage with melee weapons raised. Two more—one on each flank—loosed arrows. 
One of the arrows struck a newly assigned guard just as Edmund passed by, prompting him to move closer to the carriage.
“Defend the carriage!” Thomas shouted, releasing a gust of wind in a wide arc to disperse the mist that was meant to conceal the approaching attackers.
“Wind? Damn it... bad matchup!” the soldier with echo of water that uses it as a mist shouted as he drew his bow.
Thomas glided toward the incoming soldiers, wind-enhanced speed carrying him forward. He dodged arrows fired from one soldier at the flank, while another continued targeting those near the carriage.
The guards fought back, but they were heavily outnumbered.
“Jude, guard the other side of the carriage!” Edmund shouted to the only Echo user among the new recruits—a young fighter wielding a summoned spear.
“Yes, sir!” Jude responded, deflecting a soldier’s sword before circling around the carriage.
Thomas dodged a swipe from another soldier’s blade, then countered with a quick strike to the throat, killing his attacker.
Suddenly, Claren hurled a high-pressure spinning ball of water at one of the guards. It slammed into the man’s chest with a thunderous splash, hurling him backward across the wet dirt road before he landed hard and motionless.
Nearby, the guards' only archer loosed an arrow toward the bowman soldier. Both archers fired at each other, ducking and weaving between attacks.
Then, beside Edmund, a guard fell under the combined assault of an axe-wielding soldier and a swordsman. 
This is bad. We need to do something, he thought, considering whether to drive the carriage forward, though doing so would leave Thomas and the others behind.
As he struggled to fight off the soldiers and decide what to do, an arrow suddenly struck the enemy bowman at the side, killing him.
“Gian! Kel!” Edmund shouted, spotting Gian, Kel, and another man sprinting toward them.
Seeing the reinforcements, Claren barked a command to the mist user. “Intercept them!” he ordered, while preparing to engage Thomas himself.
“Yes, sir!” the soldier replied, releasing mist in Gian’s direction while firing arrows. The mist didn’t block his vision, but he failed to notice the small figure darting in from the side and clawing at his eyes, blinding him. Screaming in pain, he dropped his bow. Oldman arrived a heartbeat later and stabbed him through the chest.
Claren quickly assessed the situation. Gian’s group had arrived, shifting the numbers to eight against six. 
“Retreat!” he shouted.
But Edmund didn’t let them go. “Jude! After them!” he called, urging the others to pursue.
Kel charged in, hacking his axe into the back of a soldier fighting beside Edmund.
“Where do you think you’re going?!” Thomas shouted at Sergeant Claren, dodging a spinning ball of water and hurling his dagger.
At the last second, the dagger shifted course slightly, striking Claren’s hand.
“Ugh!” Claren grunted in pain, stumbling. As he turned to flee, an arrow hit his leg, dropping him to the ground.
Thomas seized the moment, reached him, and ended it.
After the battle ended, Thomas approached Gian and the others.
“Nice timing. Thank you for helping us.”
Edmund stepped forward and opened the carriage door.
“Lady Clara, it’s safe now,” he announced.
Inside, Clara was on the floor, holding Cedric tightly, shielding him just in case.
“Lady Clara is here?” Kel said, quickly jumping to his feet from where he had been sitting.
Clara and Cedric stepped out of the carriage, their eyes falling on the aftermath—the narrow road strewn with fallen soldiers and guards, dark blood soaking into the dirt between wagon ruts.
“Gian, Kel—you’re here,” Clara said, her voice filled with relief.
“They helped us, my lady. We wouldn’t have won without them,” Thomas added as he wiped the blood from his dagger.
“Once again, you two have saved us. Thank you.” Clara gave them a graceful bow. Cedric, seeing her, followed suit and bowed as well.
“No need to thank us. We were just in the area and saw what was happening,” Gian replied, glancing around at the fallen enemies.
“Yeah, it was no big deal, my lady. The great Kel is always here to protect you,” Kel said proudly, striking a pose like a knight in shining armor.
“Whoa! Is that a Wildborn?” Cedric exclaimed, pointing at Tata, who was now perched on Oldman’s shoulder. He ran toward them with curiosity.
“What’s going on?” Gian asked, frowning slightly.
“Well… I suppose I owe you at least an explanation,” Thomas said, glancing at Clara.
“It’s alright. They’re not strangers, we know we can trust them,” Clara said gently. 
“Then I’ll be quick,” Thomas replied.
As Thomas explained the situation to Gian and Kel, Edmund and the others began moving the dead bodies to the roadside and covering them, hoping to buy time in case anyone came by. They would need to be gone before then.

After listening to the story, Gian and Kel remained silent for a moment, then exchanged a glance.
“Oldman, the two of us need to leave for now,” Gian said, turning to him.
“Where are you going?” Oldman asked, letting Tata continue playing with Cedric.
“We need to return to the city and assist Lord Henry, especially if something big is about to happen there. It might also help us capture Ronald and uncover more about the masked men,” Kel replied.
Clara and the others turned to them in surprise.
“Kel, Gian, what do you mean?” Clara asked.
“We have business with the younger brother of the Highlord. Since your father might be planning something against the Highlord, helping him benefits us too,” Gian explained, his gaze drifting toward the distant silhouette of Tillara’s stone walls, faint against the horizon beneath the paling night sky.
Clara looked down at her hands and quietly said, “Thank you.” 
“It’s nothing, my lady. And like Gian said, it’s hitting two birds with one stone,” Kel added with a grin.
“Are you really prepared for this?” Oldman asked. The moonlight revealed the scar on his forehead.
“Yes,” Gian and Kel answered together.
“I see. Then follow me. I know a secret passage that will get us into the city,” Oldman said.
“I guess this is goodbye again,” Kel said to Clara and the others.
Clara smiled. “Thank you. Goodbye.”
“Please tell Lord Henry and Sir Torren what happened here,” Thomas added with a smile.
“We will,” Gian replied.
“Goodbye, guys. Let’s meet again someday,” Edmund said as he climbed onto a horse to scout ahead
the carriage.
“Goodbye!” Cedric shouted from the window as the carriage rolled forward.
Oldman turned to Gian. “Are you ready?”
“Yes,” Gian replied, turning to face the direction of Tillara.
“Alright, let’s go. There is someone you need to talk to,” Oldman added mysteriously.

Later that night, inside Tillara City—after slipping through a narrow passage beneath the outer wall—Kel asked, “Where are we going, man?” as they approached the quiet edge of the western district. They passed rows of simple homes before stopping in front of one and knocking on the door. 
“It’s me,” Oldman said.
The door opened to reveal a large man with bulging arms, standing in the doorway and eyeing Gian and Kel with a threatening stare.
“Dareth, they’re fine. They’re with me. I need to meet with her,” Oldman said.
“Did anyone follow you?” Dareth asked, glancing warily outside.
“Who do you think you’re talking to?” Oldman replied, stepping inside and leading Gian and Kel in with him.
“She’s in a meeting,” Dareth called out as the trio moved down the hallway.
They entered a dimly lit room where heavy curtains blocked the windows. A single candle burned at the center of the table, its flame steady despite the faint draft slipping under the door. Lady Rose sat at the table, facing Lord Henry, who was seated across from her. Sir Torren stood behind him, arms crossed, his gaze sharp.
“Lady Rose, I have something to report,” Oldman announced.
At the same time, Gian and Kel both spoke.
“Lord Henry?”
Lord Henry looked up in surprise.
“Gian? Kel? What are you doing here?”
Kel blinked, turning to Oldman. “Wait... did you just say Lady Rose? As in the Lady Rose of Rosendale?”
Lady Rose looked to Henry with a calm curiosity. “Do you know them?”
“Yes,” Lord Henry replied. “They were with me when I traveled from Tala to Tillara. They're trusted friends.”
“I see,” Lady Rose said with a faint smile. “Why don’t the three of you sit down and share your report first?”
She gestured to the chairs nearby, inviting them to join the meeting.
After explaining what had happened outside the city, Lord Henry bowed to Gian, Kel, and Oldman.
“Thank you again for saving my children. I owe you.” 
“Glad we were there in time,” Kel said with a grin. Gian just gave a quiet nod. 
“We were lucky you happened to be in the area,” Sir Torren added.
“Thank you for the report, Oldman. That’s important information,” Lady Rose said, then turned to Henry.
“I’m afraid you’ll need to go into hiding now, Lord Henry. It’s only a matter of time before the missing soldier patrol is discovered. Once that happens, and the tip you gave to the western gate guards allowing your family to slip will be exposed and everything will be traced back to you.”
“You’re right,” Lord Henry replied. “Torren, send word to Michael and the other newly recruited guards. Tell them to leave the mansion immediately.”
Torren gave a sharp nod and left the room without another word.
“I know a place where we can keep you safe, Lord Henry. You don’t need to worry,” Lady Rose said. “But before we proceed with the arrangements…”
She paused and looked at Gian and Kel.
“Lord Henry, whatever you’re planning to do, we want to be part of it,” Kel said, locking eyes with him.
“Very well,” Henry said. “Then once again, I’ll ask for your help.”
“May I ask what exactly you are after?” Lady Rose asked, her voice calm but her eyes sharp.
“We need to capture your brother, Ronald,” Gian said, watching her closely for a reaction.
“They’re alright, Lady Rose. They want the same thing I do,” Oldman spoke up. “I vouch for them.”
“I see,” Lady Rose replied. “My youngest brother may not be my top priority, but I do intend to deal with him. And as I promised you, you may do whatever you wish to him so long as you help me overthrow Richard.”
“Thank you, Lady Rose,” Oldman said with a respectful nod.
“We are short on time, Lord Henry. I’ll need the funds now to arm my people,” she continued.
“Here,” Henry said, handing her a small relic box filled with tightly stacked fangnotes, the faint clink of metal echoing softly in the quiet room.
“Thank you, Lord Henry. These funds will be a great help to our revolt.” Lady Rose bowed slightly.
“Sam will escort you to a safe location,” she added, as a man stepped quietly into the dim room. “I’ll need a few days to make preparations and avoid suspicion from my brother. Let’s meet again tomorrow night. By then, we’ll observe his reaction to what happened tonight and begin planning our next move.”
“Thank you. I’ll be in your care,” Henry said with gratitude.
“Oldman, I’ll need you to gather more information with the help of your adorable driftmouse,” Lady Rose said with a faint smile, glancing at the Wildborn on his shoulder.
“No problem, Lady Rose,” Oldman replied. He turned to Gian and Kel. “I’ll see you both tomorrow evening. Take care.”
“Stay safe,” Gian said, as he, Kel, and Lord Henry followed Sam out of the room toward the safe house.
Now alone in the candlelit chamber, the last of the footsteps fading beyond the door, Lady Rose lowered her gaze to the trembling candle flame and whispered, “Finally... now we really have a chance.”
