Chapter 66: Scar of the Past
Gian sat on the ground, panting. El stood over Diego’s reptilian face, her ice greatsword shrinking and melting into a dagger of frost. Without hesitation, she drove it down into the Wildborn’s skull with a sharp, decisive thrust, the frost blade cracking bone with a dull, sickening sound.
Noticing Gian’s stare, El said, “What? This is an Ascendant. We can’t leave its core behind.” She split the skull open, revealing a small, perfectly round core that gleamed with a deep, glassy green. Carefully, she wiped away the Wildborn’s blood.
“So that’s a core,” Gian said, still catching his breath.
“Yeah,” El replied. “Worth a lot of fangnotes. Useless for us, but for someone with the Echo of Shape, it’s valuable—perfect for forging a relic or echotech. She slipped the core into her pocket. “Don’t worry, we’ll split it later. I’m not keeping it for myself.”
“Or, if we can’t find a buyer, we could try hiring a skilled crafter to make Whispering Scrolls from it,” El added, tapping the core through the fabric of her pocket. 
“Isn’t that expensive and risky?” Gian asked. 
“Yeah. There’s a chance we could waste the core completely if the process fails. Still, it’s cheaper than hiring an Echo Shaper, though those experts could definitely make at least two scrolls from this. Anyway, we’ll decide with the others.” 
“Let’s go. We need to find them,” Gian said, pushing himself to his feet.
Suddenly, a branch above them shook. Something dropped down and landed lightly on the ground. Gian looked up in surprise.
It was Anni.
“You used it?!” El’s voice sharpened as her eyes locked onto Anni.
Confused, Gian glanced between the two.
“I’m sorry, Auntie El,” Anni said quickly, lowering her gaze. “I couldn’t find you and didn’t know if you were safe, so… I used it, just a little. Don’t worry, I made sure no one saw me.” She stopped short when her gaze met Gian’s.
“Is that…?” Anni asked, changing the topic as she stepped toward the Wildborn’s body. 
“Yeah. Diego,” El said. “We managed to kill it.” She glanced toward the trees. “Let’s get back to the others.”
Tata came scampering from the underbrush and leapt onto El’s shoulder.
“Guide us, little one,” El said with a faint smile. The driftmouse squeaked in reply.

It took some time, but Gian and the others finally found their way back to Oldman and Kel, who was awake now but still lying down to rest.
“It’s good you’re safe,” Oldman said.
“Yeah… and we finished Diego,” Gian replied, giving a quick rundown of what had happened. 
“Unfortunately, we didn’t find any storage relic on Diego’s body, so we still don’t know where he hid those fangnotes,” El said.
“And that stupid Maro didn’t know either when we interrogated him yesterday,” Anni added, dropping onto one of the stones with a scowl.
“And I didn’t find that Glimstep,” El said, her tone sharpening at the memory. “I swear I’ll kill it next time.”
“Maybe that Ascendant fled far after what you did to it,” Gian said.
“Yeah. The important thing is, we did what needed to be done. Diego’s finally dead,” Kel said.
Anni shot him a cold look. “From what I heard, we were the ones who actually did something. All you did was try to kill your friends.”
“Anni—manners,” El scolded. “Don’t be rude. The guy did try.”
As the group talked, Gian noticed Oldman had been unusually quiet, his expression distant, eyes fixed somewhere far beyond the group ever since hearing the story.
“What’s wrong?” Gian asked. The conversation died instantly.
“It’s nothing… it’s just that I’ve met Jorgumo before.” Oldman said at last. 
“What? The one Diego mentioned?” Kel asked.
“Probably. So… she’s on the move again,” Oldman muttered.
The others looked at him with curiosity. He sighed. “I’ll explain everything. But it’s getting dark, so let’s get back to the bandits’ hideout.”
The group agreed and set off, their footsteps fading into the night.

After settling inside the wooden house at the bandit camp, Kel couldn’t hold his curiosity any longer.
“So… who is she?” he asked.
Oldman removed his favorite brown gambler hat, resting it on his knee. “She’s an Ascendant Wildborn. The one who gave me this.” His fingers brushed the old scar on his forehead.
“It happened during my last job before I retired as a mercenary,” he began. “A noble who was well-versed in Primeflora posted a job in the Falcon Order. He wanted to study a newly discovered Primeflora in a lesser village deep within the Unknown. The pay was good, so many signed up. We had over twenty Echo Users, including an Echo Master.”
He paused, glancing at Tata, who sat quietly on the table as if remembering the journey.
“The village lay on the far side of Fallen Cliff, at the ground below its sheer drop, east of here. Almost beyond the Doom Forest. It was the farthest I’d ever gone into the Unknown. But with such a strong group, made up of B-rank mercenaries and two A-ranks, I was confident. My role was as a scout, with Tata’s help.”
He leaned back, voice turning heavier.
“We faced plenty of Wildborn along the way, but nothing we couldn’t handle. After a few weeks of traveling, we reached the village and stayed there for several weeks while the noble studied the Primeflora. It was a unique plant with only a single leaf on top, but I wasn’t interested in studying it—so I didn’t pay much attention. Instead, I roamed the village and got to know some of the people there.
One of them was Marcus, the village chief. He was a funny man, yet strong and dependable. We sparred every day. He had a charming three-year-old daughter, whose mother had died giving birth to her. I became close to both of them, even staying at their home while waiting for the noble to finish his research. Those were relaxing days. The village only faced small hordes of Wildborn, and with Marcus using his Echo of Armor and leading the villagers with steady confidence, we mercenaries didn’t even need to lift a finger to help defend the place.” Oldman chuckled .
“Then… one night, everything changed.” Unconsciously, his hand brushed over the scar.
“A horde of Weblings attacked the village. We were outnumbered, and their sticky webs made every step a struggle. It was a hard-fought battle, but we were holding our ground until three massive Crawlers appeared. Their hairy bodies were a mix of blue and black, and each creature loomed with twenty long, powerful legs. They moved slowly, but their sheer bulk made them deadly. Each step could crush a house. Unlike the Weblings, they didn’t spin webs, yet their presence alone brought terror. With Marcus and the Echo Master leading the charge, we managed to bring down two of them after a brutal fight. For a moment… we thought we were winning.”
Oldman’s eyes darkened. “And then… we saw her. Jorgumo. She was pure black—her lower body that of a massive spider, her upper half eerily human, twisted into something unnatural. The Echo Master fought her, but it was useless—she was too strong. Thick, sticky webs shot from her hands in rapid bursts and her legs were as sharp as swords. We rushed to help, but one of those legs slashed my head.”
Tata leapt into his arms as if sharing his fear.
“In the end, she killed the Echo Master… then laughed, a cold, chilling sound that froze us all. She declared herself Jorgumo, the Queen of Spiders. That was the beginning. Mercenaries and Villagers fell one by one. Chaos. Screams. It was a massacre. Tata and I ran. There was no hope left.” His grip tightened around his hat. 
“On the way, we found Marcus, still clutching his daughter. But the horde was closing in, surrounding the village. He… gave his life so we could escape. I carried the girl in my arms and climbed Fallen Cliff, step by step, with the sounds of the horde echoing below.
“That child was Althea?” Gian asked quietly.
“Yes. From that day on, I adopted her as my own. The horde didn’t follow us beyond the cliff. I took her to another lesser village, and later I met an expedition team from Tillara City. The previous Highlord, the father of Lady Rose, was kind enough to let us travel with them back to the Lanthea Kingdom. That’s where I finally retired… for good. I devoted my life to her. I promised to always protect her… and yet.” His voice broke, the weight of guilt cutting his words short as his grip tightened around his hat.
Gian placed a hand on his shoulder.
El and Anni exchanged a confused glance, until Oldman spoke again. This time, his voice was thick with hatred. 
“She died last year… killed by the men we’re hunting.”
“I’m sorry to hear that,” El said softly to Oldman.
“Thank you,” he replied quietly.
“So… these men you’re hunting—do they have something to do with Diego?” Anni asked, her gaze sharp. 
“Yes,” Oldman said. “We’re looking for a group of masked men in violet robes. We got word that a squad of soldiers was attacked, and the sole survivor swore the attackers wore violet.”
Kel gave a small snort, waving the purple robe they’d recovered. “Except, like I said—purple isn’t violet. I’ll teach Private Joey about colors when we see him again.”
Anni glanced at El. “Maybe—”
“No. Don’t,” El cut her off firmly.
“What are you talking about?” Gian asked, sensing there was more between the two women than they’d let on since meeting them yesterday.
“Let me join you,” Anni said suddenly.
“I will not allow it,” El replied, her tone hard.
“But I want to—”
“You can’t. You’re not strong enough.”
Anni’s head dipped. “When will I ever be strong enough for you?” she asked, her voice trembling with frustration. “I’ve trained my whole life for this, but I can’t leave this place.” Then her eyes, blue and burning, locked on El. “I want to avenge our clan.”
El sighed, glancing at Oldman, Gian, and Kel as if weighing her next words.
Gian’s eyes widened as realization hit him. “You’re after the violet-robed men too, aren’t you?”
Anni’s gaze met his, unwavering. “Yes. And I’ll kill them all.”
