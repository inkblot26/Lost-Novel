Chapter 43: Bonded Wildborn
Kel supported Gian as Tata led the way through the quiet forest, the dim light barely reaching the forest floor. When they finally stepped out of the treeline into the clearing before the mansion, Oldman was waiting by the open main door. The bodies that had once littered the yard were now gone, the ground cleared.
He rushed forward the moment he saw them.
“Get him inside,” Oldman said urgently, slipping under Gian’s other arm. Once they were in, he gestured to the couch. “Lay down, kid. I’ll heal you.”
Oldman knelt beside Gian, closed his eyes, and activated his Echo. Tiny yellow-green firefly-like lights appeared around his hands and slowly floated toward Gian’s wounds. They sank into his abdomen, sealing the gashes little by little. The pain eased with every flicker of light.
After a while, the wounds had fully closed, though the bloodstains still marked Gian’s clothes. Oldman sat back, breathing heavily.
“I will only heal major injuries,” he said between breaths. “This Echo takes a toll on me and the patient.”
Kel glanced over, curious. “Why’s that?”
Oldman didn’t look up. “Even if two people share the same Echo, how they use it can differ, based on their understanding and practice. My version speeds up the body’s natural recovery. It drains energy fast. That’s why you’ll feel exhausted afterward.” 
“Thanks…” Gian muttered, slowly sitting up. 
“Lay back down if you can’t stay up. Don’t push it,” Oldman warned, then turned to Kel. “Now, come here. Let me take care of that bite.”
Kel sat beside him, and again, the yellow-green fireflies appeared. When the healing was done and only minor injuries remained, Oldman stood with a groan.
“So? How did it go?” Kel asked.
“I haven’t asked him anything yet,” Oldman said, his expression dark. “I just beat him to the edge of consciousness… then healed him so he wouldn’t die.”
Kel and Gian exchanged a glance.
“He’s unconscious now. We’ll question him together once you’re ready. Vince is guarding him in that room.” Oldman nodded toward a closed door down the hall. 
“Michael’s upstairs with the girls, keeping them safe in one of the rooms on the second floor. We’ve sent a bird for updates. Now we wait.”
“It’s good that the girls are safe,” Gian said with relief.
“Yeah,” Oldman replied, just as Tata jumped up to perch on his shoulder.
“Is that really a feral type?” Kel asked, eyeing the little driftmouse as it puffed out its chest proudly.
“Yeah. Why?”
“Well, it’s smart. Helped us out on its own,” Kel noted.
Oldman smirked. “It is a feral type, but it’s been with me for over two decades. That kind of bond changes things. I also ordered it to assist you after we split up, Kel.”
Seeing their puzzled expressions, Oldman chuckled. “Feral Wildborn aren’t stupid. They usually act on instinct and survival, yes, but once they’re bonded through a Rootseal or Echo, they begin to develop intelligence and personality. The connection with their master shapes them over time.” 
“So the longer a Wildborn is tamed, the stronger and smarter it becomes?” Gian asked.
“Exactly,” Oldman said with a nod. “But don’t assume unbonded ferals are mindless. Some might’ve been bonded in the past, then abandoned, or they broke the connection themselves. You never really know what you’re dealing with.” 
“Then… what’s the difference between them and Ascendants?” Kel asked.
Oldman’s face turned grim. 
“Ascendants… they’re something else. They don’t just fight with their bodies. They have ability no feral can develop, no matter how long they’re bonded. Superspeed, fire-breathing, illusions—and some of them can talk.”
Both Gian and Kel flinched at the memory of the Blinkclaws they’d faced, creatures too fast for any normal Wildborn. 
“And those that can talk…” Oldman added grimly, running his fingers across the scar on his forehead. “Those are the dangerous ones.”
He looked at them seriously.
“Thankfully, the truly powerful Ascendants are mostly confined to the Unknown. But sometimes, weaker ones appear in the kingdoms, and if you’re unlucky, you’ll run into one.”

After resting for a while, the trio made their way to the room where Ronald lay, his hands tied behind his back. He was asleep, without a single injury, a sharp contrast to his torn, bloodstained clothes.
Gian stared at him. He felt no pity, only disgust for people like him.
“Thanks, Vince,” Oldman said, nodding to the fighter now free of injuries. “Can you leave us for a moment?”
“I’ll stand guard at the main door, just in case,” Vince replied, stepping out.
Oldman turned his gaze back to Ronald on the floor. “I’ll wake him up,” Kel said, stepping forward.
But just then, Vince rushed back in, urgency written all over his face.
“I spotted a Wildborn flying this way. I don’t recognize it, and it’s not one of ours.”
The four exchanged glances. Without another word, they left the room. Oldman paused only to lock the door behind him.
“Kel, go upstairs and inform Michael,” Oldman ordered. Kel nodded and dashed up the stairs.
“We’ll stay inside the mansion,” Oldman continued. “Vince and I will hide near the main door. Gian, get your bow ready.”
As the three of them moved to their positions, Kel reached the second floor. He opened the door where Michael stood watch. Inside, the girls were finally calm, resting.
“There’s a problem,” Kel said. “An avian Wildborn is approaching. We don’t know its intent, but it’s definitely not ours. We’ll need your arrows.”
Fear spread across the girls’ faces.
Michael gave them a reassuring look. “You all stay here and lock the door. Whatever happens, stay quiet. Understand?”
One braver girl nodded. “Okay, Sir Michael. But… please be careful.”
“Don’t worry, it won’t take long. Just stay hidden,” Michael said, placing a finger to his lips before gently closing the door.
"The girl locked it behind him. Then, one by one, they huddled into closets or slid under beds, holding their breath and hoping they would be safe as the footsteps faded away."
In the upstairs hallway, a few steps from the girls’ closed door, Kel and Michael crouched low behind a corner where the corridor turned toward the stairs. Michael quietly summoned a bow, fingers already nocking an arrow. Beside him, Kel drew the Crimson Blood Axe from his side, the weapon glinting faintly in the dim light.
Downstairs, just inside the shattered front windows near the main entrance, Oldman and Vince waited with a sword in each hand—tense, silent, ready. A few steps away, Gian remained hidden inside one of the rooms, bow drawn, an arrow steady against the string, his breathing slow and focused.
The mansion had fallen into stillness, dust hanging faintly in the air from the earlier fighting.

Clutching the stump of his missing right hand, Richard gritted his teeth through the pain as he sat atop the Sunquill, gliding above the treeline at the edge of Ronald’s estate. 
“Damn it… I’ll kill them all and take back my ring,” he muttered. “I’ll get treated at Ronald’s place, then we’ll find General Douglas. With his help, I’ll reclaim my position.”
Blood dripped steadily from his arm, but his mind raced.
“We just need to act fast, before word reaches the king. I’m still Highlord by name… that’s enough for now.”
But then, something below caught his eye. A black carriage stood abandoned, one door hanging open. No guards. No movement.
Richard’s eyes narrowed. Something’s not right.
Where the hell is Ronald’s security?
The Sunquill circled the mansion once.
No matter. I’ve got a Wildborn with me, and I need to stop this bleeding now. Maybe Ronald’s just off fooling around again.
With a growl of frustration, Richard gave the order.
“Break through the main window.”
The Sunquill folded its wings and dove, crashing through the wide front window above the entrance hall, scattering glass across the tiled floor.
It was too quiet.
Richard's eyes swept the room. The scorched floor. The scattered rubble. The faint stench of burned wood and blood.
No… something's wrong. I need to get out—
Two arrows whistled from the second-floor railing overlooking the entrance hall, striking the Sunquill’s left wing. Blood burst into the air, splashing across its golden feathers as the creature shrieked and crashed to the floor, sending Richard tumbling off its back.
“Attack!” Oldman roared, rushing forward with Vince at his side.
Gian released his own arrow, striking the Willborn’s right flank. The Sunquill staggered, screeching in pain.
“Aghh!” Richard cried, flinging his arm forward. Multiple flaming birds burst from his palm, streaking toward Vince. The fiery Echo engulfed him mid-charge, flames devouring his body. As he screamed, the Sunquil struck, its curved beak piercing his skull and ending his life.
Oldman swung his sword, but the Sunquill’s tail whipped violently, slamming him into a wall with a loud crack.
Michael rose on the second floor and loosed an arrow toward Richard. 
The creature didn’t pause. There was no love in its bond, no trust behind its loyalty. It obeyed because it was chained, fear tightening its will in Richard’s grasp, the command pressing like iron.
The Wildborn lunged, its massive frame blocking the shot to protect its master. 
“Protect me no matter what!” Richard growled, forcing the command through his bond with the creature. “Kill that archer.”
As the Sunquill charged, Richard yanked a brown parchment from his back, a Whispering Scroll.
Oldman’s eyes widened. Earth-type.
A massive stone spike erupted from the tiled floor, cracking the stone as it surged upward. He dove to avoid a fatal hit, but it speared clean through his leg. A howl of pain tore from his throat as he was pinned to the ground.
You’ve forced me to use all my Whispering Scrolls, Richard seethed inwardly. I’ll kill you all for this. One day… I swear it, Rose—I’ll kill you.
From the doorway of the side room across the entrance hall, Gian aimed and fired at Richard. The arrow flew straight, but Richard raised his hand and activated his Echo of Rot. The arrow corroded on impact, crumbling into flakes before hitting the floor.
From the corner, Tata spotted its injured master and rushed toward him, ignoring the danger, driven by its own will.
High near the damaged ceiling, the bleeding Sunquill let out a shriek and beat its wings once before diving toward Michael on the upper level. The impact sent them both crashing across the hall in a heap of feathers and shattered wood.
A short distance away, Kel saw it all unfold. Vince dead, Oldman impaled, Michael struck down. Rage and desperation surged in him. 
“No more,” Kel growled.
He slashed a deep cut into his own palm, ignoring the pain. A lot of blood gushed out, but he shaped it, condensing it into a sharp, crescent blade.
With a cry, he hurled it toward the Sunquill. The blood-blade sliced through the air, severing part of the creature’s left wing. The Wildborn reeled back, screeching, barely managing to block the rest of the attack with its other wing.
“Damn… it blocked just in time,” Kel muttered, swaying. His vision blurred. His knees buckled. Too much blood.
Gritting his teeth, Kel tightened his grip on his axe and forced himself forward toward the monster.
