Chapter 17: Echoes we keep
The fourth day of travel brought yet another round of unexpected trial. Only yesterday the caravan had fought off wildborns, and now nature itself turned against them. Clouds had swallowed the sun without warning, turning the summer afternoon into a dim, churning gray. Thunder rolled across the hills, and rain fell in sheets so thick the road ahead blurred into a shifting wall of water.
Wind howled through the trees, bending branches low and ripping leaves free. Mud sucked at boots and wheels alike, each step a struggle against the earth itself. Cloaks clung heavy and cold to soaked bodies, and armor felt twice its weight beneath the relentless downpour.
“Sir, David found a small underground cave. We can use it to wait out the storm,” Margaret reported to Torren, her cloak plastered to her skin by the rain.
“That’s good. We need to get out of this downpour,” Torren replied, water streaming from his armor.
“But we’ll need to detour a bit, sir,” Thomas added, clutching his red traveling hat to keep it from flying off in the wind.
“It’s better than staying exposed. I’ll inform Lord Henry. You pass the word to everyone else,” Torren said as he strode toward the carriage.
“Everyone, hang in there! We found a place to shelter, but we’ll be taking a short detour!” Thomas shouted as he moved down the line of wagons.
The wagon wheels had sunk deep into the softened road, swallowed nearly to their spokes. Every push sent mud splashing up their legs, the ground squelching and shifting treacherously beneath them.
Inside one of the wagons, Kel groaned. “First we fight drooling, ugly beasts, and now this stupid weather?”
“Stop complaining and get out here, you lazy punk! The wagon’s stuck in the mud—help us push!” Baldwin shouted, straining against a wheel half-sunken in the muck.
“Why do we have to be so unlucky?” Kel muttered.
“No use complaining. Just get out. We can’t move this thing,” Gian said, bracing his shoulder against the other side of the wagon.
“Yeah, yeah, I know. You guys are hopeless without the great Kel,” Kel puffed his chest as he climbed down, striking a mock-heroic pose.
At the front, Edmund tugged at the reins of the restless horse. “Push harder, we need to move fast!”
“Aye aye, sir! Fret not, for I am here!” Kel replied, flexing his arm before joining Gian and Baldwin.
“Why are we stuck with this guy?” Baldwin muttered, rolling his eyes.

After the grueling detour, the cave entrance emerged through the curtain of rain — a dark slit in a rocky hillside, half-hidden by hanging vines and runoff streaming down the stone face. Water cascaded around its mouth like silver threads, but inside lay shadow and stillness.
“Sir, it’s a small grotto, but no sign of wildborn so far. Just a few bats,” mercenary David reported.
“It’ll do. We just need to wait out the storm,” Torren said, stepping inside to inspect it.
The grotto was shallow but wide enough to fit the caravan’s essentials. The air inside was cool and damp, thick with the mineral scent of wet stone. Droplets fell steadily from stalactites above, tapping softly against rock and wood.
Outside, thunder continued to rumble, distant but unrelenting.
“Why are we waiting? It’s right there already,” Kel complained.
“Can’t you stop talking for one second?” Baldwin snapped, irritation clear in his voice.
“Okay, okay, Mr. Baldwin relax. Keep it up and you’ll earn yourself a new name—Mr. Hairloss, Get it? ” Kel teased, ducking quickly as Baldwin swung at his head.
“Hey, cut it out, you two,” Edmund said, glancing up at the stormy sky.
“Sir Torren’s just making sure it’s safe for Lord Henry,” Michael explained as he joined their wagon for shelter, stretching his arms.
“Did you hear anything with that new Echo of yours, sir?” Kel asked, turning curiously to Edmund.
“I wouldn’t use my newly gained ability to eavesdrop on my superior, Kel,” Edmund replied dryly.
“Too bad. If I got that Echo, I’d use it all the time,” Kel said with a grin.
“Not everyone’s a gossip like you,” Baldwin muttered.
“He’s coming back,” Gian said, cutting off the quarrel as he nodded toward Torren, who was walking back to the carriage.
“Alright, everyone, let’s unload our things and supplies. We can’t risk leaving them out here,” Thomas said, sticking his head into the wagon.
“Can’t we just leave them?” Kel groaned.
“We don’t know how long this storm will last,” Michael replied as he started unloading.
“You heard him. Let’s go,” Baldwin said, stepping out of the wagon.
Through the downpour, from the corner of his eye, Kel spotted her—the Lady of Rosendale. Miss Clara, with her slender frame and gray hair tied in a loose braid, stepped gracefully out of the carriage. The storm seemed to soften around her, umbrella tilted carefully overhead as Margaret shielded both her and Cedric. Raindrops slid from the fabric’s edge in glittering streams.
“Don’t run, Ced. You might fall,” Clara said gently, holding her brother back.
“But it’s raining, Sister! We need to play!” Cedric said playfully.
“Maybe another time, Ced. It’s pouring, and you’re getting all dirty,” Clara smiled warmly.
“Gian… punch me,” Kel muttered, frozen in place in the middle of the rain.
“Huh? Why are you stopping? We need to unload fast, Kel,” Gian said, lugging a barrel.
“I think I’m in love,” Kel whispered, eyes wide.
“The only kiss you’re getting is my fist, kid. Move it,” Baldwin said, raising his hand threateningly.
“She’s an angel. I was meant to protect her,” Kel continued, completely entranced.
“Just hit him and let’s get this over with,” Gian said, rolling his eyes at Baldwin.

As darkness deepened, the small campfires sputtered and hissed, smoke rising weakly before being swallowed by the damp air. Wet wood cracked half-heartedly, flames shrinking into dull embers that struggled against the creeping cold.
The storm’s steady drumming outside echoed faintly within the grotto, blending with the soft patter of water dripping from the cave ceiling.
“What do you think I should tell her?” Kel asked quietly.
“You can tell her you’re a prince. A prince of idiots,” Baldwin chuckled softly.
“How long do you think this storm will last?” Edmund suddenly asked.
“It depends,” Thomas said, sitting down beside them. “But I don’t think it’ll be long. What worries me is how dark it’s getting, and our flames are starting to die out.” 
“Why?” Kel asked.
“We don’t have much wood left,” Thomas answered.
“And everything we do have is soaked anyway,” Edmund added.
With a heavy sigh, Baldwin stood up. “Guess I don’t have much choice.”
A soft golden radiance bloomed into existence above them. It wasn’t harsh like torchlight but warm and steady, casting long, gentle shadows against the cave walls. The wet stone shimmered under its glow, and for the first time since the storm began, the gloom retreated.
Even the constant dripping seemed quieter beneath the light.
“Whaaat? You could summon light this whole time?” Kel shouted, eyes wide.
Everyone turned to look at Baldwin.
“I’m sorry I kept it from you, sir. Truth is, I hate my Echo,” Baldwin admitted to Thomas.
“That’s alright. I understand why you didn’t use it at night. The light’s strong, and it covers a wide area. Out in the open, it might even attract wildborn,” Torren said, approaching them. 
“Lord Henry wants to know how long you can keep the Echo of Glimmer going,” he added.
“An hour at most, sir. After that, I’ll need rest,” Baldwin replied.
“That’s fine. We’ll eat while we have the light.” Torren nodded, then walked back toward Lord Henry and his family to report.
“Wow…” Cedric whispered, stepping closer and reaching toward the glowing orb.
“You don’t like it?” Kel asked Baldwin.
“I don’t like it. It’s useless in a fight,” Baldwin said with a shrug. “And you heard Sir Torren—it’s dangerous to use out in the field at night.”
“Then why’d you learn it if you don’t like it?” Gian asked.
“It’s the only Verdant Echo I’ve ever gotten, from a Wildborn I killed during one of my travels. It’s my treasure… that, and this old plain buckler entrusted to me by a dear friend.” He placed the buckler on his lap. 
“How can you hate an Echo?” Cedric asked curiously.
“It’s mostly useless, kid. Except for rare moments like this. Damn this storm,” Baldwin muttered.
“Anyway,” he continued, turning to Cedric, “did you know the world used to be different? A long, long time ago?”
“How different?” Cedric asked as he tried to light a candle, comparing its flickering glow to the floating orb above.
“A much kinder world. No wildborn. No unstable weather like this,” Baldwin replied. “But also, no Verdant Echoes.”
“And where’d you hear that, old man?” Kel said, clearly skeptical.
As Baldwin continued the story he had heard during his travels, Gian was lost in thought, having heard something like this for the first time.
A world without Echoes, huh…? he wondered, watching Cedric struggle to keep his tiny flame alive.
Beyond the cave mouth, rain continued to pour into the darkened forest, the world outside reduced to shadow and storm. Inside, the glow of Glimmer flickered softly across thoughtful faces.
