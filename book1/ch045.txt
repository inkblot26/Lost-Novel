Chapter 45: The Room Below
The following night, deep beneath Lord Henry’s mansion, in a stone-walled underground chamber lit by a few iron-bracketed torches, Lord Ronald sat fully healed. His chubby face showed no trace of the torture he’d endured just hours earlier. Only his torn clothes remained, leaving him in nothing but a pair of shorts. 
“Please, stop! I’ll do anything. Whatever you want. I’ll be a dog, lick your shoes, just—please—don’t hurt me anymore!” Ronald sobbed, trembling in the chair, eyes wide with fear.
In front of him stood Oldman, his face shadowed beneath the brim of his leather hat. Tata sat quietly on his shoulder. Behind him, the thick wooden door reinforced with iron bands creaked open, spilling brighter hallway light across the stone floor as Gian and Kel stepped inside.
“I think it’s time we talk,” Oldman said calmly.
“Yes! Yes, I’ll talk! Whatever you want, I’ll tell you everything. Just please, stop hurting me!” Ronald blurted out between sniffles. 
“I won’t hurt you,” Gian replied evenly. “Just tell us what happened last year at your old mansion.”
“Wh-what do you mean?” Ronald stammered.
Without warning, Kel smacked him across the face with the flat of his axe.
“Wait! Wait! I’ll talk! Just… just be specific!” Ronald cried, a trickle of blood running down his brow.
Gian glanced at Kel.
“My bad,” Kel said with a shrug. “His face just annoys me.”
Gian continued, “Last year, twelve-year-old girls were taken from the village of Dimuara.”
Panic lit Ronald’s eyes. “I-I had nothing to do with that! I don’t even know that village!”
“It was southeast of Tillara City,” Kel said, stepping closer. “Your soldiers kidnapped those girls.”
Recognition dawned on Ronald’s face. Sweat poured down as he stammered, “W-wait… that wasn’t me! It was my assistant—Jonas! He handled all of that. I let him take care of everything…”
“What happened that night?” Gian asked, his voice hard.
“You have to believe me. I didn’t know those girls meant anything to you! Jonas chose the places, the girls… he picked everything,” Ronald said, then suddenly froze, realizing he had said too much.
Kel smacked him again with the flat of the axe.
“I’m sorry! I’m sorry!” Ronald cried as more blood dripped from his nose.
“Then tell us what happened that night,” Gian repeated.
“Okay, okay, just promise you won’t hurt me again! I swear I didn’t lay a hand on those girls!” Ronald pleaded. 
“I promise,” Kel said, letting the axe rest at his side. “As long as you tell us everything.”
Seeing the weapon lowered, Ronald swallowed hard.

It was a quiet evening in the Noble District, inside one of the larger mansions owned by the Highlord’s younger brother, its tall windows overlooking manicured gardens. Lord Ronald laughed heartily, swirling a glass of red wine in his hand. Across the polished dining table sat his assistant Jonas, a tall, scholarly man with thin glasses and a calculating gaze. 
“I must say, Jonas, I’m lucky to have you as my assistant,” Ronald said with a grin, chuckling to himself. “My big brother is pleased with the income we’ve been pulling from the slave trade you manage.”
“It is my honor, Your Majesty,” Jonas replied with a respectful bow.
“‘Your Majesty’—hah! I like the sound of that, Jonas!” Ronald burst into laughter again.
“But I know my limits,” he added, his voice lowering slightly. “I have no intention of rebelling against the king and especially my brother. He’s not someone we can afford to cross.”
“Apologies, my lord. I only meant that I believe you’re fit to rule this kingdom,” Jonas said, bowing again.
“Never mind that. What matters is that we enjoy the position we have now.” Ronald waved a dismissive hand and took another sip of wine. “By the way, how’s that forbidden hobby of yours?”
“Don’t worry, my lord,” Jonas replied calmly. “I don’t leave a trace. I conduct my research in secret.”
“I’m not worried,” Ronald said with a smirk. “As long as you do your job properly, I’ll let you enjoy your little hobby of digging through old bones and books. I’m just curious.”
“So far,” Jonas said, adjusting his glasses, “I’ve uncovered scattered texts speaking of seven powerful families who once ruled, often clashing with one another.”
“Why?” Ronald asked.
“I don’t have solid evidence yet, but it seems likely it was due to territorial disputes.”
“Who are they?” Ronald asked, now slightly intrigued.
“The text was incomplete. All I know is that they were the pillars of humanity at that time.”
“What I find more fascinating, though,” Jonas continued, “is a particular religion I came across. Its followers called themselves the Verdant Keepers.”
“Religion?” Ronald scoffed. “Aren’t those just for lunatics who bow to statues and chant meaningless rituals?” 
“Yes, my lord. As you know, religion has long been banned across the kingdoms. Only the mad still practice it in secret,” Jonas answered.
“So what’s so special about these ‘Keepers’?” Ronald asked, raising an eyebrow.
“The first I heard of them was during my time as a student,” Jonas said. “My mentor, also a student of forbidden history, claimed to have stumbled upon traces of their beliefs. What’s more, I recently found a letter mentioning them during one of our raids. It was hidden in an abandoned lesser village near the Wildborn territories.” 
“You’re telling me this cult, or whatever they are, still exists?” Ronald leaned forward now, genuinely curious.
“Yes, my lord. That’s why they interest me. Religion has been outlawed since the Last Pact. Anyone who still believes in one is either crazy or suicidal. But something as ancient as this, surviving in secret for so long… it’s incredible.”
“Well,” Ronald snorted, “good luck to them. If the kingdoms ever catch wind of them, they’ll be crushed for good.”
“Yes, milord,” Jonas said with a faint smile.
“Anyway,” Ronald said, setting down his wineglass and squinting with a pleased grin, “where’s that surprise batch you mentioned?”
“They’re on the way, milord. I’ll have them prepared in the usual room. If you’ll excuse me.”
“Sure, sure. I’ll take a bath first,” Ronald said, rising to his feet and waddling off down the corridor toward the private bath chamber at the rear of the mansion.

“That was the last time I talk to Jonas,” Ronald said, his voice shaking as he recalled the memory.
“I had just finished bathing when I heard shouting and chaos in the mansion. At first, I wanted to go out and see what was happening… but I was scared. So I locked myself in the bathroom, thinking my guards would handle it. I waited for someone to report back.”
He paused, trembling. “Everything went quiet. Only my heartbeat filled the air. It kept pounding, louder and louder, until I couldn’t take it. I cracked the bathroom door open and peered into the corridor. The lanterns along the walls were still burning. I stepped into the hallway… and there they were. My guards lay scattered across the carpeted floor. Every last one. Dead.”
Ronald’s hands shook against the ropes binding him to the chair.
“I crept toward the room where Jonas should’ve been. The girls were being kept in the adjoining chamber beside it. But when I arrived, it was chaos—bookshelves overturned, glass shattered across the floor. Jonas was lying in front of the fireplace, soaked in blood.” 
“You didn’t see who was responsible?” Kel asked, watching him closely.
“No,” Ronald whispered. “I don’t know who could’ve done it. Jonas wasn’t just a scholar, he was a skilled fighter. That’s why I chose him. Even my brother wanted him as a retainer. But Jonas preferred working under me because I gave him time for his hobby.”
He swallowed hard, then continued.
“After that… I panicked. I ran through the front hall, past the open doors and silent guards, and fled the mansion toward my brother’s estate. He sent soldiers to investigate, but they didn’t find anyone. Nothing.”
Ronald looked up, eyes wide and pleading. “That’s all that happened that night. I swear I didn’t hurt anyone. Please, believe me.”
For a long moment, only the faint crackle of torch fire filled the underground chamber.
Then Oldman finally spoke, and something about his calm tone was more terrifying than any shout. “I believe you.”
Gian and Kel turned to look at Oldman, then at each other. Without a word, they moved toward the door.
As Gian pulled open the heavy door, torchlight from the hallway spilled in once more. He paused and said, “We’ll leave him to you.” Then the door shut with a dull thud, sealing the chamber again. 
“Wait… what now?” Ronald stammered. “Can you at least untie me? I promise I won’t run away. Please!”
Oldman stepped forward, drawing a dagger from his side. Tata leapt off his shoulder and padded silently to the edge of the room.
“What are you doing?” Ronald’s voice cracked. “W-Wait! You promised—y-you said you wouldn’t hurt me!”
“No,” Oldman said quietly, stepping closer. “They promised not to hurt you.”
He now stood directly in front of Ronald, eyes burning.
“I made no such promise.”
“Please—!” Ronald sobbed, but his words were cut short as Oldman raised the blade.
A moment later, screams echoed against the stone walls of the underground chamber, fading into the thick silence below the mansion.
