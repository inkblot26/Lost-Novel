Chapter 13: Flicker of Fire
They kept to the left side of the hill, following a narrow path that curved along its base. After nearly an hour of steady travel, the trees thinned, and the road finally split in two. 
One path stretched north into the deep forest toward the city of Tillara. The other veered west, back toward the lands the caravan had come from.
The group paused beneath the canopy’s shade, taking advantage of the brief rest. Leaves rustled overhead, and the distant hum of insects filled the warm afternoon air.
“This is exciting!” Kel said, practically vibrating with energy as he scanned the crossroads.
“What are you grinning at like a fool, kid?” Baldwin, the old man with the stapled buckler, asked, noticing Kel’s enthusiasm.
“Well, it’s my first time leaving Tala. The world—and the ladies—better watch out!” Kel declared with a wide grin. 
“Idiot. Boys like you die young,” Baldwin muttered, shaking his head before turning to Gian. “You’re Gian, right?  Some advice from an old mercenary: stay close to the caravan. Never wander alone. And don’t let confidence outrun caution.” 
“I understand. Thank you,” Gian replied.
At the front of the formation, Torren stepped forward.
“Since we’re nearing the forest, I’ll assign positions.”
Birdsong drifted faintly from the treeline ahead.
“Eight at the front, including myself. We’ll serve as scouts and first response if anything comes out of the woods. Mercenaries David, Agnes, and Hugo—you’re with me. Use your Echoes to clear anything before it reaches the caravan.” 
He gestured toward three mercenaries. “You three Non-Echo users, stay alert and support wherever needed.” 
“Margaret,” he said, turning to the lone female guard, “keep using your Echo of Perception. Constant sweep. Report anything unusual immediately.”
“Don’t worry sir. I’ll immediately alert you if I sense a nearby lifeforms.” Margaret nodded.
Kel leaned slightly toward Gian. “Good thing we’ve got her.”
Gian didn’t respond, listening instead as the rest of the formation was assigned.
“Thomas and his unit will guard Lord Henry’s carriage,” Torren continued, pointing to two guards and Baldwin. “Hold your position at all times.”
“Michael,” he added, meeting the sharp-eyed archer, “if the front needs support, don’t hesitate to use your Echo of Weapon. We’ll rely on your range.”
“Yes, sir.”
“As for the supply wagons,” Torren said, “one is food storage. You three will handle it.” His gaze stopped on Kel, Gian, and a crew-cut guard. “Edmund, you’re in charge.”
Edmund nodded once. “Understood. I’ll stake my life on it.”
“Looks like we’re a team,” Kel said, nudging Gian.
Gian nodded in return.
“We’re in your hands, Sir Edmund,” Kel said lightly.
“I’ll drive. You two walk the sides,” Edmund instructed with a calm smile.
“Yes, sir,” they answered in unison.
Torren turned to the final pair of guards. “You two will guard the rear wagon. Watch for pursuit.”
“Move out,” he ordered.
The caravan began rolling again, wheels creaking as it entered the shadow of the forest.
“So all the Echo users are up front or guarding the carriage,” Kel muttered as they walked beside the wagon.
“Seems like it,” Gian said. 
“Alright then. Let’s talk at the next break,” Kel said, drifting to one side. 
“Stay sharp,” Gian replied, taking the opposite side.
The caravan disappeared deeper into the trees.

After some time, Margaret raised her hand. 
She rode atop a translucent wolf-like construct—a manifestation of David’s Echo of Pack. Its body shimmered faintly, as if woven from drifting smoke and thin glass, its outline hinting at muscle and bone. 
Torren guided his horse to her side.
“What is it?”
“Movement ahead,” she said. “Six or seven. Tall figures… slightly larger than men.” 
“Likely Bloomfangs,” Torren muttered. “Territorial around these parts.” 
He raised a hand, signaling the caravan to halt.
“Wildborns ahead,” he announced. “Our forward team will engage them. The rest of you, hold position and stay sharp.”
Turning to the carriage, he called, “Thomas. You’re in charge here.” 
Thomas grinned, gesturing toward the beast pulling the carriage. “No problem. With my amazing Echo of Wind and this supposedly weak Tremorhoof? Nothing’s getting through.” 
Torren allowed himself a faint smile before lifting his war hammer onto his shoulder.
“Move.” 
The eight members of the forward unit slipped into the forest between the trees.
Moments later, they returned.
Mercenary Agnes, her Echo of Beast manifesting as bestial claws that extended from her fingertips like hardened bone, dragged a Bloomfang carcass behind her. Its dark green fur was matted with blood. One wound—massive and crushing—could only have come from a war hammer.
“Well, that was quick,” Kel muttered, watching how casually Torren walked back, unbothered.
“Lord Henry, Torren and the others have returned,” Thomas called toward the carriage window.
The carriage door opened.
A small gray-haired boy burst out first, shouting with joy.
“At last! Freedom!” 
Lord Henry followed, adjusting his neatly styled hair. Beside him stepped the Lady of Rosendale, a graceful, kind-looking woman dressed in soft, earth-toned gowns.
“Cedric, stay close,” she warned gently, pulling him back.
“How did it go, Torren?” Lord Henry asked.
“It’s taken care of, my lord,” Torren replied, resting his heavy war hammer against the ground with a  heavy thud.
“The sun is setting. I think we’d best make camp for the night,” Lord Henry said, glancing up at the fading light.
“As you wish, my lord.”

As dusk settled, camp was raised beside the track. Fires crackled. Supplies were unloaded. A perimeter was formed. Margaret and David moved ahead quietly to scout the surrounding area.
Gian, Kel, and Baldwin worked near the firepit, waiting for mercenary Agnes and guard Thomas to return with roasted Bloomfang meat.
Kel scanned the camp, visibly disappointed. 
“Still haven’t seen the young Lady of Rosendale.”
“Would you shut up and help us here? It’s going to get dark soon,” Baldwin snapped.
“But what if she’s beautiful?” Kel said, striking a dramatic pose like a knight. “Maybe I’d be more motivated to escort this caravan if I knew what the noble lady looked like.” 
Suddenly, Cedric approached with a bright smile, full of energy.
“Hey! I can help with the campfire! I have an Echo of Fire!” he announced proudly, holding out his hand.
A small flame flickered at his fingertip — no larger than a candle. 
“Haha! What a mighty fire you’ve got there, young lord,” Baldwin chuckled.
Cedric puffed his chest proudly. 
“Keep practicing,” Baldwin said more seriously. “Power grows with discipline.” 
“I will! Someday I’ll help protect my father,” Cedric said. 
Kel muttered under his breath, “Must be nice being rich… I wonder how much a Verdant Echo costs.”
“Hey—when is your sister coming out?” he added.
Thwack.
Baldwin smacked him on the head.
Gian said nothing. His eyes lingered on the fragile flame. 
Elder Eldon’s voice echoed in his memory:
“Echoes grow with the user. A weak Echo of Fire may only light a candle—but in the hands of a master, it can ignite storms or let you swim through lava.”
The tiny fire flickered against the darkening forest.
