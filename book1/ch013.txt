Chapter 13: Flicker of Fire
They kept to the left side of the hill, following the narrow path that curved along its base. After nearly an hour of steady travel, the trees thinned and the road split in two.
One path stretched north into the deep forest toward the city of Tillara. The other veered west, back toward the lands the caravan had come from.
The group paused beneath the canopy’s shade, grateful for the brief rest. Leaves rustled overhead, and the distant hum of insects filled the warm afternoon air.
“This is exciting!” Kel said, practically vibrating with energy as he scanned the crossroads.
“What are you grinning at like a fool, kid?” Baldwin, the old man with the stapled buckler, asked, noticing Kel’s enthusiasm. 
“Well, this is my first time leaving Tala. World—and ladies—get ready for me!” Kel declared with a dazzling grin.
“Idiot. Boys like you die young,” Baldwin muttered flatly before turning to Gian. “Gian, was it? Some advice from an old mercenary: stay close to the caravan. Never wander off alone. And never let confidence outrun caution.”
“I understand. Thank you,” Gian replied.
At the front of the formation, Torren stepped forward.
“Since we’re nearing the forest, I’ll assign positions.”
Birdsong echoed faintly from the treeline ahead.
“Eight at the front, including myself. We’ll act as scouts and first defense against ambushes. Mercenaries David, Agnes, and Hugo — you’re with me. Your Echoes will eliminate threats before they reach the caravan.”
He gestured toward three others. “You as well.”
“Margaret,” he added, turning to the lone female guard, “we’ll rely on your Echo of Pulse. Sweep the area constantly.”
Kel leaned toward Gian. “Good thing we’ve got her. She can sense life nearby, right?”
Gian said nothing, listening carefully as the assignments continued.
“Thomas and his unit will guard Lord Henry’s carriage,” Torren continued, pointing to two guards and Baldwin.
“Stay sharp, Michael. If we need backup at the front, use your Echo of Bow,” he added, giving the guard with sharp, narrow eyes a steady look.
“Yes, sir,” Michael replied.
“As for the two wagons—one holds our food supplies. That one will be guarded by you three.” He pointed at Kel, Gian, and a guard with a disciplined crew cut. “Edmund, you’re in charge.”
Edmund nodded firmly. . “Don’t worry, sir . I’ll stake my life on it.”
“Looks like we’re a team,” Kel said, nudging Gian.
Gian nodded in return.
“We’re in your hands, Sir Edmund,” Kel added playfully.
“I’ll drive. You two walk alongside,” Edmund instructed with a calm smile.
“Yes, sir,” they answered in unison.
Torren turned to the last pair of guards. “You two will guard the rear wagon.”
"Move out," Torren announced as the caravan continued its journey.
"So all the Echo users are up front or guarding the carriage, huh?" Kel said as he walked toward their assigned wagon.
"I guess so," Gian replied.
"Alright, take care. Let’s talk again at the next break," Kel said, heading to one side of the wagon.
"Okay. Stay sharp," Gian said, then moved to the opposite side of the wagon.  
The caravan rolled forward once more, wheels creaking as they entered the forest’s shadow.

After some time, farther ahead, Margaret raised her hand.
She rode atop a wolf-shaped shadow — a manifestation of David’s Echo of Pack — its form flickering like black smoke given muscle and bone.
Torren guided his horse to her side.
“What is it?”
“Movement ahead,” she said. “Six or seven. They’re tall, slightly larger than men.”
“Likely Bloomfangs,” Torren muttered. “Territorial around these parts.”
He raised a hand, signaling for the caravan to halt as it approached.
“Wildborns ahead,” he announced. “Our forward team will engage them. The rest of you, hold position and stay sharp.”
Turning to the carriage, he called, “Thomas. You’re in charge here.” 
Thomas grinned, gesturing toward the beast pulling the carriage. “No problem. With my amazing Echo of Wind and this weak Tremorhoof? Nothing’s breaking through.”
Torren allowed himself a faint smile before lifting his war hammer onto his shoulder.
“Move.” 
The eight members of the forward unit vanished between the trees.

Moments later, they returned.
Mercenary Agnes, using her Echo of Claw, dragged a Bloomfang carcass behind her, its dark green fur matted with blood. One wound — massive and crushing — could only have come from a war hammer.
“Well, that was quick,” Kel muttered, watching how casually Torren walked back, unbothered.
“Lord Henry, Torren and the others have returned,” Thomas called toward the carriage window.
The carriage door opened.
A small gray-haired boy burst out first, shouting with joy.
“At last! Freedom!” 
Lord Henry followed, adjusting his neatly styled hair. Beside him stepped the Lady of Rosendale, a graceful, kind-looking woman dressed in soft, earth-toned gowns.
“Cedric, stay close,” she warned gently, drawing him back.
“How did it go, Torren?” Lord Henry asked.
“It’s taken care of, my lord,” Torren replied, resting his heavy war hammer against the ground with a  heavy thud.
“The sun is setting. I think we’d best make camp for the night,” Lord Henry said, glancing up at the fading light.
“As you wish, my lord.”

As dusk settled, camp was raised beside the track. Fires crackled. Supplies were unloaded. A perimeter was formed. Margaret and David advanced quietly ahead to scout.
Gian, Kel, and Baldwin worked near the firepit, waiting for mercenary Agnes and guard Thomas to return with roasted Bloomfang meat.
Kel scanned the camp, disappointed.
“Still haven’t seen the young Lady of Rosendale.”
“Would you just shut up and help us here? It’s gonna get dark soon,” Baldwin snapped irritably.
“But what if she’s beautiful?” Kel said, striking a dramatic pose like a knight. “Maybe I’d be more motivated to escort this caravan if I knew what the noble lady looked like.”
Suddenly, Cedric approached them with a big smile, full of excitement.
“Hey, hey! I can help with the campfire! I have an Echo of Fire!” he announced proudly, holding out his hand.
A small flame flickered at his fingertip — no larger than a candle. 
“Haha! What a mighty fire you’ve got there, young lord,” Baldwin chuckled.
Cedric puffed his chest proudly. 
“Practice it,” Baldwin advised. “Power grows with discipline.”
“I will! Then someday I’ll help protect my father,” Cedric said.
Kel muttered under his breath, “Must be nice being rich. Wonder how much a Verdant Echo costs”
“Hey, when is your sister going out?” he added—then got smacked in the head by Baldwin.
Gian stared at the fragile flame.
Elder Eldon’s voice echoed in his memory:
“Echoes grow with the user. A weak Echo of Fire may only light a candle—but in the hands of a master, it can ignite storms or let you swim through lava.”
The tiny fire flickered against the darkening forest.
