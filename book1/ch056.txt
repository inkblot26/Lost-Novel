Chapter 56: Where the Cliff Casts Its Shadow
The trio rested under the shade of the Baobao Tree for a few hours after refilling all their water jugs and storing them inside the Void Ring.
Kel, his hands now fully healed thanks to Oldman’s Echo, was busy cooking the meat from the Scorchbleak’s legs over a small fire set near the base of the tree. “Just a few more minutes, guys—I’m almost done,” he said confidently.
“Are you sure that’s going to taste good?” Oldman asked, sounding skeptical. “I’ve eaten roasted Scorchbleak meat before, and trust me, it’s not pleasant.”
Kel grinned. “Well, in my hands it’ll be good. Don’t forget, I’m a son of a chef. I’m the Great Kel, after all.” He flashed a playful smile as he carefully prepared the food.
Off to the side, Gian was standing with an oval Bao fruit in his hand. Its dark gray pod was tough, but he stabbed it open with a knife and drank the liquid inside. “It’s surprisingly delicious… like drinkable water with a sweet aftertaste,” he remarked, setting it down.
“Exactly why I’m boiling the meat with it,” Kel replied, stirring the pot with satisfaction.
Meanwhile, Oldman absentmindedly toyed with a direstone, watching it levitate for a moment before spinning slowly in the air. He glanced at the others. “Anyway, what do you two think? Should we keep heading north, or stay here for the night?”
Kel didn’t even look up. “Don’t worry about me. I’m still good to go. I only used one Blood Crescent, so I can still fight. I can probably get away with one more, but a third? No chance. I’m not risking severe blood loss.”
Gian sat cross-legged nearby and started sharpening the edge of his short sword. “Well, if you’re sure, then I suggest we move northward. Based on the map, we should reach the village in a day, maybe two at most.”
Oldman nodded, but his tone was serious. “I’m just reminding you, Kel—your technique is dangerous. You still haven’t mastered your Echo. We can’t have you collapsing out here in the Unknown. Even with my healing Echo, there’s no guarantee you’ll be okay.”
Gian paused for a moment, his mind flashing back to that night during training—when he and Oldman had found Kel lying in a pool of his own blood, unconscious, his heartbeat faint and dropping fast. He had suffered critical blood loss from overusing his Echo. He would’ve died if not for Oldman and Miss Mira, who worked together in time to save him. 
Kel let out a short laugh, breaking the heavy moment. “Relax. I won’t be using it four times in a day. I don’t have a death wish anymore.”
Finally, the meat finished cooking. The aroma wasn’t exactly pleasant, but it was warm food. The three of them sat together to eat, with Tata hopping down from the branches of the Baobao Tree to join them.

The next day, just before sunset, the trio finally saw it—Graylatch, the lesser village.
Nestled against the rocky base of the Fallen Cliff, the settlement bore the scars of a recent raid. Low stone walls still encircled the village, though one wooden gate hung shattered and splintered. Beside it, a watchtower had partially collapsed into a heap of charred timber, and nearby stood a strange, elevated stone platform, its purpose unclear.
Outside the shattered gate, a handful of children kicked a ragged ball through the dirt beside the broken gate. The moment they noticed the approaching strangers, their eyes widened. Panic replaced their laughter, and they scattered, running back toward the village while screaming for help.
Within moments, seven men—limping, bandaged, and clearly injured—emerged to block the broken gate. They carried whatever weapons they could muster: sickles, axes, and a couple of dull swords. Their faces were pale with exhaustion, but their eyes remained sharp, wary of Gian’s group. 
“So… they survived,” Gian murmured, taking in the torn-down homes and battered defenses.
Kel nudged him with an elbow and motioned toward the far end outside the village, where the cliff’s base was dotted with makeshift tombstones. A few graves were still freshly dug, the soil left open and unfinished.
Oldman’s expression hardened. “The Wildborn horde hit them hard… it’s a miracle they’re even standing. No wonder they’re on edge, they probably think we’re raiders.” He glanced sharply at Kel and Gian. “Keep your weapons sheathed. We don’t want to spook them.”
With Tata perched quietly on Oldman’s shoulder, the trio continued their slow, cautious approach toward the weary survivors.
As they reached the broken gate, an older woman stepped forward while several more injured men and women emerged behind her, weapons in hand, forming a tense barrier to block entry.
“Who are you, and what do you want?” the old woman asked, her sharp gaze scanning Gian and the others.
Oldman raised both hands calmly. “We mean no harm. We’re travelers from Tillara City, sent under Highlady Rose’s command to check on your village and ask a few questions.”
“We have a letter bearing her seal,” Gian added, reaching into his black leather armor.	
But at the sight of him moving, the villagers tensed. Weapons lifted. A man with a sickle took a step forward.
“W-wait, we really mean no harm!” Kel said quickly, raising his hands as well.
The old woman raised her hand, stopping the villagers. “Easy, everyone.” She stepped closer, then looked back at them. “I’m Granny Emma, the acting leader of this lesser village.”
A younger man holding a rake stepped forward, concern in his voice. “It’s dangerous, Granny Emma.”
She gave him a firm but gentle glance. “It’s alright. I’ve heard of Highlady Rose’s kindness, even out here in the Doom Forest. Let’s at least hear what they have to say.”
She approached slowly and took the letter from Gian. After carefully breaking the seal, she read its contents in silence.
A tense pause hung in the air.
At last, Granny Emma looked up, her expression softening. “I see… The Highlady truly is a rare kind of noble. Just like her father was. Unfortunately, the food relief never reached us, but the fact that she still thinks of us, even though we’re outside her territory… it means more than you know.”
Oldman nodded. “She said she’ll try to send supplies, once she confirms the village survived. But to be honest, before we left the outpost, there were urgent matters that might delay it.”
Emma gave a tired smile. “We’re grateful, regardless. Just knowing someone still remembers us... that’s already a comfort.”
She turned to her people. “They’re safe. Let them in.”
The villagers lowered their weapons hesitantly. Granny Emma gestured for Gian’s group to follow her. “Come, we’ll talk inside. I’ll answer all your questions once you’ve had a place to rest.”
As they walked into the village, she called back to reassure the others, “It’s alright. Nothing to worry about.”
Inside the small, battered village, Gian saw children peeking nervously through the shattered windows of their torn-down homes. Broken cart wheels lay abandoned in the streets, scattered among splintered wood and rubble. Even the village well, their lifeline for water, was destroyed. Everything felt hollow and ruined.
Granny Emma led them to her modest home. As they stepped inside, a young boy holding a small wooden sword stood in the corner, glaring at Gian with fierce eyes.
“You already took everything! Leave us alone!” the boy shouted, charging forward with his toy sword raised high. “Get out of this village!”
“Lapin, stop that!” Granny Emma quickly moved to intercept him. She caught him gently by the shoulders. “They’re not bandits—they’re our guests.”
The boy froze, still frowning but lowering his sword.
Emma turned to the trio and gave a small bow. “Please forgive him… We don’t have much left, not even proper food. He’s just scared.”
She motioned for them to sit at a worn wooden table set in the center of the small room. “Lapin, bring them some water.”
“But Granny… we don’t have much water left,” the boy said quietly, glancing warily at Gian and the others.
Oldman let out a soft chuckle. “Don’t worry, boy. We won’t take your water.” He looked over at Gian. “We still have some food left from yesterday, don’t we? Bring it out.”
Tata hopped onto the table, sniffing curiously as Gian nodded. He summoned the small pot of leftover food Kel had cooked the day before.
Kel scratched his ear, a little embarrassed. “Well… there’s not much. Just a little left. But I hope you’ll like it.”
Lapin’s eyes widened, a little drool forming at the corner of his mouth as he stared at the food, too hungry to even question how it had suddenly appeared.
“Thank you,” Granny Emma said, her voice soft with relief. “We’ll gladly eat it. After the Wildborn horde attacked… and then the bandits came… we’ve been left with almost nothing.” She bowed her head deeply in gratitude, then gestured for Lapin to take the food.
The three travelers exchanged shocked looks.
“What? You were also raided by bandits?” Kel asked in disbelief.
Emma nodded grimly. “Yes… let me tell you what happened to our village.”

The sun had just risen, but the massive Fallen Cliff still cast its long shadow over the humble village below. This was normal life here—days often began in cool shade before the light crept in.
The villagers went about their routines. Some drew water from the well, others patrolled the low stone walls. A few gathered children to teach them what little knowledge they could pass down. Farmers prepared to tend the small field beyond the gate, while hunters readied themselves to roam the outskirts, keeping the Wildborn population low to prevent future attacks.
Among them, Lawrence, a retired mercenary and the strongest fighter in the village, was already preparing for another hunt. As the unofficial leader of the hunters, he kept a watchful eye on the settlement. 
“Brother Lawrence!”
A panicked voice broke the morning calm. A hunter came running, breath ragged, a machete still in his trembling hands.
“What is it?” Lawrence asked, offering him a water gourd to steady his breathing.
The man gulped down air, his face pale. “It’s… it’s bad,” he stammered. “There’s a horde of Wox… heading straight for the village. And…” His voice faltered, shaking with fear. “We… we think an Alpha Wox is leading them.”
Lawrence’s jaw tightened. An Alpha Wox… an ascendant Wildborn. This was worse than anything they’d faced usually. His mind raced with worry, but he didn’t let it show to the villagers now gathering around.
“Don’t panic,” he said firmly, his voice steady and commanding. “I’ll hold off the Alpha. The rest of you—focus on the horde. Protect the village.”
The fear in their eyes didn’t vanish, but his words steadied their hearts.
“Let’s go, brothers!” one man shouted, raising his sword. “We’ll defend our home no matter what!”
The village chief, Dominic, a one-armed man with weathered eyes, grabbed an axe and stepped forward. “Tonight, we’ll feast on Wox meat!” he roared, rallying their spirits.
The village scrambled into action. Children and the elderly were quickly gathered and sheltered in the village hall, Granny Emma leading them to safety. Men and women capable of fighting took up what weapons they had and joined the defense.
Dominic and Lawrence stood at the gate, the hunters forming a loose line behind them.
“Brother Lawrence,” Dominic said quietly, his voice heavy, “I’m sorry, but we have no choice. The Alpha Wox… we’ll have to leave it to you.”
Lawrence gripped his weapon, a heavy stone sword, shaped and reinforced by his Echo of Earth. “Don’t worry, Chief. I’ll keep it busy. But I won’t be able to help with the rest of the horde.”
Dominic placed his one good hand on Lawrence’s shoulder. “I know what you’re thinking. But we’ve all accepted our lives here. Some of us may fall… but as long as the children are safe, there is hope for a better future.
Lawrence nodded silently, determination burning in his eyes as the first distant roars of the approaching horde echoed through the forest.
Leading the pack was a creature far more fearsome than the rest—a larger, leaner, foxlike Wildborn that stood nearly as tall as a man at the shoulder. Its fur shimmered with streaks of orange and silver, a sharp contrast to the plain orange coats of the Wox trailing behind it.
Without warning, the Alpha Wox lunged. With a single bound, it dashed through the air in a short burst of speed, its movement almost a blur. Then it unleashed a piercing, echoing cry—a shriek so sharp it sliced the air itself. The cry summoned cutting gusts of wind that tore through the ground and ripped into anything in their path.
Lawrence reacted instantly. Having mastered the Echo of Earth, he stomped the earth and raised a thick stone wall before the village gate. The wall groaned into existence just in time to shield them from the deadly wind.
But the moment the Alpha struck, chaos erupted. The rest of the Wox horde broke formation, circling around the stone barrier and launching themselves at the defenders from every direction. Villagers screamed as claws and fangs came tearing in, and Dominic stepped forward, rallying the fighters to meet the assault head-on.
Above the frenzy, the Alpha Wox landed atop the very stone wall Lawrence had raised. It stood there with eerie calm, silver-orange fur ruffling in the wind, its cold eyes fixed solely on Lawrence.
And Lawrence… ignored everything else. He kept his gaze locked on the Ascendant Wildborn, knowing this duel would decide the fate of the village.
I’ve fought Wildborn before—even Ascendants. But this one… this isn’t about glory or coin. I won’t let it destroy this village. Not while I still stand.
