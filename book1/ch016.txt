Chapter 16: Nothing serious
As the battle raged on, more Bloomfangs broke through from the forest side, but none reached the carriage. The Tremorhoof’s crushing hooves and Thomas’s relentless winds made sure of that.
The three non-echo guards regrouped at the carriage, holding the line alongside Baldwin. Thomas darted from one skirmish to another, gliding with the grace of the wind. Each sweep of his arm unleashed powerful bursts that sent wildborns reeling, giving the others room to strike.
The Tremorhoof’s massive frame became a wall of destruction, kicking and trampling any beast that dared come close. Above, Michael stood firm atop the carriage, loosing arrows with deadly precision—each one striking true, splitting skulls and felling the creatures that slipped past the front lines. When needed, his aim shifted seamlessly, cutting down wildborns that threatened his comrades locked in the melee below.
Dust swirled around the wagon as Gian, Kel, and Edmund held the perimeter, blades and axes flashing in brief glints of sunlight. Blood-soaked fur and mangled vines marked the path of the Bloomfangs’ charge, and the smell of iron and smoke clung to the air 
“Damn, that Tremorhoof’s terrifying,” Kel muttered, wiping the sweat from his brow as he watched the towering beast rear and slam its hooves down, crushing a Bloomfang into the dirt until it stopped moving.
“I’ll head to the carriage side. You two hold here—shout if you need me,” Edmund said before sprinting off.
“Kel,” Gian called sharply, sword raised, “two more—behind the trees, heading this way.”
Kel grinned, kicking aside the corpse at his feet. “Good. Let them come. I’ve still got blood to spill.”

It took several minutes, but finally Gian spotted one last Bloomfang breaking away—only for an arrow to pierce its back and drop it mid-run.
“Damn ugly beast,” Michael muttered, panting heavily as his summoned longbow dissolved into mist.
Gian scanned the battlefield, littered with corpses—six of them by his own hand.
“You good?” Kel asked, collapsing onto the ground beside him, his dark red axe landing with a dull thud.
“Yeah. Thanks for covering me at the start,” Gian said.
Still catching his breath, Kel smirked faintly. “Don’t sweat it.”
Edmund soon returned, drained and bleeding from a gash in his left shoulder, though his eyes stayed sharp. “Good work, both of you. I didn’t expect you to hold out this well and let me focus on the carriage side.”
“It wasn’t that serious, sir. Just feral wildborns,” Kel laughed, though he stayed seated, too tired to rise.
“Haha, true enough. Feral types aren’t worth bragging about,” Edmund said with a weary chuckle. He climbed into the wagon, set something down with a dull thump, then pulled out a jug and drank deeply.
“What now, sir?” Gian asked, his gaze flicking toward the treeline.
“Wait for Sir Torren. We’ll likely be here a while. Use the time to rest—I’m more concerned about the larger horde Margaret’s tracking at the rear.”
“I see,” Gian murmured, his eyes following the direction Edmund had indicated.
Kel, noticing what Edmund had dropped, leaned forward. “Sir… is that what I think it is?”
“Yeah... a Verdant Echo,” Edmund said, still looking at the tome with disbelief. He touched his bleeding shoulder and laughed quietly. “It appeared over the Bloomfang the moment I killed it. I got distracted watching it form, and that’s how I ended up with this.” 
“Damn, you’re so lucky, sir,” Kel said in awe as he stood, staring at the tome. Its cover was plated with fangs, and at its center, a faint green symbol pulsed: a small circle surrounded by expanding rings, like ripples spreading across water. 

Up front, Torren returned, his armor streaked with wildborn blood, his massive warhammer hanging in one hand. At his side, David supported an injured Agnes, her arm draped over his shoulder, while Hugo steadied her from the other side, blood running down his own left arm. The two other mercenaries trailed behind—battered, bruised, and carrying the lifeless body of a fallen comrade.
“Everything alright here?” Torren asked, his eyes on Thomas, who was dusting off his red traveling hat from the ground.
“Yeah, nothing serious. Edmund caught a claw to the shoulder, but he’ll live,” Thomas replied, nodding toward Edmund, who sat by the wagon speaking with Kel and Gian. “On the upside, he got an Echo of Perception out of one of them.”
“I see. That’s good. As Lord Henry instructed, whatever Echo you find, it’s yours. He can choose to use it, trade for gold, or pass it up. Let him decide later. He needs rest.” 
“We all need rest,” Thomas muttered, his gaze flicking toward the Tremorhoof, its massive form bloodied but still standing. “Good thing you left that beast here. It held the line better than some men.”
“And your side, sir?” he asked.
“Mostly minor wounds. Agnes took it worst—bit to the leg. Could’ve been fatal if Hugo hadn’t dropped the beast with an arrow.”
Torren’s eyes shifted to the corpse being dragged to the side of the road. His jaw tightened.
“One of the mercenaries panicked, ran toward the forest. Maybe he thought our formation would break. I couldn’t get to him in time.”
Thomas lowered his voice. “Can’t blame him. The pack was larger than we expected.”
“Yeah. The fighting drew in others nearby. Good thing they scattered in the end, or more would’ve fallen,” Torren said, scanning the carnage. 
“What now, sir?” Thomas asked.
“We rest. And we bury the dead,” Torren replied, letting his warhammer fall heavily against the ground.
“I’ll see to it,” Thomas said, moving quickly toward the fallen man.
“David, check on Margaret and bring me a report on the large pack we avoided earlier. I need its status as soon as possible—we can’t handle that pack right now,” Torren ordered.
“On it, sir,” David replied, summoning a smaller, wearier translucent wolf. It was proof of his fatigue, yet he understood the urgency.
“Michael, scout the direction the pack came from,” Torren said, pointing north. “But stay within sight.”
“Don’t worry, sir,” Michael replied, strapping on a quiver from the second wagon. He now carried a real longbow, conserving his stamina in case of another attack.
“You two,” Torren called, pointing at nearby non-echo guards, “patrol both sides of the path. Watch the forest edges.”
With the perimeter secured, Torren turned toward the open carriage, where Lord Henry stood at the door, waiting for the report.

After reporting to Lord Henry, Torren stepped away and scanned the aftermath. His gaze settled on Gian and Kel, both oddly unfazed. Their expressions were calm, almost indifferent, as if no lives had just been lost. Yet this was supposed to be their first journey.
By the wagon, Gian sat in silence, wiping his blade clean before carefully sharpening it. Beside him, Kel whistled cheerfully while bandaging Edmund’s arm, seemingly untouched by the chaos they had survived.
Torren’s brow furrowed. The sight unsettled him. And then a memory surfaced—
The Eclipse of Tala.
It had been only a few years ago, a tragedy so widespread it shook not only the kingdom but the nearby borders as well. A massacre that claimed hundreds of lives, including the mayor and a military captain. Entire districts of the town were wiped out: the north and west reduced to rubble, the plaza left in ruins.
And it all happened in under two hours. Like an eclipse that appeared without warning, the killers vanished just as swiftly, leaving behind a scar the townsfolk would never forget.
The reason was never discovered, which made it all the more mysterious. The King of Lanthea eventually abandoned the search; there were no clues as to where the killers had come from.
Torren narrowed his eyes at Gian and Kel.
They were probably there, he thought grimly. They must have lived through it. That’s why they’re numb now.
