Chapter 11: Luck Never Saved Anyone 
The sun slowly rose behind the hills, casting a warm, golden light over the quiet town of Tala. Though the town appeared unchanged, something subtle had shifted in the years since that tragic night. It looked the same, but it didn't feel the same.
Atop a hill, an 18-year-old young man stood, chest heaving as he delivered a final downward slash. His morning training complete, he sheathed his blade and began the descent toward the town below.
As he walked, the air was filled with the laughter of children chasing each other through the dirt paths. Their joy was a welcome contrast to the heaviness he carried.
Passing through the central plaza, he noticed groups of teenagers training under the watchful eyes of elders. Some grumbled with frustration, while others pressed on with quiet determination. At the front, a new group of students, eyes wide with innocence, listened intently as an elder began their first lesson.
As he reached his destination, he pushed the front door open, and the old wind chime hanging from it clattered, a familiar sound that tugged at his heart. Inside the small home, he started to cook a simple meal.
With everything patched up and essential items packed, he paused at the door, his eyes sweeping over the familiar room one last time. Then, steeling himself, he stepped out, not knowing when or if he would ever return.

He pushed open the door to the blacksmith’s workshop, and heat rolled over him in a wave. The air was thick with coal smoke and the sharp tang of hot iron. Somewhere in the back, metal rang against metal—steady, practiced blows. 
“Is it finished, Dolt?” Gian asked, stepping carefully around scattered tools and buckets of water turned black with soot. 
“Yeah, just a second. I’ll grab it,” Dolt replied, disappearing behind a hanging curtain of oiled leather.
The hammering stopped.
A moment later, Dolt returned.
Draped over one arm was a black chainmail shirt, its tightly woven links catching the forge-light in muted silver flashes. It looked light enough to move in, yet dense as iron scales. In his other hand rested a single-edged short sword, the steel polished clean, its edge thin and hungry. Even at a glance, the balance was evident—made for speed, not brute force.
Dolt laid them carefully on the scarred wooden counter. Then he reached behind him and brought forward a longbow stained a deep green, its limbs sleek and unadorned, built for quiet strength rather than show. A dark leather quiver followed, filled with thirty arrows fletched in matching green, their feathers trimmed tight for clean, silent flight.
The forge crackled behind them.
Dolt hesitated. “Are you sure about this, Gian?”
Gian nodded once. “I’ve stayed long enough. If I hadn’t needed time to prepare… I would’ve left right after the Rite of Age.”
The words hung between them.
Dolt pushed the weapons closer, jaw tight. “Just… be careful out there.”
“I will. Thanks—for everything.”
He slung the chainmail over his shoulder. At the door, he paused and gave Dolt a final nod before stepping out into the pale morning light.

Outside, the air felt cooler, cleaner. He moved toward a quiet alley between two stone buildings, checking the street before slipping into the shadows.
One by one, he placed the short sword, longbow, and quiver into his Void Ring. Each vanished without a sound, swallowed by the ring’s hidden space—twenty-seven cubic meters of silence and secrecy resting on his finger.
He turned the ring slightly.
A memory surfaced.
He had burst into the house that day, breathless with excitement, telling his mother about the traveling merchant and the strange relic he’d acquired. She had listened quietly, her expression unreadable. Then she had cupped his face and spoken softly.
Keep it secret. You’re not strong enough yet. A tool like that draws danger.
The memory tightened something in his chest.
He looked down at the ring again. This time, there was no excitement—only resolve.
Before putting on the chainmail, Gian ran his fingers over the interwoven links. Cold. Solid. Reliable. The metal felt like a promise—and a warning.
He slipped it on.
The weight settled against him, not heavy, but undeniable. A reminder that this was no longer preparation.
This was departure.
He pulled his clothes over the armor, hiding its presence. His old, worn sword hung at his side, its familiar weight grounding him more than the new steel ever could.

At the thought of his mother, his resolve hardened.
Then he headed toward the market.
Vendors were just opening their stalls, calling out half-hearted greetings as he purchased dried meat and small jugs of water. When no one was watching, the supplies disappeared into the Void Ring.
By the time he stepped away from the stalls, he carried nothing visible.
But he was ready.

Gian stepped into the bar, the dim interior swallowing him whole after the glare of the midday sun. Outside, the hanging sign creaked faintly in the heat, its paint long faded, chains rusted thin. It looked as though one strong storm might finally tear it loose.
Inside, smoke curled beneath the rafters, turning the air thick and sour. The scent of cheap liquor and old wood lingered in every corner. Low conversations hummed like insects, cautious and half-muted. In the far corner, a musician lazily strummed a battered guitar, the notes drifting without direction.
This wasn’t a place people came for comfort.
It was where information changed hands.
Gian didn’t hesitate. He walked straight to the bar.
Bruno stood behind it, polishing a glass with slow, deliberate strokes.
“Hey, Bruno. Anything heading toward Tillara?” Gian asked as he took a seat.
Bruno paused mid-motion and glanced up. His brows knit slightly. “Tillara?” he repeated. “That’s not one of your usual errands.”
“It is now.”
Bruno studied him for a long moment, weighing the answer. “You sure about this kind of work?” His tone carried no judgment—only concern. He knew the difference between local coin-grabs and contracts for travelling. 
“I’m prepared,” Gian said evenly. “I know what I’m doing.”
Bruno set the glass down with a quiet thud. “I don’t meddle in my customers’ business,” he said. “But you’re not just a customer.”
Gian looked away briefly, memories surfacing—nights when he barely had enough coin for a meal, when Bruno had slipped him odd jobs and small tasks just to keep him afloat.
“I need to do this,” Gian said.
After a moment, Bruno nodded. “I’ll check.
He gestured for his partner to mind the counter and disappeared through the back door.
The minutes stretched.
Gian stared into his drink, watching the surface ripple faintly with each distant footstep. Tillara. The Border City of Lanthea. If no one was hiring, the journey would take weeks alone. Dangerous weeks.
But danger was part of the point.
Bruno returned, shaking his head. “Nothing that fits. Not yet.” A faint crease lined his brow. “I’ll keep listening.”
“Thanks,” Gian said quietly.
He drained the rest of his glass and stood.
Weeks on the road, he thought. Fine. I’ll treat it as training.
He pushed toward the door.
The hinges creaked—
—and the room shifted.
A tall figure stepped inside, bringing with him a gust of daylight and dust. Conversations faltered, not entirely stopping, but dimming in instinctive awareness.
He moved with measured confidence, broad shoulders outlined beneath a dark, travel-worn cloak. The fabric shifted as he walked, revealing glimpses of fitted armor beneath—steel that had seen use, not ceremony.
He didn’t look around.
He didn’t need to.
He walked straight to the counter.

Gian was nearing the north gate when a voice cut through the bustle behind him.
“Gian! Wait—stop!”
He turned as Bruno came jogging down the street, weaving through carts and pedestrians, slightly out of breath.
“Can’t you walk slower?” Bruno puffed when he finally caught up. “You left minutes ago and vanished.”
Gian frowned. “What’s wrong?”
Bruno held out a folded sheet of parchment. “You’re not going to believe this. You’re lucky. Right after you walked out, a man posted a contract—caravan heading to Tillara.”
Gian’s fingers paused before taking it.
Luck, he thought bitterly. The word felt hollow. Mocking.
Luck never saved anyone.
Bruno didn’t notice the shift in his expression. “Details are there, but short version—small caravan traveling from Esmara to Tillara.”
Esmara, Gian mused, recalling the town west of here. A few days’ journey. 
“They were hit on the road,” Bruno continued, voice lowering. “Wildborns. Strong ones. Lost a few mercenaries. Since we’re the nearest safe stop, they’re hiring replacements.”
The noise of the gate drifted around them—wagon wheels grinding against stone, guards calling out inspections, travelers arguing over tariffs. Life moving forward, unaware.
Gian scanned the sheet quickly. The assignment was to guard a wealthy family of four traveling to Tillara City. Payment was set at eighty silver, with thirty upfront and the rest upon arrival. Provisions would be supplied throughout the journey.
“I see…” he murmured.
Bruno searched his face. “You don’t have to take it.”
Gian folded the paper once more. “I was leaving either way.”
Bruno exhaled slowly. “Just… stay sharp. It’s usually safe travel from here to the city but still be careful.”
Gian gave a short nod. “Thanks. For everything.”
Bruno clapped his shoulder once, firm and familiar, before heading back toward the bar.
Left alone near the gate, Gian tucked the parchment into his coat and stepped toward the plaza where caravans gathered.
