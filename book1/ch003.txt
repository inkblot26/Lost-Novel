Chapter 3: Lines on Parchment
In one of the quieter rooms of the learning hall beside the town plaza, the walls were draped with faded maps whose edges curled like dry leaves.  Shelves sagged beneath the weight of old books, their spines cracked and faded from years of use. At the front of the room stood Elder Eldon, cloaked in deep green. His sharp gray eyes swept a handful of students who murmured to one another, only half-listening seated cross-legged on woven mats.
Behind him stretched a wide parchment map pinned to a wooden board. Its inked lines shimmered faintly in the warm torchlight, dotted with symbols, runes and sketches of strange beasts.
“Now listen well, children of Tala,” Eldon began, his voice calm yet carrying the weight of command. “What I tell you today has been spoken since the seas were veiled in mist, and the Line was erected to protect us from the Wild.”
He tapped the center of the map with the tip of his cane.
“This… is your world.”
The students leaned forward, curiosity flickering. Even Gian, seated quietly at the back, lifted his gaze from the rain streaking the window. 
“This vast land before you—the Great Continent—is where the light of civilization burns brightest. Look closely at its shape. To the west, a sharp sideways-tilted triangle carved with purpose and order. To the east, a boxed shadow—uncharted, untamed. And between them lies not sea nor mountain, but the Verdant Ring… our living barrier.
A boy snorted under his breath. “But it’s just a red line on the map, isn’t it?”
Another voice spoke up, louder this time. “And how do we even know it’s boxed if it’s uncharted?”
Eldon didn’t flinch.
“To the far north, across the frozen sea, lies the Norvalen Kingdom, the realm of silence and snow. Icebound and proud, it holds only two cities—Nivara, its capital, locked in winter all year, and beyond it, an isolated city in the Unknown… Draemreach. They say those who serve there return… changed.”
He let the silence linger, settling over the students like frost.
“Changed how?” a girl asked tentatively. “Like… frostbite?”
Ignoring her, Eldon swept his sleeve westward across the map.
“Across the Sea of Glass lies the island kingdom of Faylora. Its lone city, Freymar, rests at the water’s edge like a shell watching the tide. Traders, storm-callers, wanderers—all cross paths there. Its markets hum with exotic spices and salt wind.”
“What does he mean by storm-caller?” a boy in front of Gian muttered.
“It’s just random stuff,” Ren replied, rolling his eyes.
Penny, the girl in their class who always asked questions, raised her hand.
“What are those dots around the island of Faylora?”
“Those are smaller islands, also part of their kingdom,” Eldon answered. Before Penny could press further, he added, “There’s no exact count. They vary in size, and some don’t even have a single community. So don’t ask me.”
His hand drifted back toward the mainland before Penny could speak again. 
“Now… to the great land.”
His tone deepened, heavy with pride.
“Aethelgard Kingdom. Strongest in number, richest in soil. It claims the northern triangle of the Great Continent. At its heart stands Aethermarch, its capital, its towers gleaming like fire at sunset. Guarding it are two adjacent cities—Halvard to the north, the First Wall City, and Ravenscar to the south, the Second Wall City. Together, they anchor the northern reach of the Verdant Ring—our line of vigilance.”
Penny frowned.
“Aren’t those cities too close to the Unknown? Wouldn’t the Wildborn try to attack them?”
Eldon raised a hand to silence the thought.
“They are close by necessity, not by mistake. And at the center of their kingdom, hidden deep within misted woods, lies Mistwood, a city of rangers and whisperers who tame hawks and speak to wolves.”
He let the image linger before continuing.
“And last, near the western tip of the triangle, stands Jubila, a city of color, music, and celebration. Known across the kingdoms for its week-long festivals, gladiator fights, and moonlit dances. They say even the most burdened heart finds joy there.”
Next to Gian, a small boy in glasses with a bowl haircut scribbled furiously, trying to copy the map with all the energy he had. Kaito’s head bobbed up and down, eyes darting between the parchment and his messy lines.
On Gian’s other side, Ren chuckled. “I’m the one getting dizzy for you, Kaito.”
Gian nudged him to quiet down, but for the first time he felt the weight of how vast the real world truly was.
Elder Eldon’s cane traced a zigzag river south of Jubila City. “This is the Naga River, dividing two kingdoms. Below lies Lanthea, the kingdom of golden fields. Its capital, Liora, rests beside a shimmering lake. To the east, near the Verdant Ring, stands Tillara—a border city kissed by both grain and danger.”
He paused, then tapped a small green circle between Tillara and the Gobe Desert’s edge, at the southern reach of the Great Continent.
“And here… a town not yet etched into history. Small, yes, but greatness does not always bloom in palaces.”
“Wait… is that Tala? Our town?” Ren asked. Eldon, as always, ignored the interruption. 
“Lanthea also holds Salrima, city of salt and wind, perched upon sea-cliffs near Jubila. Its cliffs are white as bone, and its waters whisper back to those who listen.”
Then came the inevitable question.
“What is the Verdant Ring?” Penny asked softly.
Eldon stepped back from the map.
“It is no mere line of cities and outposts. It is a promise. A boundary. It marks where order ends… and the Wild begins. No road crosses it without risk. No map beyond it holds truth. The lands to the east bear but one name—Unknown. But the Wildborn?”
His voice dropped.
“They do not mark it. They live it.”
Slowly, he turned to face the students, his gray eyes catching the flicker of torchlight. 
“And so this map, children, is not just lines and colors. It is your past. Your defense. Your test. Some say the Ring will fail from within. Others say the Wild will rise. But the wise…”
He paused, whispering words that seemed to fill the room.
“…the wise know—the Ring does not hold the Wild back.
It holds us in.”
Silence. Even the restless students stilled.

When class finally ended and the students began to stretch and chatter again, Ren groaned and slung his satchel over one shoulder.
“This is giving me a headache with all this useless information,” he muttered. “I’m not smart like Kaito.”
“Aren’t you excited to go to these different places?” Kaito asked, still carefully copying the map into his notes.
“I’m not from a rich family like you, mate. I can’t pay for those traveling fees,” Ren said, nudging Gian toward the door.
“Well, see you later, Kaito,” Gian said, adjusting the strap of his training bag.
“Yeah. Take care, guys,” Kaito replied, eyes never leaving the page.
As Gian and Ren passed Penny and a cluster of other girls near the doorway, snippets of animated chatter drifted their way. They were talking about the Four Kingdoms—arguing, dreaming, imagining where they’d go if given the chance.
“I’d definitely choose the Norvalen Kingdom,” Penny said, her eyes bright with excitement. “I want to join the exploration teams and venture into the Endless White.”
“That sounds boring,” another girl scoffed. “There’s nothing out there—just ice and snow as far as you can see.”
Ren clicked his tongue. “Your topic’s boring me,” he said flatly, rolling his eyes as he nudged past them. “Now, if you don’t mind, stop blocking the door.”
He and Gian stepped out into the corridor, the noise of the room fading behind them.
“I can’t keep up,” Ren muttered, rubbing the back of his neck. “Eldon talks nonstop. When do we actually start fighting for real?” 
“It’s Elder Eldon,” Gian replied calmly. “What did you expect? We’ve got a whole year of book learning and body training before we even touch real combat.”
Ren scoffed. “Yeah, yeah. ‘Foundations are important,’ and all that.” He glanced at his hands, flexing his fingers. “But seriously—when do we finally get a sword in our hands?”
Gian grinned.
“But this is essential knowledge, so don’t be disheartened, Master Ren.” He gave a mock bow and chuckled.
Ren laughed sarcastically. “Very funny.”
“I’m not curious about some distant island, like that frozen kingdom or whatever. It’s not like we’ll ever set foot there. So what’s the point?”
Gian didn’t answer. Through the window, his gaze drifted back to the parchment map still pinned to the board.
Maybe we won’t… yet, deep down, a quiet spark whispered that one day, he might.
