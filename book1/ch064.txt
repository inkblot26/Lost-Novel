Chapter 64: Awareness in the Dark
Oldman and Gian rushed toward Kel, who lay sprawled in the damp grass, blood pooling beneath his hand and soaking into the earth.
“Quick, Gian, give me a Lileaf! I need to patch up his hand. The healing properties of that echotech work faster when I combine them with my Echo,” Oldman barked.
Gian immediately summoned a dried green leaf with a faint sweet aroma and handed it over. Oldman wrapped the deep gash on Kel’s hand, then closed his eyes. Tiny yellow-green fireflies began to glow from his palms, drifting gently into the wound as flesh knit together at a visible pace.
“I’ll leave him to you. I need to back up El and Anni,” Gian said, stepping back.
“Follow Tata. He’ll lead you to them,” Oldman replied without opening his eyes. “They ran into the denser part of the forest on the opposite side. You might get lost.”
Right on cue, Tata leapt onto Gian’s shoulder, squeaking.
Just before Gian could dash off, Oldman added grimly, “Be careful. Don’t forget, Diego is an Ascendant wildborn who can even speak. He’s dangerous. I’ll follow as soon as I’m sure Kel is safe.”
Gian gave a sharp nod. Tata scurried down into the grass and bolted ahead, with Gian sprinting close behind.

After several minutes of running, Anni grew impatient. Summoning four ice butterflies that swirled around her shoulders, she quickened her pace, desperate to catch up to Gromo. Suddenly, he stopped and turned toward her with a wide grin.
Anni skidded to a halt and raised her saber.
“What are you smiling at, bandit?”
“I’m smiling because you were stupid enough to let yourself get lured away from your companions,” Gromo chuckled, slowly stalking toward her. “Especially that woman… she looked dangerous. Not that it matters—Diego will kill her soon enough.”
“Hmph, you wish. That wildborn will be shredded to pieces once Auntie El unleashes her full power,” Anni shot back, adjusting into her stance.
Gromo paused mid-step. “What do you mean wildborn?”
Anni laughed. “Looks like you’re the stupid one. You’ve been following your precious ‘Diego’ without even realizing it’s a mimic — just a Mimivane in disguise.”
“Stop spouting nonsense!” Gromo snarled.
“Believe whatever you want. It won’t matter. You’re all dying tonight,” Anni said coolly, glancing skyward as the last light faded and night finally claimed the sky. 
“We’ll see,” Gromo sneered. Flesh rippled as he activated his rare Echo of Flesh, regenerating his once-missing limb.
Anni’s expression darkened. “So that’s your Echo… You’re the only one we didn’t get intel on from that stupid monkey.”
“Who are you talking about?” Gromo asked, shrugging off his purple robe to reveal a back lined with strapped short swords.
“That other idiot bandit — Maro,” Anni said coldly. “He’s dead now. Told us everything we needed before he went. You all deserve to die.”
With that, she lunged. Her saber came down in a flurry of slashes, Gromo blocking with a long sword in one hand and a short sword in the other.
“Oh, we’ll all die someday, little girl,” Gromo laughed, and then two more pairs of arms burst from his sides.
Anni leapt back, eyes wide.
Now he stood before her with six limbs, each extra hand armed with a short sword.
Gromo lunged. His upper four arms lashed out in a relentless storm of slashes, while the lower pair guarded his torso, ready to intercept any counterattack. Steel flashed toward Anni in a storm of blades. She deflected the longsword, then ducked and twisted away from the others, her boots skidding across the grass as she gave ground under the relentless assault.
“Where are you going, little girl?” Gromo laughed, pursuing her mercilessly. One strike caught her, slashing across her right leg. She couldn’t keep up—not without the help of her ice butterflies, which collided with blades she couldn’t dodge in time.
Gromo threw his head back, laughing wildly.
“Let’s see how many of those you can summon while I carve you up!”
The last ice butterfly shattered as it parried a blow, and Anni darted sideways, rolling to create space between them.
“Run, little girl, run,” Gromo taunted, turning toward her once more.
Anni slowly rose, her blue eyes blazing. 
“I’m done running. I’ll never run again… now that I’m strong.”
Five new ice butterflies formed from her left palm, spinning outward like glassy shards.
Gromo sneered. “You’re no fun. I was hoping to chase you as I killed you.”
He charged again. Anni met him head-on, saber flashing. The butterflies swarmed around her, intercepting the blows she couldn’t dodge or block, catching steel at the very last second as she fought back fiercely.
Anni fought on, step by step retreating. Blood trickled from multiple cuts — one dangerously close to her eye — while Gromo remained completely untouched. Finally, her foot caught on a root. She fell backwards, her saber clattering into the grass beside her.
Gromo laughed and strode forward, settling all six blades at his sides in a relaxed pose.
“You’ve got spirit, girl. I’ll admit that. Lasting this long with nothing but a saber and those little ice bugs… but I’m getting bored now.”
“You’re right,” Anni said, breathless, meeting his gaze with her blue eyes. “Let’s end it.”
Gromo grinned, showing sharp yellowed teeth.
“What a shame. You’re a real beauty. I usually like long blondes like you. How about you stop resisting and become my bride instead?”
Anni burst into laughter.
His face darkened. He raised the longsword. “Guess you choose death, then.”
Before he could swing, he stopped — suddenly noticing the swarm of a dozen ice butterflies surrounding him on every side.
“What—?” His eyes widened.
Anni smiled.
The butterflies shot inward all at once, their razor-sharp wings carving through flesh as frost spread instantly from each cut, crackling across his skin like creeping ice. Gromo didn’t even have time to scream. He toppled to the ground with a heavy crash, body shredded, breath rattling in his throat as he lay there bleeding and rimed with ice.
Anni picked up her saber and stood over him.
“You’re ugly. Definitely not my type,” she muttered, driving her blade clean through his neck.
She wiped the blood from her saber and slid it back into its sheath. 
“If you’d been a little less arrogant, you might have noticed…” She glanced at the frost-laden grass.
“Every step I took left an ice butterfly behind. They’ve been waiting just above the grass this whole time.”
With a flick of her blond hair, she turned away.
“Hmph. Ugly and stupid. As if I don’t have standards.”
Then she broke into a sprint in the direction she had come from, racing to find El.

In the middle of the forest, beneath the cover of night, El’s right leg bled steadily as she stared ahead.
“Just my luck… another Ascendant wildborn. Glimstep,” she muttered, ignoring the pain.
Before her stood a small, deer-like wildborn with sleek fur that shimmered in soft hues of blue and silver, its body glowing faintly in the darkness.
I can’t move carelessly. Diego is nearby… hidden among the trees with that camouflage of his, waiting to strike again—just like he did with my leg, she thought, eyes sweeping her surroundings. She gripped two razor-edged ice swords, maintaining a tight, compressed field of gravity around herself—minimizing its range to conserve stamina while keeping its pull ready in case she needed to yank down any incoming projectile.
The Glimstep drifted slowly through the night like a flicker of moonlight, leaving faint trails of luminous dust in its wake. Between its antlers, a distorted white orb of energy began to form, charging… pulsing… preparing to fire.
When it releases that blast again, I’ll double the gravity pull, dodge, and that’s when Diego will make his move. I just need to be patient, El told herself, tightening her grip on the icy blades.

“Come on, Tata. Feel the surroundings. We need to find them. They need us,” Gian urged the small driftmouse perched on his shoulder.
Tata twitched, whiskers quivering as its head darted left and right, feeling for vibrations in the air and earth.
There must be wildborns nearby. That’s why Tata’s on edge, Gian thought, remembering the two Apelang he had killed not long ago.
Suddenly, Tata squeaked sharply, glanced up at Gian, and gave a quick nod before sprinting east. Gian hurried after it.
After several minutes of running, Gian spotted a faint, flickering glow in the distance—unnatural against the darkness of the forest. 
“What’s that?” he whispered, slowing as Tata came to a stop and crouched low in the grass. Gian followed its lead, creeping forward on his haunches toward the strange glow.
Creeping closer, Gian finally saw it — the Glimstep. He watched as it fired a distorted white orb toward something ahead. Following its trajectory, he realized El was standing directly in its path.
Just before the sphere reached her, its momentum suddenly slowed. El hurled one of her ice swords into it. The impact detonated the orb, the blast flinging her violently to the side.
Gian’s heart clenched.
In that same moment, the Glimstep vanished in a burst of light, reappearing several paces away in a flicker—a short-distance blink. 
“Tata, stay here and hide. I’m going to back her up!” Gian barked, sprinting forward while summoning his black longbow.
He took aim, but before he could loose an arrow, something moved above. Perched in the treetops where El had fallen was a long-limbed, lanky reptilian wildborn with a whip-like tongue, tensing to pounce.
Gian’s arrow flew in an instant. The creature noticed him at the last moment and sprang away, landing on a branch of a nearby tree.
Gian reached El just as she rose to her feet, staring at the tree branch where the creature had vanished.
“Thanks,” she said, her tone sincere. 
“What’s going on? Where’s Diego?” Gian asked. He dismissed his bow, drawing his short sword in one hand and buckler in the other, eyes fixed on the Glimstep.
“That was Diego,” El replied. “His real form — a chameleon-type wildborn. He’s blending into the trees now. Things were going smoothly... until that Ascendant wildborn showed up and gave him an opening to escape. Now he’s waiting for another opening to kill me.”
Gian blinked, glancing toward the Glimstep as it began charging another distorted white orb.
“That’s an Ascendant?”
“Yes. It’s fragile in close combat, but relies on blinking to evade danger.” As she spoke, El reshaped her ice weapons into a large glaive of solid frost and gripped it with both hands.
“Now it’s two versus two,” she said calmly. “I’ll focus on killing one. Your job is to watch our surroundings and guard against Diego’s ambushes.”
“Got it,” Gian replied, stepping behind her as he scanned the trees, alert and ready.
The forest whispered around them. Leaves rustled softly in the wind, branches creaking like quiet warnings. Somewhere in the darkness, unseen eyes lingered—Diego was watching… waiting.
