Chapter 57: The man called Lawrence
The battle had raged for minutes, and now chaos spilled deep into the village streets beyond the gate. 
“Kill those damn Wox, quickly!” Dominic shouted, his voice raw with urgency. He swung his leg and released a curved burst of flame, striking a Wox from behind before sprinting toward the village hall at the center of the settlement.
But a glance through the shattered gate made his breath hitch. Just beyond the wall, Lawrence was locked in a brutal duel with the Alpha Wox.
Brother Lawrence… you have to win. Even if every one of us falls here, you must kill that Alpha Wox, Dominic thought desperately.
A sudden blur in the corner of his vision made him roll instinctively just as a Wox’s claws slashed through where he’d been. But before he could recover, another leapt at him, its fangs sinking into his left leg. 
“Argh!” Dominic roared in pain, gritting his teeth as he hacked downward with his axe. The blade split the creature’s skull, releasing its bite. Blood ran down his leg, but he had no time to stop. Around him, other villagers fought tooth and nail, their screams mixing with the growls of the Wildborn.

Outside the gate, Lawrence swung his stone-forged sword in relentless arcs, striking at the Alpha Wox. But the Ascendant was agile, slipping to the side or springing into the air—sometimes even walking on it for a brief moment before attacking again.
My stone armor’s already been shredded in places by its wind-cutting cry… I can endure that. But this can’t drag on any longer. Lawrence’s thoughts were sharp, focused.
Gathering his Echo, he stomped the earth. A new stone wall surged upward from the ground beneath him, not as a shield this time, but as a platform. A jagged stairway of stone rose before him, forming a path into the air.
He would meet the airborne Alpha Wox head-on.
As the Alpha Wox’s ability to levitate finally faded, its body crashed onto the elevated stone platform Lawrence had raised.
This is my chance… I’ll take the hit if it means I can strike it down, Lawrence thought, gripping his stone-forged sword with both hands.
With nowhere left to escape, the Alpha Wox dashed toward him in a feral charge, its maw opening to unleash another wind-cutting cry. Blades of air slashed into Lawrence’s stone armor, cutting through to his flesh beneath. He gritted his teeth, ignored the pain, and swung with every ounce of strength he had left.
The Alpha Wox tried to leap over him, but it was too slow. The blade cleaved through its right hind leg, severing muscle and bone. The Ascendant Wildborn stumbled, howling in rage as it tumbled off the platform and hit the ground below with a heavy thud.
Bleeding heavily from his chest, Lawrence staggered to the edge of the platform. Below, the Alpha Wox limped, its silver-orange fur now matted with blood. Its eyes burned with hatred as it released a different cry this time—deep, resonant, and commanding.
The sound carried across the entire village.
Instantly, the remaining Wox turned from the battle. The horde that had been tearing at the villagers suddenly broke away, retreating through the shattered gate and vanishing back into the trees of the Doom Forest.	
The Alpha Wox, struggling to stay upright, used what little strength it had to half-run, half-glide on the air. It limped away toward the forest, its form soon swallowed by the shadows between the trees.
The fighting was over.

Lawrence’s breath grew ragged. He dropped to one knee on the platform, clutching his chest where blood seeped through the cracks in his armor. I need… to stop the bleeding… the cut is deep.
He looked toward the gate. There, among the wreckage, lay the bodies of villagers he had called brothers and sisters. So many… too many.
With trembling hands, he dissolved the smaller stone walls he had summoned, leaving only the platform. It was too large and would drain what little strength he had left.
When he finally stepped inside the village, devastation greeted him. The streets were strewn with corpses—both Wildborn and villager. Those still alive were wounded, helping one another in stunned silence.
A woman, her face smeared with blood and dirt, ran to him, tears streaming down her cheeks. “Brother Lawrence,” she choked, struggling to keep her voice steady, “Chief Dominic… he’s dying.”
She led him toward the village hall. The ground around it was piled with Wox carcasses, evidence of a desperate last stand.
At the doorway, a crowd of wounded villagers circled their chief. Dominic lay on the floor, his body broken and bleeding, yet his gaze was calm as he saw Lawrence approach.
Lawrence forced himself forward, despite the pain screaming through his body. He knelt beside Dominic, not allowing himself to show weakness. “Brother Dominic… I’m sorry. I couldn’t protect the village as I should have.”
Dominic gave a faint, pained smile. “What are you saying? Without you… the village would’ve been lost long before this day. Thank you, my friend…”
With that, his breath faded.
The hall fell silent—until the villagers began to weep. Their dependable chief was gone.
But before the villagers could even begin to bury their fallen families and friends, a loud horn echoed outside the ruined gates.
“No…” Lawrence muttered, his face darkening. “The bandits. They timed it perfectly. They must have been watching us from a distance.”
Panic rippled through the wounded village. Some tried to stand, grabbing what weapons they could, while others rushed toward the gate despite their injuries.
“Everyone who can still fight—on me!” Lawrence shouted, forcing himself forward despite the bleeding wound in his chest. Step by step, he walked toward the gate.
Outside the shattered gate, a group of bandits was gathering in the open clearing. But unlike the usual ragtag raiders, these wore matching robes, their hoods hiding most of their faces, with some wearing simple masks.
I’ve never heard of a bandit group like this, Lawrence thought grimly as he counted at least four dozen men. In our state… we can’t fight them head-on. Maybe if we give them money, they’ll leave just like always.
The man leading them stepped forward. He removed his mask, revealing a cruel smile.
“With a hoarse voice, he shouted, “Surrender this village. Hand over all your treasures, your coin… and every scrap of food you have.”
Lawrence froze. That face… isn’t that Diego, the bandit leader? But why… why ask for everything? Even food? Bandits never strip a village completely. They always leave just enough for the villagers to survive—so they can raid again another day.
Behind Diego, a nervous bandit approached and whispered, “Boss… are we really taking everything? If we leave them nothing, they won’t survive. We won’t be able to raid them again later.”
Another one stepped forward, grumbling in a low voice, “Yeah, and what’s with these stupid matching robes? We look like soldiers or cultists or something. This isn’t how we—”
“Ah. I see.” Diego turned slowly toward the two of them, his smile widening.
In a flash, he thrust his sword into the first bandit’s chest, piercing straight through his heart.
“B-Boss!?” the second bandit gasped, stumbling backward and tripping onto the dirt. “W-we were just making a suggestion!”
Diego tilted his head slightly, his eyes cold. “And I appreciate it… but let me remind you—” he turned his gaze on the rest of the shocked bandits, “—I am your boss. When I say we wear these robes, we wear these robes. Because I like this color. Do you all understand?”
“Yes, boss!” the bandits answered in unison, trembling.
The man on the ground forced a weak laugh, raising his hands. “Y-yes, boss… I actually like this color too! It’s my favorite—haha!”
Diego smiled down at him. And in the same motion, he swung his blade and cleanly beheaded him.
The other bandits fell silent, fear etched on their faces.
The villagers stood frozen in shock, unable to understand why the bandits were killing their own.
This is bad… Lawrence thought grimly. There’s no choice. We should just give them everything. As long as we’re alive, we can find a way to survive.
He raised his hand subtly, signaling the villagers not to resist. He was preparing to surrender.
But Diego’s cold voice shattered that fragile hope.
“You’ve ruined my mood.” He glanced at the fresh corpse of the beheaded bandit, then turned his piercing gaze on the rest of his men. “All of you—” his lips curled into a cruel grin, “—I’ve changed my mind. Attack this village. Loot everything. Take what you want. Kill the rest. Leave only a few alive.”
“Go.”
The bandits snapped from their brief hesitation and charged toward the gates like a hungry pack of wolves.
“No!” Lawrence’s heart pounded. “Everyone, protect the village! Hold the gates—no matter what!”
But the fight was hopelessly one-sided. The villagers, already exhausted and wounded from the earlier horde attack, fell one by one. Only a few bandits were cut down before the remaining defenders, like Lawrence himself, were overwhelmed, beaten, and captured.

When it was over, the survivors, children, elders, and wounded alike, were dragged in chains and forced to kneel before the village hall. Diego stood before them, smiling faintly, a predator savoring the helplessness of his prey. 
“Please, sir, we beg you,” Granny Emma cried, bowing deeply before Diego. Her frail voice trembled with desperation. “Take whatever you want, just… please, don’t kill anyone else.”
One by one, the rest of the villagers followed her lead, heads lowered in silent pleading.
Diego threw his head back and laughed. “Hahaha! I love this. This is how it should be.” He stepped closer, his boots crunching on the blood-stained dirt. “Fine. I’ll give you all a chance. Starting today, every four months you will bring me everything—your money, your food, your so-called treasures. That will be your tribute.” He smiled like a serpent.
“That’s absurd!” a wounded villager dared to protest. “You’ll leave us nothing to survive!”
Diego didn’t even bother replying. He simply snapped his fingers.
Thwip!
An arrow struck the man straight through the chest, silencing him forever. The villagers gasped but none dared move or speak again.
“We’ll do it,” Lawrence said, his voice steady despite the bleeding wound on his chest. He locked eyes with Diego—eyes that remained unnervingly still, cold and unreadable, like a predator studying its prey. “Whatever you want… just don’t hurt anyone else.”
Diego tilted his head. “Oh? Then let’s make it interesting.” His smile turned twisted. “I’ll burn you alive. Then, after I’ve had my fun, we’ll take the loot and leave. How’s that? Fair enough?”
The villagers erupted in panic.
“No, don’t!”
“Please, not him!”
“Sir Lawrence, no!”
“Everyone!” Lawrence’s booming voice cut through their cries, silencing them instantly. He looked at them all, his gaze calm despite the pain. “Remember what Chief Dominic always said… as long as the children are safe, there is hope for a better future.”
Then, turning to Diego, he nodded. “I agree.”
“Excellent.” Diego clapped his hands. “Bring out the big gridiron. Tie him up. We’ll start by burning his back so I can see his face while it cooks.”
The villagers wept silently as their strongest protector was bound. The bandits pressed the scorching-hot gridiron against Lawrence’s back. Flesh sizzled. The smell of burning skin filled the air.
Diego watched, grinning, savoring every moment.
Yet Lawrence did not scream. His jaw tightened, his muscles tensed, but not a single cry of pain escaped his lips.
Finally, a small voice broke the silence.
“Stop it!”
Everyone turned. Lapin stood trembling.
“Lapin!” Granny Emma cried, pulling him back down. “Forgive him, Boss Diego. He’s just a child, please!” she pleaded desperately.
Diego raised his hand to snap his fingers.
But before he could, Lawrence suddenly laughed.
“Hey… hey! Turn me over!” he shouted loudly, his voice ringing over the area. “I’m done on this side!”
Diego blinked in surprise, then burst into laughter. “Hah! I like you!” He waved at the bandits. “Turn him over! Let’s see how well the other side cooks!”
