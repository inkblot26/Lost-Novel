Chapter 49: Lesser Villages, Greater Dangers
Kel kicked a small pebble along the dirt road, the sound of it clacking against gravel breaking the stillness of the high-noon trail flanked by low grass and scattered shrubs.
After a while, he spoke up.
“Hey… I know it sounds greedy, but maybe we should’ve taken Lord Ronald’s offer to pay for everything we bought.”
Oldman shot him a sideways glance, his tone dry. “You really have no shame, huh?”
A few paces ahead on the worn path, Tata padded silently, whiskers twitching as he scanned the edges of the trail for movement among the bushes and trees.
Kel shrugged. “What? Highlady Rose has a whole city, and Lord Henry did say she owed us for taking down Richard. We prevented a future disaster for Tillara, didn’t we?” He gave the stone another kick, sending it off into the grass this time.
“We’ve still got a few Fangnotes left. It doesn’t matter,” Gian said, walking with calm, measured steps.
“Yeah, but what if we come across a relic for sale one day?” Kel persisted, his tone turning wistful. “We’ll need every penny we can save.”
Oldman sighed, tired of the same argument. “Can you drop it already? We told you the crafters didn’t have any relics for sale. They’re not something you just stumble upon at a market.” 
“Can’t blame me for wishing,” Kel muttered. He pointed at Gian. “Look at him—he doesn’t even need a bag. That Void Ring makes life way too convenient.”
Oldman raised an eyebrow. “Funny. Where’s your bag, then?”
Kel grinned. “Well, all our stuff fits in Gian’s ring, so why bother?”
“It’s better this way,” Gian said. “We’re ready to fight anytime without being weighed down.”
“Yeah, you’re lucky to have that ring. Makes moving around a lot easier,” Oldman admitted.
Then his tone shifted a little, thoughtful. “Anyway, stop dreaming about more relics. They’re rare. Honestly, I was shocked to learn you two already have three.”
“Four, actually—since we got that Stickk,” Kel said with a smug smile.
Oldman blinked. “Right… damn. Four relics for a small group like ours? If bandits ever hear about that, they’ll hunt us down for sure.”
“Let them come,” Gian said seriously, his voice low. “We’ll deal with them.”
“Yeah! That way we get their money too,” Kel added with a grin.
Oldman chuckled. “You really want to stack up those Fangnotes, huh?”
Squeak. Squeak.
Tata chirped suddenly and stopped, turning back toward Oldman.
In an instant, Gian and Kel’s expressions hardened as they unsheathed their weapons. Gian’s short sword gleamed, and Kel’s Crimson Blood Axe rested ready in his grip as they scanned the trees.
But Oldman just laughed softly. “Relax. Tata’s just hungry. It wants to rest.”
Gian let out a slow breath and sheathed his blade. He tilted his head back, noting the sun. “Looks like it’s around noon. We should stop here.”
“Fine by me,” Kel said, rubbing his hands together eagerly. “Gian, bring out the stuff I bought. Now that I’ve got proper seasonings and ingredients, you two can leave the cooking to me—the Great Kel.”

Twelve days of travel passed uneventfully along the well-trodden road. As the group crested a gentle rise, the silhouette of a fortress-like outpost emerged in the distance, pale stone walls outlined against the horizon under a soft afternoon sun. 
“Well, that was a peaceful trip,” Kel said, stretching his arms overhead with a yawn.
“Of course it was,” Oldman replied. “This is a direct road from Tillara City to Outpost Two. It’s mainly used by soldiers, so Wildborn rarely stray near it. And bandits? Only fools would try anything here.”
“So that’s one of the outposts forming the Verdant Ring,” Gian said quietly, his gaze fixed on the looming fort.
The outpost rose at the edge of the Known Lands, its high limestone walls weathered by wind and sun, a silent sentinel against the misty expanse of the Unknown beyond. Evenly spaced watchtowers punctuated the walls, each with narrow arrow slits scanning the surrounding terrain. Atop the walls, green-and-gold banners displayed the Lanthea Kingdom’s sigil—a crossed hoe and sickle encircled by a ring of vines—fluttering faintly in the breeze. 
“Our kingdom, with Tillara City on the edge, has four outposts in the Verdant Ring, right?” Kel asked. 
Yeah,” Oldman nodded. “The great Aethelgard Kingdom has ten. Three guarding their northern city of Halvard, three for the southern city of Ravenscaar, and four for their capital, Aethermarch.”
“They really are different from Lanthea,” Kel muttered. “Our capital’s smack in the middle of the kingdom.”
“I heard Aethelgard built those cities right on the Ring on purpose,” Gian added.
“They’re not afraid of the Unknown,” Oldman said. “They send expeditions past the frontier every year—harvesting ores, food, rare materials, and keeping the Wildborn in check.”
Ahead, Tata squeaked several times.
“Yeah, yeah,” Oldman said with a chuckle.
“What?” Kel asked, confused.
“Nothing,” Oldman said casually. “Tata and I spent time in Aethelgard during our mercenary days.”
“What?!” Kel turned, eyes wide. “You’ve been there?!”
Oldman just smirked, offering no further explanation.

As they neared the outpost gates, a squad of five soldiers approached with disciplined steps.
“Good day, soldiers. We’re here under Highlady Rose’s command,” Oldman said, handing over a sealed letter stamped with Tillara’s emblem.
The sergeant broke the seal and scanned the contents. His expression shifted to recognition. “I see. Welcome to Outpost Two. Please, follow me. I’ll take you to Captain Kirk.”
They followed him through the heavy wooden gate.
Inside the walls, the outpost hummed with quiet activity. Watchtowers along the front wall faced outward, their guards alert, crossbows raised. Rectangular stone barracks lined the inner perimeter, enclosing an open yard where soldiers jogged in formation or moved between stations. A central stone fire pit served as a gathering point, where a handful spoke quietly while others sharpened blades or repaired armor, their routines deliberate. Some simply gazed toward the distant mist of the Unknown, lost in thought.
Gian’s eyes swept the yard. “How many soldiers are stationed here?” he asked.
The sergeant gave him a sidelong glance but then smiled faintly. “Normally I wouldn’t share that with outsiders. But considering the letter… this outpost holds around two hundred. We rotate patrols along the Verdant Ring.”
He gestured toward the largest building ahead. “Anyway, here we are.”
They stopped before a two-storey stone structure, clearly the main barracks and command post.
Inside, they followed the sergeant upstairs to an office.
A man in green military armor with a crew cut stood by the window, watching the yard below with a silent, stern presence.
“Captain Kirk,” the sergeant announced with a salute. “These men are from Tillara City. They carry a letter from Highlady Rose.”
The captain turned, took the letter, and dismissed the sergeant. “Thank you. You’re dismissed.”
As the door closed, he motioned for them to sit. Then, pulling out the letter, he read it silently.
When he finally spoke, his tone was calm but heavy. “I’m sorry, but we haven’t uncovered much information so far.”
The trio exchanged confused looks.
Kirk continued, “After Sergeant Brina’s squad was ambushed by those masked men in violet robes, I planned to send another squad of twenty to investigate. But our primary duty is holding the Verdant Ring. Outpost One to the north has reported sightings of a horde. Tomorrow noon, I’ll be sending a platoon of fifty soldiers to reinforce them. That delayed the investigation of the ambush.”
“May I ask what information you do have?” Gian asked.
Kirk leaned forward slightly. “It was a well-planned attack. Our men were caught off guard. They’d just finished clearing a horde of Wildborn and were resting when the ambush struck.”
“Only Private Joey survived. He hid and later made his way back toward the outpost. A patrol found him at the edge of the Doom Forest.”
Kel whispered, “What’s Doom Forest?”
“I’ll explain later,” Oldman murmured back. Tata sat motionless on his lap.
“At Highlady Rose’s request, I sent a message bird to Graylatch,” Kirk continued. “But I haven’t received a reply. Not unusual—sending birds into the Unknown is unreliable. Wildborn often kill them before they reach their mark.”
He paused grimly. “That, or the village is no more.”
“We understand,” Oldman said quietly. “It’s not uncommon for villages to rise and fall in the Unknown.”
“Yes,” Kirk nodded. “It was out of Highlady Rose’s kindness that she even showed concern for such a lesser village.”
He leaned back slightly, then gestured to the door. “It’s getting late. You’re likely tired from the journey. Sergeant Thompson is waiting outside. He’ll escort you to your quarters.”
As he made a small motion with his hand, the office door swung open on its own.
Echo of Grasp, Oldman noted silently. It lets you move objects within reach without touching them.
“Tomorrow you’ll meet Private Joey. You can question him directly,” Kirk added.
The three stood, thanked the captain, and followed Sergeant Thompson out of the office.

Their guest room occupied a corner of a secondary barracks building, a simple chamber with two bunks stacked along one wall, a small wooden table near the window, and minimal furnishings.
Kel flopped onto the lower bunk. “So, what’s Doom Forest?” he asked.
“And what did he mean by lesser village?” Gian added, removing his gear and setting it carefully aside.
Oldman stood by the table, gently feeding water to Tata from a wooden cup. After a moment, he walked to his pack, pulled out a rolled-up scroll, and spread a map across the table.
“Alright. Here’s the thing,” he began. “The Unknown is home to the Wildborn. It’s uncharted, unpredictable. That’s why if you look at this map we bought, it only shows the big picture—the four kingdoms, their cities, towns, and outposts.”
He tapped a blank stretch of parchment beyond the borders marked on the map.
“Ever wonder why no settlements appear out here?” 
“Because… they’re too small?” Kel guessed.
“Close,” Oldman nodded. “But the real reason is that villages can rise and fall fast. Their survival depends on leadership and whether they can pay taxes to the kingdom. If they can, they’re acknowledged, protected, connected. If not…” he let the word hang, “…they’re on their own.”
He sat back and tapped a section just beyond the Verdant Ring.
“Outside the official lands, out here in the Unknown, there are folks who choose to live without kingdom oversight. Sometimes it’s because they can’t afford taxes. Sometimes they just refuse to be ruled. The kingdoms call these lesser villages.”
“There are countless small settlements like that,” he continued. “They rise and fall every day. Graylatch was one of them.”
He shifted the map slightly, pointing east of their location.
“Beyond the Verdant Ring, close to the Tillara border, a dense stretch of forest known locally as the Doom Forest rises. It was along the forest’s outer edge that the ambush took place.”
“So there are actually people living in the Unknown, building their own communities?” Gian asked, brows furrowed.
“Yeah. But most stay close to the kingdom’s reach. You can imagine how hard it is to create a truly safe place deeper in the Unknown.”
“I see…” Gian nodded, gazing at the map, wondering if Graylatch still stood or if it had already vanished into silence like so many before it. 
“Well,” Kel muttered as he leaned back on the bed, “we can talk to that private tomorrow. For now, I just want to rest.”
