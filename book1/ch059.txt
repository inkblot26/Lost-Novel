Chapter 59: The Cliffside Warning  
The group had been traveling for three days when, just as night fell, a sudden downpour crashed over them. Heavy rain soaked through their clothes within seconds as they took shelter beneath the trees, but the canopy offered little protection against the relentless rain. 
“Well, I’ll be damned, we’re getting drenched out here!” Kel complained, shaking his messy brown hair as they huddled under the half-assembled cover of their tents. 
“Damn those crafters, swearing Nightlily is perfect for outdoor travel. It’s useless when it rains like this,” Oldman grumbled.
Gian said nothing. The rain was falling so thick they could barely see beyond an arm’s length.
“Now that I think about it,” Kel muttered, “what was the point of transporting all that water from the Baobao Tree back to the village if it’s going to rain this heavily?”
“Well,” Gian said, trying to find some silver lining, “at least we know they’ve got plenty of clean and tasty water to drink.”
“You got a point there,” Kel sighed. “Anyway, how the hell are we supposed to eat? I can’t see anything past my own... scratch that, I can’t even see my hand.” He waved it in front of his face for emphasis.
Then Gian suddenly remembered something. “Wait… didn’t we get something from the crafters? Uh—hey, Oldman, isn’t this Flicks thing waterproof? The branch that lights up or whatever?”
“What?” Oldman glanced over, while Tata, soaked to the bone, shook himself off on top of Oldman’s head, sending droplets everywhere. 
“Hey! Stop that, Tata, you’re just making it worse!” Oldman scolded, grabbing the little driftmouse and holding him in his hand. 
“This,” Gian clarified, summoning the Flicks from his Void Ring. “The echotech branch thing. How do we use it?”
“Ohhh, right. I forgot we had those.” Oldman’s tone shifted as he realized their usefulness. “Just strike the tip against something solid. The ground works too. That’ll light it up.”
Following the instructions, Gian scraped the tip against a rock. The end flared softly, glowing like burning wood but giving off no heat.
“Whoa,” Kel said, his eyes lighting up. “Hey, gimme one!”
Gian handed him another, and soon Kel lit his own Flicks, the glow cutting through the sheets of rain, giving them some much-needed light.
Studying the faintly glowing branch in his hand, Gian asked, “So… how do we turn it off? It’s consumable, right?” He noticed it was already slightly shorter.
“Yeah,” Oldman replied. “If you leave it alone for a while, it'll dim on its own. But every time you use it, it shortens.”
“Anyway, how many days until we reach Lesser Village Altan?” Kel asked, pulling some food from his pack, rations they’d previously stored in Gian’s Void Ring.
Gian unfolded the map and traced the route with his finger. “From Graylatch, heading north along the Fallen Cliff, it should be about ten days of travel, give or take.”
“I’ve been there before,” Oldman said, leaning back as he recalled the place. “It’s usually the stopover for anyone wanting to climb the cliff, one of the few areas you can actually hike up without risking your neck. Wonder what it looks like now, huh, Tata?”
Sqk! Tata squeaked softly in agreement.
Kel bit into his food and spoke through a mouthful, “But based on the report, Altan was one of the other villages hit by the bandits.”
“Yeah,” Oldman said, taking out his share of food and tossing a small piece to Tata. “We’ll stop there first.”
“After that, we climb the cliff,” Gian added seriously, folding the map and slipping it back into his Void Ring. “Somewhere in the forest beyond, those bandits are hiding.”
“They’re definitely not what we’re really looking for,” Kel said, chewing thoughtfully. “But maybe there’s a tiny chance there’s a connection… like why that wildborn wore the same violet robes.”
“Even if there’s no connection, those bandits still deserve to die,” Gian said flatly.
“And if we take them out, we’ll be helping the villagers anyway,” Oldman replied.
“Yeah,” Kel mused, “and maybe the loot’s still with them.”
Oldman immediately shot him a glare. “It’s not yours. Don’t even think about it. We’ll return all the fangnotes to the villagers.”
“Hey, I’m not saying we won’t give it back,” Kel argued defensively.
Oldman just shook his head.
And with that, the group settled in, letting the long, damp night pass over them. The cold rain seeped through their clothes, clinging to their skin with every gust of wind.

After a few more days of travel, the group finally caught sight of Lesser Village Altan. A rough stakewall surrounded the settlement, but several sections had collapsed, with wooden posts splintered and scattered across the ground like broken bones. Some parts of the fence still stood, leaning at odd angles, while others lay completely shattered, leaving gaping wounds in the village’s defenses. 
“I guess that’s Altan,” Kel said, squinting at the damaged wooden walls ahead.
As they approached, a small group of men emerged from the village, walking toward them with cautious steps.
“Yes, this is it, though not in the state I remember,” Oldman muttered. He stepped forward, raising a hand in greeting. “Let me talk to them.”
The villagers stopped a short distance away, their leader—a muscular man with a sword at his side—watching the trio warily.
Oldman smiled disarmingly and waved. “Hey there. This is Altan Village, right?”
The man narrowed his eyes. “Yes. Who are you, and what’s your business here?”
“We’re just travelers,” Oldman replied in a friendly tone. He gestured to each of his companions. “You can call me Oldman, that’s Gian, and that grumpy one is Kel. And this little guy,” he poked the tiny Driftmouse perched on his shoulder, who batted his finger away, “is Tata, my partner.”
To ease their suspicion, Oldman pulled out a silver, feather-shaped coin and held it up. “I’m also with the Falcon Order.”
At the sight of the token, the villagers’ tense shoulders eased slightly, though their eyes still flickered with caution.
Kel leaned toward Gian and whispered, “Hey, what was that?”
Gian only shook his head, just as curious.
The muscular man repeated, “And what do you three want?”
“We’ve been traveling for days,” Oldman said calmly. “We’re looking for a place to rest. I’ve visited Altan before, back when it welcomed adventurers. After a short break, we plan to hike up the Fallen Cliff.” He pointed toward the trail leading up the Fallen Cliff just beyond the village. 
The man hesitated before replying. “I’m sorry, but we can’t let anyone in right now. The village is still on edge. We were attacked by bandits just a few weeks ago. I hope you understand.”
“That’s too bad…” Oldman scratched his chin. “Out of curiosity, do you know which bandit group it was?”
The men exchanged wary glances before their leader finally spoke. “Since you’re from the Falcon Order, I’ll tell you this much—don’t hike the Fallen Cliff. There’s… danger up there.”
Oldman raised an eyebrow. “Danger? What do you mean?”
“The bandits that raided us were under a man called Diego,” the villager explained. “It used to be a small group, but recently, they’ve been expanding—absorbing other bandit gangs.”
Kel interrupted, “So they’re a huge bandit army now?”
The man shook his head. “No. Strangely, Diego has been attacking lesser villages and rival bandits one after another, bleeding his own numbers in the process. A few days ago, we sent scouts to check on them. Only one returned. He said there was infighting among Diego’s men, and their numbers have shrunk even further.”
“Well, that’s a good thing, right?” Kel said lightly.
The villager stared at him coldly.
Oldman quickly chuckled. “Ignore him, he’s new.” Then he grew serious. “So why warn us against going up there?”
The man’s voice dropped. “Because the danger isn’t how many men Diego has. The danger… is Diego himself.”
Oldman’s smile faded. “What do you mean?”
The villager’s expression hardened. “We don’t know how, but he slipped into our village at night. He killed our chief and the elders silently, then opened the gates for his men. By the time we woke, it was already over. That’s how Altan fell.”
He might have disguised himself as a villager to infiltrate their ranks, Gian speculated.
Oldman’s voice softened. “We’re sorry for your loss.” He paused, then asked, “What happened after? Were you able to drive them out?”
The man shook his head, shame flickering in his eyes. “No. They looted everything from our hall, left us with nothing, and gave a warning—they’ll be back. We were leaderless, caught in our sleep. We couldn’t even fight.”
“I see…” Oldman sighed. “Thank you for telling us. We’ll respect your wishes. But can we at least camp outside the village walls?”
The villager nodded. “Yes, that’s fine. I’m sorry. The people are… afraid right now. I’ll leave you to set up camp.”
With that, the muscular man turned and led his group back toward the village gate.
“If they’re a small force now, we can just kill him,” Kel whispered to Oldman and Gian.
“It’s not that simple,” Oldman refuted, his tone firm. “That man was right, Diego himself is the real problem.” He turned, his gaze serious as it shifted between Gian and Kel. “Remember this—Ascendants are dangerous, but the ones that can speak are far worse.” His fingers brushed the old scar across his forehead. 
“We agreed to go after Diego for three reasons,” he continued, raising three fingers. “First, to confirm he has no connection to the people we’re really after, even if all signs suggest he doesn’t. Second, to kill him and stop him before he hurts anyone else. And third, if possible, to recover at least some of the stolen fangnotes for the villagers.” He fixed them both with a firm look. “But if any of us senses it’s too dangerous, we turn back. No questions asked.”
“But—” Kel started to argue, only to be cut off by Gian.
“Oldman’s right, Kel,” Gian said, his eyes burning with quiet hatred. “If we can’t finish it now, we’ll leave him alive… and come back to kill him later.”
Seeing them both agree, Oldman nodded. “Good. For now, it’s going to get dark in a couple of hours. Let’s set up camp.”
The group moved silently, preparing their campsite as the shadows of dusk crept closer.

After setting up camp, the group noticed a hunting party from Altan Village in the distance. They were bringing down a Crashstripe—a stocky, muscular Wildborn resembling a cow, its hide patterned with bold black-and-white stripes—in the open field. A few more Crashstripes grazed nearby, seemingly unaware of the hunters. 
“Hey, maybe we should hunt one for ourselves. It looks delicious,” Kel said, licking his lips as he watched the hunters work.
“I’m tired, kid. Go play by yourself,” Oldman replied lazily, fanning himself with his hat.
Kel turned to Gian hopefully, but Gian shook his head. “We’ll do that tomorrow. It’s getting dark soon, and we should conserve our stamina—just in case.”
Dejected, Kel glanced at Tata, who simply curled up tighter and closed its eyes, clearly uninterested.
“I’m bored…” Kel groaned as he flopped onto the ground. Then, suddenly sitting upright, he asked, “Hey, what was that feather-shaped coin you showed those guys earlier?”
Oldman reached into his pocket and pulled it out. The silver coin glinted faintly in the fading light. “You mean this?”
“Yeah,” Kel said, curiosity sparking in his eyes. Gian, sharpening his short sword nearby, glanced up as well, silently listening.
“This,” Oldman explained, holding the coin between his fingers, “is a Feather Coin. It’s the symbol of the Falcon Order.”
Seeing Kel’s confused but intrigued expression, he continued, “The Falcon Order is a renowned adventurers’ guild in the Kingdom of Aethelgard. I’m part of it. They take on all kinds of jobs—capturing criminals, gathering information, escorting merchants or nobles, hunting dangerous Wildborns, collecting Primeflora, you name it.”
“Wow. Maybe we should join, Gian,” Kel said excitedly.
Gian didn’t answer right away, which made Oldman lean forward. “I wasn’t keeping it a secret. After this, I was actually planning to suggest you two join.”
Kel grinned, while Gian raised an eyebrow, skeptical.
“Before you say no, let me explain,” Oldman added quickly. “Our main goal will always be the same—finding those men in violet robes. But to do that, we need information, resources, and connections. Joining the most famous guild in Aethelgard is the smartest way to gain all of that.”
Gian paused, thinking it over. “…I see. Through the guild, we can earn fangnotes, gather intel, and maybe even find clues about the Verdant Keepers. Or at least have access to information channels.” 
“Exactly,” Oldman nodded.
Kel turned to Gian with a huge smile. “Sooo…?”
“Fine,” Gian said at last, resuming his sharpening. “We’ll join after we finish here.”
“Yes!” Kel pumped his fist in the air, startling Tata awake. The little Driftmouse blinked in annoyance, squeaked once, and went right back to sleep.
