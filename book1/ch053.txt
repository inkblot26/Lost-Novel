Chapter 53: The Silent One
The group moved silently through the dark forest, slipping away in the opposite direction of the creeping mist. Jimmy led at the front of the formation, summoning three translucent rabbits that darted ahead along the forest floor, mere cannon fodder in case something lurked unseen.
At the center of the tight formation were Sergeant Thompson, Gian, Kel, and Oldman, with Tata perched on Oldman’s shoulder. The small driftmouse twitched its whiskers, sensing faint vibrations in the ground.
“Is it following us?” Kel whispered, glancing nervously over his shoulder.
“Tata isn’t sensing anything close,” Oldman murmured.
“If it really comes to it, we’ll kill it,” Thompson said quietly, his voice calm but edged with resolve.
Suddenly, a cold drop of water slipped through the canopy and splashed onto Kel’s head. 
“What the—?” He looked up just as more droplets began to fall.
“Damn, it’s gonna rain,” Jones muttered from the rear of the formation, walking with the three non-Echo soldiers between the trees. 
“We don’t know if that’s good or bad,” Thompson said as the drizzle thickened.
“Yeah… on one hand, the rain might weaken the Duskfang’s mist. On the other, it’ll cut our visibility, make it harder to spot—or fight—it. Especially at night,” Oldman added as the rain grew heavier, drumming on leaves and armor alike.

After trudging through the downpour for a while, they finally gathered beneath a massive tree with dark red leaves, its wide canopy offering partial cover from the rain. 
“It’s too dangerous to keep moving in this weather,” Thompson decided. “We’ll stop here for now and rest. With any luck, the Duskfang didn’t follow us.”
“Should I check it, sir?” Jimmy offered, stepping forward.
“No, it’s too risky to separate now,” Thompson said firmly. He pointed at Jimmy, Donny, and the two non-Echo soldiers. “You four, form a tight box perimeter around the tree. Stay close enough that we can still see you through the rain.”
“Yes, sir”
Squeak.
Oldman glanced at Tata, now tucked deep into his pocket. “Yeah, I know. Don’t worry, just stay there,” he murmured softly.
“What is it?” Gian asked curiously.
“Nothing serious,” Oldman replied. “Tata just isn’t good in the rain. Driftmouse can’t see well to begin with. They rely on heightened hearing, smell, and vibrations to sense their surroundings. But heavy rain messes with all of that.” 
“I see…” Gian muttered, his eyes scanning the dark forest around them as the downpour grew heavier. “So we really can’t rely on it to warn us if the Duskfang is nearby.”
Unbeknownst to the group, something stirred high in the canopy, creeping silently along the branches.
Then—plop.
Something dropped from the branches above onto the head of the non-Echo soldier holding a bow. 
“Ugh, damn this rain,” the soldier muttered, brushing at his hair. But his fingers snagged on something sticky—thick, stringy, and hard to pull free.
“Huh?” He froze, slowly looking up.
Through the dim light and heavy rain, countless Weblings crawled across the branches before lowering themselves on thick strands of glistening web. Each was about the size of a human hand, with eight wiry legs twitching beneath bulbous bodies as clusters of tiny eyes reflected the faint light. Within moments, they surrounded the tree, swaying silently in the rain. 
“W–Weblings!” the soldier shouted just as the first of the wildborn dropped onto him.
“Get away from the tree!” Thompson barked, his voice cutting through the chaos as more of the creatures dropped from the canopy overhead. 
“Help me!” the soldier screamed as a spider-like Wildborn wrapped his face in thick webbing.
A Webling dropped in front of Gian—he reacted instantly, slashing it in half with one clean strike.
“Don’t let them cover you with their web!” Oldman shouted over the rain. “It’s too tough to cut once it hardens. Aim directly at the Webling, their bodies are fragile!”
More of the creatures rained down.
“Ughh, my hand’s stuck!” Kel cursed, his Crimson Blood Axe already half-coated in sticky strands.
“Forget it—just move!” Oldman barked, yanking him away.
Nearby, Thompson spun his star-shaped water blades, slicing apart three Weblings that tried to leap at him. But when he saw the previous soldier overwhelmed, six Weblings cocooning him in a matter of seconds, he grabbed Jessica’s arm. 
“It’s too late for him! Go!” Thompson ordered, pulling her back as the man vanished under the writhing pile of webs.
Kel struggled to peel the webs off his axe. “A little help here—!”
But before anyone could reach him, Gian froze, staring upward. “This… this is bad.”
Oldman followed his gaze. From every surrounding tree, dozens more Weblings were descending, their webs glistening in the rain.
Gian’s voice dropped. “This is their nest. We need to move. Immediately.”
Thompson, Jessica, and Jones regrouped a few steps away from the tree as the forest floor around them began to crawl with more of the creatures. 
“I can’t see much in this rain,” Thompson muttered. “We have to—”
Then a scream ripped through the storm.
They turned. Through the rain and between the trees, one of the patrol soldiers was backing away in horror as a massive Duskfang slithered into view. Its hood flared open, marked by dark, swirling patterns, its scaled body stretching six meters long.
“It’s here!! The Duskfang!” the soldier shouted, stabbing at it desperately.
The strike missed. The serpent lunged, fangs flashing, and with one swift bite tore the man’s head clean off.
“Everyone, stay together!” Thompson roared over the rain. “We’re fighting it here!”
Oldman glanced at him, grim. “Are you sure? Running might—”
“No,” Thompson cut him off, spearing another falling Webling. “If we run in this heavy rain, we’ll scatter. That thing will pick us off one by one. Here, we make our stand!”
Weblings kept dropping from the branches above, their tiny mandibles clicking in a rapid, unsettling rhythm. 
“Some of us hold the Weblings back. The rest focus on the Duskfang!” Thompson commanded.
Donny, Jimmy, and Nathan—the last remaining non-Echo soldier—finally regrouped with the others, panting. 
“What now, sir?!” Donny shouted, breathing fire to clear a cluster of Weblings.
“Damn this rain. It’s dampening your flames! Fire’s the one thing that easily burns through Webling webs,” Thompson cursed, his eyes darting around.
He pointed sharply. “Jimmy, Jones, Nathan, on me! We kill that snake. Donny, Jessica, you keep the Weblings off us!”
Then he turned to Gian, Oldman, and Kel, who had finally freed his weapon from the sticky webs. 
“I need one of you with me for the Duskfang. The other two hold back the Weblings.”
Kel didn’t hesitate. “I’m with you, Sergeant.”
“Good. Gian, Oldman, hold the line here!” Thompson barked. 
“Got it,” Gian said, slashing down another Webling. “We’ll cover you.”
“Alright.” Thompson’s tone hardened as more spinning, star-shaped water formed around him. “We wait for the Duskfang to come to us… then we strike!”
After four grueling minutes of fending off the endless Weblings, Gian, Oldman, Donny, and Jessica held the outer perimeter, their breathing growing heavier with each strike. The constant clicking of mandibles above them mixed with the oppressive weight of the Duskfang’s gaze. It was coiled in the shadows, unmoving, yet its presence alone was suffocating.
Inside the box formation, Thompson, Jimmy, Jones, Nathan, and Kel stayed focused on the massive serpent. None of them dared blink.
“Ugh… what’s taking it so long?” Kel muttered, tightening and loosening his grip on the Crimson Blood Axe nervously.
“It’s waiting for the right moment to strike,” Thompson answered grimly, his eyes shifting toward the exhausted perimeter team. This isn’t sustainable… there’s no end to these Weblings. Sooner or later, they’ll break.
“What’s the plan, sir?” Jones asked, glancing between the falling Weblings and the unmoving serpent.
Thompson hesitated for only a moment. “We have no choice. It won’t come to us. If we wait, the Weblings will wear us down. We’re the ones who have to close the distance.”
Kel frowned. “But… why aren’t the Weblings attacking the Duskfang?”
“Because they’re terrified of it,” Jones replied flatly. “To them, it’s a predator far worse than us.”
“Then let’s end this!” Oldman shouted from the perimeter as he smashed another Webling into the mud.
“Jimmy!” Thompson barked. “Summon every Rabbit you can to clear a path. Everyone else, stay tight—don’t break formation when we move!”
“Yes, sir!” Jimmy gritted his teeth, summoning twelve translucent rabbits despite the strain.
“Go!”
The group surged forward through the wet forest ground, charging straight at the Duskfang between the trees. Gian and the others cut down Weblings along the way, the summoned rabbits scattering into the swarm to draw their attention. Any that slipped through were sliced apart by Thompson’s spinning star-shaped water. He didn’t bother conserving strength anymore.

When they finally reached the Duskfang, Gian and the others widened their formation to hold off the relentless Weblings. Jimmy, already drained from summoning so many rabbits, didn’t join the assault on the serpent. Instead, he stayed on the flanks, helping delay the Weblings that threatened to collapse their perimeter. Together, they formed a loose pentagon formation around the core team.
Inside that formation, Thompson unleashed five spinning, star-shaped blades of water. Two struck the Duskfang’s glistening black scales, carving shallow wounds that oozed thin streams of dark blood. The serpent hissed, its hood flaring wide, but it didn’t falter.
Jones lunged next, his Echo-transformed body covered in furred skin, and swung a powerful fist—but the Duskfang slid aside with terrifying grace. Its massive tail whipped around like a steel cable, catching Nathan mid-sprint.
“Ahh!”
The non-Echo soldier was sent flying, crashing into a tree outside the perimeter with a sickening thud.
Kel didn’t hesitate. He hurled his twin throwing axes, both embedding themselves into the serpent’s side. The wounds were shallow, barely enough to slow it down. 
“Don’t stop! Keep hitting it!” Thompson roared, thrusting his summoned translucent spear and finally piercing one of the serpent’s scales deep enough to make it reel back slightly.
Meanwhile, on the outer perimeter, Gian was drowning in enemies. He slashed down another Webling with his short sword, using his Morphguard in buckler form to deflect a spray of sticky silk. But two more crept in from his blind side. One managed to spit a web onto his right leg, anchoring him in place. 
“Tch—damn it!”
He killed both attackers with quick, precise cuts, then tried to yank his foot free. No use. More Weblings were closing in. Thinking fast, he stored his short sword in the Void Ring and switched to his black longbow, loosing an arrow into the nearest creature, then another into the next one crawling toward him.
I need to get this web off before they swarm me.
But the rain made it harder to aim. His arrows hit some targets, missed others, all while the webbing around his leg held firm.
To Gian’s right, Oldman was holding his ground better than anyone else. Stickk spun through Oldman's hands like a blur, smashing Weblings away in groups. Out of all the fighters on the perimeter, only Oldman could see the whole battlefield clearly through the chaos.
This isn’t good… Thompson and the others can’t finish that thing. The Duskfang’s too strong. Oldman thought, his eyes flicking between the serpent and the endless tide of Weblings. If we drag this out, we’ll all be crushed.
Then a piercing scream cut through the rain.
Jones, still in his furred skin form, staggered back as the Duskfang’s fangs clamped down, tearing one of his arms clean off  in a spray of blood. He howled in agony, stumbling helplessly.
“Jones!”
Thompson and Kel rushed in to cover him. Thompson seized the opening—his spear thrust forward with brutal precision, stabbing deep into the serpent’s left eye. The Duskfang screeched, jerking back violently. Without hesitation, Thompson followed up, sending two spinning, star-shaped blades of water crashing into its scaled body, slicing through flesh and drawing more dark blood.
The massive Wildborn recoiled, hissing furiously, its long body writhing as it backed away a few steps.
Kel dragged the wounded Jones toward safety. “Come on, stay with me!” he urged, but then froze as he noticed pale mist starting to seep from the serpent’s hood. The heavy downpour kept it thin, dispersing it before it could fully spread, but it was enough to feel the danger.
The Duskfang slowly rose higher, uncoiling its massive frame until it loomed over them. Even hunched slightly, its hood towered at least three meters above their heads, dark patterns swirling menacingly across its scales.
Kel swallowed hard, gripping his Crimson Blood Axe tighter. “Damn… this thing’s huge,” he muttered.
Beside him, Thompson was panting from exertion but refused to back down. He summoned three more spinning blades of water, their forms trembling slightly as his Echo strained under the relentless fight.
But then, out of nowhere, the sky thundered.
Two blazing streaks of yellow lightning came crashing down with deafening force—one spearing straight through the Duskfang’s massive body, the other slamming into its head. The impact was blinding, the serpent’s roar cut short as its colossal frame convulsed violently.
Black, tar-like blood splattered across the rain-soaked ground, hissing as it mixed with the water. The Duskfang writhed once, then fell limp, its body impaled and pinned to the earth, utterly lifeless beneath two faintly glowing crosses of lingering electricity.
