Chapter 62: The Tail’s Grip
Inside the forest, two figures darted between the trees—Kel chasing the fleeing bandit. 
“St-stop!” Kel shouted, breathless but determined. How is he this fast? He’s almost like Gian…
Thinking quickly, Kel bit his finger. Blood trickled down, and with a swift motion of his left hand, he willed the droplets forward, launching them toward the bandit.
The blood struck a tree just as the bandit dashed past it. Glancing back, Maro frowned. Persistent… but it looks like he’s alone, he thought, veering off the path.
Without warning, the bandit vanished into the trees.
Kel slowed down, eyes narrowing. He approached carefully with his Crimson Blood Axe ready. He circled around the tree the bandit had disappeared behind—but it was empty.
Suddenly, something heavy crashed down on him from above. Maro had leapt down, stamping Kel’s face into the ground. Before Kel could react, a powerful force whipped across his chest, flinging him backward into a tree. His axe slipped from his grasp.
Stunned, Kel barely saw the fist slamming into his face, followed by a hard punch to the gut. He doubled over in pain, then noticed something wrapping tightly around his neck. A thick, brown, hairy tail.
The bandit grinned up at him as Kel was lifted into the air, gasping.
“You’re done,” Maro sneered. “This tail? It’s my Echo. Stronger than chains. You’re not getting out.”
Kel clawed at the tail, struggling. His fingers were slick with blood, and that gave him an idea.
He concentrated. More blood flowed from his fingertips, and he drove it like needles into the thick tail.
“Agh! What the hell?!” Maro howled, but didn’t let go. Instead, he yanked Kel forward and landed another brutal punch to his stomach.
Kel screamed in pain, but refused to give in. He poured more blood into the Echo-bound tail, his blood droplets stabbing deeper. Finally, the tail recoiled, dropping him to the forest floor.
Wheezing, Kel landed hard. Maro staggered back, clutching his bleeding tail. With a snarl, he tackled Kel again. They crashed to the forest floor, fists flying as they wrestled.
The bandit managed to trap Kel’s left arm, coiling his tail around it. He pinned Kel beneath him and began pounding his face with a series of vicious blows.
“Let’s see you bleed your way out of this!” Maro growled, his face inches from Kel’s.
With no other choice, Kel bit into his lip until it bled. Blood pooled in his mouth.
Then, he spat—right into Maro’s face.
The bandit shrieked, rolling off and clutching his face. “Ahh!”
Kel didn’t waste the chance. Gritting his teeth, he pushed himself upright, blood running down his face and arms. With a flick of his hand, he summoned another wave of blood droplets and sent them tearing into the bandit’s side.
Then he rushed forward, yanked one of his short axes from the strap on his back, and slammed the blunt end into Maro’s head.
The forest fell quiet again as the bandit collapsed, unconscious.

After a short while, Tata arrived and found Kel slumped against a tree, the unconscious bandit nearby now tailless.
Tata let out a curious squeak as he circled the motionless figure. 
“He’s not dead, don’t worry,” Kel muttered.
“…For now,” he added under his breath.
Moments later, Gian and Oldman appeared, followed closely by a young blonde woman and another with short black hair streaked with green highlights wearing a bright, easy smile.
“Hi! You must be Kel,” the short-haired woman said cheerfully. “I’m El. We’re also after the bandits. It’s all a big misunderstanding. And, um… sorry about earlier.” She nodded toward Kel’s injured arm, where her ice arrow had struck not long ago.
Kel gave a small nod. “Don’t worry—it’s just a scratch.”
Then he glanced at the cold-looking blonde beside her, who was clearly uninterested in the conversation.
“This is my niece, Anni. Say hi, Anni,” El said.
Kel straightened and raised his hand for a shake, smiling. “Hi, Miss Anni! I’m Kel… the Great.”
Anni didn’t reply. She simply turned away to inspect the unconscious bandit.
“Sit down, kid. Let me heal you,” Oldman said as he lowered himself beside Kel. A cluster of yellow-green fireflies shimmered into view, floating toward Kel’s wounds and beginning to heal them with soft pulses of light.
Meanwhile, Gian summoned a rope from his Void Ring and began binding the bandit.
“Ooh, nice! You’ve got dimensional storage?” El said, clearly impressed.
“Psh. It’s just a basic relic. We have those too,” Anni muttered from the side.
“Let’s wait for Kel to recover,” Gian said once the bandit was secured. “Then we’ll head back to the bandits’ base. We’ll interrogate this one and decide what comes next.”
Everyone nodded in agreement.

Maro slowly opened his eyes. Realizing he wasn’t injured, he glanced around and saw he was back inside the cabin, now a mess with objects scattered all over the floor. As he tried to move, he noticed ropes pinning him to a wooden chair. Figures stood around him.
“Don’t even think about it,” Oldman warned, now without his brown gambler hat, an old scar exposed across his brow as he watched Maro sharply.
Beside him, Gian was sharpening a short sword. “Guys, he’s awake.”
Kel and El, who had been inspecting the house, stopped what they were doing and looked over. Anni, standing by the door, also turned.
“Good morning, sunshine,” El purred darkly, walking toward him as an icy longsword materialized in her hand, its blade dragging along the floor and leaving frost in its wake.
“Who are you? What do you want?” Maro barked.
Without warning, an arrow hit his chair.
“We’re the ones asking questions,” Anni said coldly, stepping in from the doorway holding a bow.
“Someone needs to stay on lookout, my lovely niece,” El muttered, glancing at her.
Before Anni could protest, Oldman cut in. “It’s fine. My partner’s outside watching the perimeter. He’ll warn me if anything’s wrong.”
El nodded. “All right then.”
Gian stepped forward. “First question — and don’t lie. What’s your group’s goal?”
“How should I know? I’m just a pawn,” Maro snapped.
Oldman crouched, drew a knife from his boot, and drove it into Maro’s stomach.
“Hey, don’t kill him yet!” Anni yelled.
Maro screamed, thrashing in his ropes. “What are you doing?! I said I don’t know. Please!” 
“Seems you’re useless to us, then,” Oldman replied flatly. He stabbed Maro’s thigh and slowly dragged the blade down to the knee.
Blood poured down the chair. Maro wailed in agony. Even Anni had to look away while El stayed calm.
“Please! Stop!” Maro sobbed.
Oldman leaned close. “Now… can you tell me what we want?”
Gian and Kel watched without blinking, while El eyed them curiously, intrigued by their lack of mercy. 
“I swear I don’t know!” Maro begged.
Oldman smiled wickedly and placed his hands over the stomach wound. Yellow-green fireflies drifted from his palms into the flesh, closing it before Maro’s eyes.
Gian nonchalantly went back to sharpening his sword. Kel stood and wandered through the mess of the cabin, whistling.
“That’s it?” Anni whispered, bewildered.
El smirked, picking up a tattered book from the ground. “I see. You three are ruthless.”
“What do you mean?” Anni murmured as she crouched beside her.
“They’ll torture him… heal him… and repeat it until he talks,” El said, loud enough for Maro to hear.
Terror washed over the bandit. These people were scarier than any he had met.
“Okay! I’ll talk!” Maro cried. “Please… just don’t do that.”
Everyone turned toward him silently.
“I’m Maro. My sister Belle leads another bandit crew that joined Diego, along with Gromo and Kelly. Diego’s recruiting strong and useful bandit leaders to fulfill his plan.” 
“And that is?” Kel asked, eyes narrowed.
“He wants to become a king… or at least rule a city,” Maro answered. Shock rippled across the room.
“Has he lost his mind?” Kel barked, but Oldman cut him off.
“How does he aim to do that, and where do you fit in?”
“Diego wants more than money. He wants noble status. He promised us we’d become a recognized clan when he succeeds, rich and powerful once we control a city. Using his Echo—he can change his face—we’ll overthrow an outpost first. During a ‘bandit attack,’ we’ll kill the captain. Diego will disguise himself as that man while we hide. Later, he’ll slowly bring us back in as ‘mercenaries.’ Once we own that outpost, we’ll do the same to a city. Then he’ll grant us clan names and titles.” 
“And you’re dumb enough to believe that?” Oldman scoffed.
“Of course not. Most of us will die, but the strong survive and end up nobles. Diego needs manpower, we need a future. We’ll use each other. Better than dying nameless as bandits.” 
“Why not just join the kingdom legally?” Kel asked.
“Easy to say,” El interjected. “Outsiders need identification. Even adventurers need proof to enter a kingdom.”
“Right,” Oldman added, showing an emerald-marked token. “Remember these  which are given to us each by Highlady Rose? This is a special crest of Tillara City. This proves you’re recognized. You can cross borders with it. That’s why people join famous guilds like Falcon Order for identification. Getting out is easy but getting in is different.”
“Of course they could also sneak in,” El added, “but I’m thinking these guys want to establish themselves permanently in a city, so they’ll need status—not just look like ordinary travelers.” 
“Exactly. My sister and I want to settle in a city permanently, but we can’t. We’re wanted. If Diego becomes an outpost captain, he’ll ‘rewrite’ our status as mercenaries. With money and influence, things can be… arranged. Then we’ll move to Tillara, infiltrate, and kill the ruler so Diego can take control. For now, Diego’s gathering money and scouting bandit crews. Those who join—great. Those who don’t—he kills and takes over, using them to grow stronger. Once he has enough men and coin, he’ll strike an outpost.”
“It’s a massive plan,” Kel said, stunned.
“And incredibly foolish,” Oldman muttered.
“We know. That’s why Diego’s first goal is just an Outpost,” Maro replied.
“So right now,” Anni asked, “Diego, your sister, and two other leaders are scouting?”
“Yes. Diego never strikes blindly. He studies every detail. That’s why they follow him. They’ll be back tomorrow evening.”
“What do you actually know about Diego himself?” Gian said, staring into Maro’s eyes.
“Not much. Used to be a minor bandit boss, nothing legendary. But when he came to my sister… he was different. Cunning. Dangerous. His group is already becoming something else.”
Gian summoned a purple robe from his Void Ring. “Then what’s with these?”
“Our uniform. Diego insists we wear them. Says it makes us feel like a real army.” 
“Damn,” Oldman spat. “No connection to what we’re actually looking for.”
“Yeah, and Private Joey’s an idiot,” Kel growled. “There’s a huge difference between purple and violet. Is he color-blind?” 
Anni and El exchanged glances.
“Well,” Gian sighed, “at least we know Diego’s a threat to Tillara.”
“Now,” Oldman leaned in, eyes hard, “tell us everyone’s Echo in Diego’s group.”
