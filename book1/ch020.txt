Chapter 20: Fire in the Eyes
They pushed through the underbrush until a flicker of firelight bled through the trees, fragile and golden against the suffocating dark. Ahead, Edmund and Cedric stood beside a small torch, waving them over.
Kel immediately dropped to one knee, grimacing as his injured leg buckled beneath him. He masked the stumble with a dramatic flourish, chin raised like a knight before his lady.
“It gladdens me to see you safe, my lady,” he declared. 
Clara blinked, then smiled faintly. “Thank you.”
Her eyes swept over him. “Are you hurt?” 
“Nothing worth troubling yourself over, my lady,” Kel said proudly, puffing his chest.
Gian shot him a sideways look that all but said really? before turning to Edmund.
“Did you hear any wildborn nearby, sir?”
Edmund shook his head. “Not a sound. But once you’ve caught your breath, I’ll need you to scout the area. Just in case.”
“Understood,” Gian replied, already stepping away.
“You should rest first,” Clara said firmly, stopping Gian. 
Kel straightened, striking another stiff, knightly pose. “Your concern honors us, my lady,” he said, ignoring the blood trickling down his leg.

After a few minutes of rest by the crackling fire, Edmund leaned forward curiously. “How did you find us?”
“Yeah, how’d you even know they were here? You run like hell,” Kel added, gulping water from Edmund’s jug before shooting Gian a sideways look.
“I tracked them,” Gian said. “Looked for signs as we ran. We didn’t come straight here. We had to take a few detours to follow the clues.”
Kel arched an eyebrow. “How? What are you, a hound?” 
“Broken twigs, disturbed ground, direction of footprints… basic things. I learned it from past work,” Gian replied casually.
“What kind of work?” Edmund asked, intrigued. 
“I’ve tracked people, hunted animals, even recovered lost pets. All sorts of things.”
Clara, who had been listening quietly, suddenly turned to Gian and Kel. “So why accept this escort mission?”  
“I wanted women and money,” Kel declared without hesitation. 
“I want money too!” Cedric piped up cheerfully, pulling a silver coin from his pouch and flashing it with a grin. 
Kel leaned toward Clara with seriousness. “But if you’ll have me as your personal guard, my lady, I might consider settling down.” He flashed a grin that wobbled awkwardly somewhere between charming and ridiculous.
Clara and Edmund burst into laughter at his expression. Even Cedric chuckled. 
“What a fine expression you make, Kel,” the guard teased.
Then, for the first time in days, Gian’s lips curved into the faintest of smiles. 
Kel froze. “Wait—did you just smile? That was a smile, right?” He whirled to the others, wide-eyed.
“I think he did,” Clara smiled.
“I saw it too!” Cedric said enthusiastically, raising his hand. 
“Even if it was just for a heartbeat,” the guard added with a grin.
Kel threw his arms up dramatically. “At last! Days of blood, sweat, and Bloomfangs… and this is the victory I’ve been waiting for!”
Clara’s gaze shifted toward Gian. “So… how about you? Why did you leave your town?”
For a moment, time seemed to stall. Gian didn’t answer right away; his eyes stayed fixed on the flames, their glow flickering across his face. 
When he finally spoke, his voice was low, steady, almost swallowed by the crackle of the fire.
“I need to kill some men.”
Silence followed. Even the night seemed to recoil, the mist thickening at the edge of the firelight as the wood popped sharply, sending a brief shower of sparks into the dark. 
“…Mind if I tag along?” Kel said at last. His grin appeared, but it didn’t quite reach his eyes this time. 
“Why?” Gian asked, sensing a shift in Kel’s tone.
Kel gave a half-shrug, standing a little straighter than usual. “Someone’s gotta watch your back, kid. Otherwise you’ll do something stupid.”
His tone was playful, but beneath it lay something unshakable, like steel hidden beneath cloth. 
“What are you, his father now?” Edmund quipped, smirking as if to cut through the heaviness.
“Not a father,” Kel shot back quickly. “More like a big brother. I’m clearly older than him.”
“So how old are you, ‘Big Brother’?” Clara asked, her smile returning with a teasing glint.
“Twenty,” Kel announced proudly. Then, with a flourish: “But you don’t need to call me that, my lady. Just Kel—or Kelly, if you prefer. We’re basically the same age anyway.” 
“FYI, Big Brother Kel,” Clara said with a sly smile, “I’m only seventeen.”
Her smile faded slightly, and the mood shifted.
“Tch. Still too young, Kel. You don’t understand women,” Edmund muttered under his breath. 
The guard cleared his throat, his voice grounding them. “Enough talk. It’s late. We’ll need rest for tomorrow.”
“We’ll rotate for night watch,” he added firmly. 
“I’ll take first shift,” Gian said quickly, grateful for the change in subject. 
“But I don’t wanna sleep,” Cedric whined.
“Shh, Ced. You heard Edmund. Let’s try to get some rest,” Clara said gently, urging her brother to lie down. 
Kel blinked at the group, his earlier bravado deflating. “…Wait, what just happened?” he muttered, still trying to piece together the shift in mood.

By the next morning, Gian returned from patrol with a pack slung over his shoulder. When he set it down, it revealed strips of jerky and a few jugs of clean water. 
"Found an abandoned stash in the forest," he said simply. 
No one asked for details. Hunger outweighed curiosity. They tore into the food with quiet gratitude, Cedric especially, chewing eagerly as if he hadn’t eaten in days. Clara’s smile softened at the sight of her brother eating his fill.
Kel, meanwhile, kept sneaking glances at the dark band around Gian’s finger. His grin faltered for a moment, but he stayed silent.
When the meal was done, they set out again. The forest pressed in on all sides, damp leaves whispering underfoot. With Edmund’s Echo of Perception guiding them, they moved carefully, adjusting their pace at the faintest sound of distant movement.
By noon, they had avoided every patrol of Bloomfangs without a single clash. Each step was quiet, each breath held until the danger passed.

On the eighth day, as they trudged through the forest, Edmund suddenly halted and raised a hand for silence. 
“I can hear footsteps. Two sets—west,” he murmured, pointing through the trees. 
Cedric’s eyes lit up. “What are we waiting for? Let’s go! Maybe it’s Father and Mother!”
“Kel. Check it out. Keep low, stay hidden,” Edmund ordered.
“Yes, sir,” Kel replied, crouching as he slipped into the undergrowth.
Clara frowned, but before she could speak, Gian leaned closer and whispered, “It could be bandits.” 
Edmund nodded grimly. “Wildborn aren’t the only danger. Bandits prey on travelers too. They might’ve even left that food behind.” His eyes flicked toward Gian’s pack. 
Gian’s hand instinctively brushed against his Void Ring. “Understood, sir.”
The silence stretched, thick with tension. Minutes dragged by until Edmund’s shoulders eased. Gian followed his gaze and finally saw Kel returning, flanked by Michael, with Baldwin close behind, David slung across his back. 
“Lady Clara, young Lord Cedric—thank the stars you’re safe,” Michael said as he reached them, his voice rough with relief. He gave Edmund a respectful nod. “Good job keeping them safe.”
Baldwin lowered David carefully to the ground. His arms trembled from the effort, and when David’s body touched the earth, Baldwin nearly collapsed beside him.
Makeshift bandages bound David’s chest and back, already dark and stiff with dried blood. Flies buzzed faintly despite the shade, drawn to the scent beneath the scorched cloth. His breaths were shallow, ragged. 
Gian dropped to his knees beside him. “David…”
“You alright, kid?” Baldwin asked between heavy breaths, wiping sweat from his brow.
“I’m fine,” Gian said quickly, already digging through his pack for clean cloth and fresh bandages. Kel knelt beside him without a word, quietly helping him prepare the supplies.
“What happened to him?” Edmund asked, crouching low, eyes sharp with concern.
Michael exhaled slowly, taking a sip from a water jug. “A Bloomfang. It got him in the back when we were fighting to escape that night.”
“We stopped the bleeding by burning it,” Baldwin added hoarsely. “But he’s lost too much blood.”
Clara moved swiftly, pulling Cedric into her arms and turning his head away, but the boy had already seen. David’s bandages were soaked through, and beneath them, glimpses of scorched flesh and torn bite marks showed. 
“Will he survive?” Cedric asked in a trembling voice, still trying to peer past her.
“Cedric!” Clara scolded, shielding him more firmly.
Edmund stood, his jaw set. “We can’t stay here. Once he’s stable enough to move, we press on. The others might already be searching for us.”
Clara nodded. “Yes. We need to find them.”
After a short rest, the seven gathered themselves. They lifted David carefully, taking turns bearing his weight. No one complained. Their pace was slow, steady—every step heavy with resolve.
At last, the undergrowth thinned. Beyond the trees stretched a dirt road.
The road to Tillara.
