Chapter 55: Blood and Water
Days passed. Sometimes they rerouted to avoid territorial Wildborns, but other times they had no choice but to fight. By the thirteenth day since parting with Thompson’s squad, their supplies were running dangerously low.
“Hey, are you sure we’re still on the right track?” Kel asked, nibbling on the last of his biscuits for breakfast.
“Don’t blame me,” Oldman replied, frustration creeping into his voice. “We may have a map, but all we see are these same damn tall trees—red, yellow, green—it all looks the same.”
“Maybe climb up one and look from above?” Gian suggested, trying to ease the tension.
“No use. Even with poor eyesight, Tata already checked. It’s just more trees and sky, with some avian Wildborns,” Oldman explained, tossing the Direstone to the ground.
“Maybe we shouldn’t have avoided all those Wildborns,” Kel muttered, turning the map around, trying to make sense of it.
When the Direstone settled, it revealed three possible directions. Gian stared east and west. “We must avoid unnecessary fights if we can, Kel.”
“Gian’s right,” Oldman added. “We won’t stay unscratched if we keep engaging. Anyway, I still think we should head east until we find the Fallencliff. Graylatch should be near there.”
“Then we trace along the cliff?” Kel asked.
“Yeah,” Oldman said, stretching his back. “But the next problem is water. Food we can manage by hunting Wildborns, but water…”
Gian nodded. “We have about seven, maybe eight days’ worth at most. Our original plan was to refill our jugs from the village well once we reached Graylatch.”
“Then let’s hope we find it fast,” Kel muttered as they continued walking.
Up ahead, they spotted a slender Wildborn resembling a fox—long legs, sleek orange fur, pointed ears.
“Hey, isn’t that a Wox? Should we kill it? Maybe its meat is good,” Kel grinned, peeking from behind a tree.
“I’ve tasted it before. It’s good,” Oldman replied. “But you’re forgetting something—they move in packs, and they’re territorial.” He scanned deeper into the trees. “Gian, give me the map. This might actually help us.”
Realizing what he meant, Gian summoned the military map and found an area marked as Wox territory. “Just a little east from here is the Fallencliff. Then a little north is Graylatch Village.”
Then he noticed a small note scribbled on the map. His expression hardened. “We need to get out of this territory. We can circle around to reach Fallencliff safely.”
“There’s an Alpha Wox leading the pack here. Definitely an Ascendant Wildborn. We must avoid it,” Gian added, leading the trio carefully while Tata remained alert for any movement.

After a few hours of walking Tata suddenly squeaked cheerfully, hopping up and down as the trio walked behind it.
“Why is he so energetic?” Kel asked, glancing at Oldman in confusion.
Oldman chuckled, his tone mysterious. “You’ll see in a while.”
Before long, the trees began to thin, with more open sky visible ahead. They stepped out of the dense forest and into a vast, open field. Beyond it loomed the tallest rocky cliff Gian had ever seen. Its top disappeared into a shroud of mist, the sunlight dimmed by the haze.
“Is that… the Fallencliff?” Gian asked in awe, craning his neck. He turned left, then right, trying to take in the sheer length of the cliff face.
“You’re telling me this is still inside the Doom Forest!?” Kel exclaimed, eyes wide as he looked around.
“Yeah,” Oldman nodded. “It’s a long cliff. If you head toward the north end, you’ll be closer to Aethelgard Kingdom than our own Lanthea. But if you trace it all the way south, you’ll reach the edge of the Gobe Desert, in the southern part of the Great Continent.” His voice grew nostalgic as he added, “There are multiple lesser villages along this cliff, like Graylatch, just ahead to the north.” He unfolded the military map, showing Gian as they compared landmarks.
Kel suddenly squinted, raising a hand to shield his eyes. “Hey… what’s that tree? It’s far, but… it looks weird. Like an upside-down tree or something.”
“Wha—What?” Oldman paused, then stared. His eyes widened. “Don’t tell me… haha!” A grin spread across his face. “We’re really getting lucky today.”
“What? Why? What is it?” Kel looked at him, confused, while Gian mirrored the same puzzled expression.
“Come on, let’s get closer. If I’m right… that’s a Baobao Tree—a Primeflora,” Oldman said, his grin widening. “Of course, all Primeflora are special plants with unique traits, and it just so happens that this one is exactly what we desperately need right now.” 
“You mean… water?” Gian asked as they walked toward the strange-looking tree in the distance.
“Exactly,” Oldman nodded. “It’s a massive giant tree with a thick, swollen trunk covered in smooth gray bark. And what makes me happiest? It stores vast amounts of water within its trunk. If we cut it, clear water will flow freely until the wound slowly seals itself as the tree repairs the damage.”
“Wow! What are we waiting for? Let’s run!” Kel shouted, excitement bubbling over as he and Tata rushed ahead.
“Hey, it’s not going anywhere. It’s not a Walking Rhiza Tree!” Oldman laughed, shaking his head as he and Gian continued at a steady pace. 
“Is this stretch of cliff always this high?” Gian asked curiously.
Walking side by side with him, Oldman shook his head. “No. There are spots where the cliff is shorter. That’s where some expeditions pass through to dive deeper into the Unknown, since going around is a hass—”
Before he could finish, a loud screech ripped through the air.
A Scorchbleak wildborn, its scorched black-red feathers shimmering with heat and a faintly glowing hooked beak, suddenly dove straight toward Kel and Tata.
“Tata! Kel! Get down!” Oldman yelled.
Kel reacted just in time, throwing himself low. The Scorchbleak missed him by inches, flapping upward into the sky with a blast of hot wind.
Gian looked up and froze—three more joined it, a small flock of four now circling ominously above.
“This is bad! Run for the trees!” Gian shouted, pointing back toward the shaded cover at the edge of the clearing.
The Scorchbleaks let out a shrill cry in unison before diving again, unleashing it’s acidic saliva toward their direction.
Gian raised his left arm. The Morphguard attached to it shifted and expanded, forming into a sturdy kite shield. The corrosive substance splashed against it with a hiss, protecting him and Oldman from the deadly spray.
Seeing the attack focused on them, Kel gritted his teeth and pulled two short throwing axes from his back, backup weapons that had been stored in Gian’s Void Ring. He hurled them with precision, but the flock was still too high, and both blades fell uselessly back to the ground. 
“Damn it, they’re too far!” Kel cursed. He glanced at the little driftmouse at his side. “Tata, go! Hide! You’re faster than me!”
The creature squeaked, hesitating for a heartbeat, glancing between Kel, Oldman, and Gian, who were struggling under the aerial assault. Panic flickered in its tiny eyes before it darted off—
—but it ran toward the cliff wall.
“Hey! Not that way!” Kel shouted after it.
While running, Tata squeaked noisily, drawing the attention of two Scorchbleaks. The creatures let out sharp cries and veered off to chase the small Driftmouse.
With fewer attackers overhead, Oldman and Gian finally had a chance to recover.
Oldman stepped out from cover and thrust the Stickk forward—its tip suddenly extending with a sharp snap. It struck one of the vulture-like Wildborn squarely, the impact jolting its body and forcing it to back off with an angry screech.
The second Scorchbleak was momentarily distracted, giving Gian his opening. He shifted the Morphguard into a compact buckler form, then swiftly drew his black longbow from his back. With a smooth motion, he nocked an arrow and fired.
The shot flew true, piercing the creature’s left wing. It let out a guttural growl of pain, flailing in the air as it tried to steady itself.
Oldman didn’t give it the chance. Gripping the extended Stickk tightly with both hands, he wrestled with its unwieldy length before swinging it in a wide arc. The blow connected with a loud crack, slamming the injured Wildborn to the ground.
Gian loosed another arrow at the remaining flying Scorchbleak, but it dodged to the side and retaliated with a spit of acidic saliva.
With no time to switch the Morphguard’s mode, Gian grabbed Oldman and yanked him aside. The acid splashed onto the ground where they had just stood, sizzling and smoking as it burned through the earth.
“Oldman, go help them! I’ll distract this one!” Gian shouted, already nocking another arrow. He released it—missed again.
Oldman glanced toward the fallen Scorchbleak and then to Kel, who was sprinting toward Tata as two more Scorchbleaks pursued the little creature. “Don’t get separated from us!” Oldman yelled back toward Gian before breaking into a run after Tata and Kel.
“Go!” Gian called after him.
He turned his attention to the fallen Scorchbleak that was struggling to stand. At the same time, another one circled above, spitting acid down at him. Gian fired an arrow upward, forcing it to veer off, then shifted his aim. Instead of shooting the flying one, he targeted the grounded Wildborn.
His next arrow struck its right eye, eliciting a guttural shriek. A follow-up shot pierced into its open mouth before it could screech again.
Closing the distance, Gian dismissed the black longbow into his Void Ring and drew his short sword. He ducked under a desperate swipe of the creature’s razor-sharp claws, then drove the blade into its neck. The Scorchbleak convulsed before collapsing lifelessly.
But there was no time to breathe—another Scorchbleak dove at him from above, its hooked beak narrowly missing his head as he rolled to the side.
Panting, Gian pushed himself back to his feet, sheathing his short sword in one fluid motion. With a thought, the black longbow materialized back in front of him as he prepared for the next strike.

At Kel’s side, he kept firing droplets of blood toward the two Scorchbleaks, which relentlessly spat acidic saliva at Tata, leaving small, sizzling burns scorched into the ground.
Damn… my blood droplets aren’t strong enough to reach them. He gritted his teeth. I’ll have to use the Crescent Blood. But I can only manage it two, maybe three times a day before I lose consciousness—and then it gets dangerous.
Kel sliced a little deeper into his palm, feeling a hot sting as more blood welled up. He shaped it into a sharp crescent blade of blood.
“Just hang on a little longer, Tata!” Kel shouted. He hurled the Crescent Blood at the nearest Scorchbleak. The Wildborn sensed the attack and swerved aside, letting it whistle past harmlessly.
Not so fast, Kel thought. The Crescent Blood curved back like a boomerang, striking the Scorchbleak from behind. The sharp blade of blood buried itself deep in its body, and with a strangled screech, the creature crashed to the ground.
Kel didn’t stop to check it. He kept running toward the remaining one.
Oldman was just a few steps behind him. “Kel! Don’t use that again! You’ll suffer severe blood loss if you release another one!” he shouted.
Breathing hard, Kel felt his vision blur for a moment—lightheadedness creeping in. He glanced back at Oldman. “Where’s Gian?”
“He’s dealing with the other one. Don’t worry, we just need to finish this one fast and get back to him!” Oldman replied, eyes fixed ahead.
He gave a sharp whistle to Tata, who was still darting frantically across the ground. The little Driftmouse responded, veering into a wide arc to lure the remaining Scorchbleak back toward them.
“Tata will bring it right to us. Get ready,” Oldman said, drawing two small daggers from his boots.
“I can hit it with my Crescent Blood,” Kel argued.
“No! Stop the bleeding and wrap it with cloth. We can’t have a repeat of what happened during your training,” Oldman snapped.
Up ahead, Tata sprinted back toward them, the Scorchbleak hot on its tail.
As the creature swooped low after Tata, Oldman hurled both daggers into the air, then swung the Stickk like a bat. The daggers spun forward at high speed—one struck the Scorchbleak’s body with its handle, glancing off, while the other found its mark and stabbed deep into its flesh.
The Wildborn faltered midair, struggling to keep balance.
Seizing the opening, Kel hurled his Crimson Blood Axe cleaving into the Scorchbleak’s body. With a final screech, it collapsed to the ground, lifeless.
Without pausing to rest, Oldman sprinted back in Gian’s direction. As he passed Kel, he pointed at the fallen Scorchbleak.
“Make sure that one’s dead!” he ordered.
Kel nodded and ran toward the creature with Tata at his side. It was still weakly struggling, its wings twitching. Kel drew his axe and hacked down repeatedly until the Wildborn finally stopped moving.
Meanwhile, Tata picked up one of Oldman’s daggers in its tiny mouth and scurried after Oldman to return it.
Kel turned next to the wailing Scorchbleak he had wounded earlier with the Crescent Blood, tightening his grip on the axe as he moved in to finish it off.

When Oldman returned, he saw Gian running and loosing arrows at a Scorchbleak, the Wildborn retaliating with spit of acidic saliva. The two were locked in a stalemate—neither landing a decisive hit.
The Scorchbleak faltered when it noticed Oldman’s arrival. With a screech, it turned sharply, attempting to flee.
Seizing the chance, Gian planted his feet, inhaled deeply, and steadied his aim. He released an arrow that struck the creature’s wing, sending it lurching in the air. Without wasting a second, he pulled another arrow from his quiver, drew, and fired again. The shot hit, but the Scorchbleak, though wounded and struggling, still managed to climb higher and finally disappear into the sky.
Oldman jogged up to him. “You okay?” he asked, scanning Gian for injuries.
Lowering the black longbow, Gian nodded. “Yeah. How are Kel and Tata?”
Before Oldman could answer, they heard an odd squeak. Tata came scampering toward them, a dagger clutched in its tiny mouth by the handle.
“Thanks, Tata,” Oldman said with a small smile, patting the little Driftmouse as he retrieved the dagger. He slid it back into the sheath in his left boot.
Together, they started walking back toward Kel.
Gian handed Kel the two short throwing axes they had retrieved while walking toward him.
“Thanks,” Kel said, sheathing them onto his back.
“Stop losing those axes, Kel,” Oldman commented from the side as the trio made their way toward the Baobao Tree, Tata perched on his shoulder. “You only bought ten of them, and you already lost two the night we fought the Duskfang.”
“Yeah, yeah… oh, and look what I found,” Kel replied with a grin, holding up one of Oldman’s daggers.
“Shut up,” Oldman muttered, snatching the dagger back. He slid it into the sheath on his right boot.
Finally, they reached the massive Baobao Tree. Its thick, swollen trunk was covered in smooth gray bark, and its short, heavy branches spread outward near the top, giving it the strange appearance of an upside-down tree.
“Finally! No more worrying about water,” Kel said with relief, hurrying to the trunk. He made a small cut along the bark, and clear, cool water immediately began to flow. Without hesitation, he pressed his mouth to the stream, drinking deeply until his thirst was satisfied.
