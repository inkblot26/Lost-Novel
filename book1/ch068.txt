Chapter 68: The Pinky Vow
“So that’s what happened. I’m sorry for your loss,” Kel said to El and Anni.
Gian stayed silent, his fists tightening at his sides as he thought of how many tragedies the Verdant Keepers had already caused. 
“Since that day,” El continued quietly, “I stayed with Anni. I trained her every day, because I was afraid those people would come back for her someday.”
“What do you mean?” Kel asked.
“Auntie El believes those violet-robed men attacked our clan because of our Echo… that’s the only explanation that makes sense. That’s also why she forbade me from using mine,” Anni explained.
“Wait… if that’s true, then what exactly are they after?” Oldman muttered, frowning as he tried to think of an answer.
Kel then explained to El and Anni about the attacks carried out by the violet-robed men—first on Tala Town a few years ago, and later on Ronald Rosendale’s mansion a year ago.
“Based on what we know so far,” El said, “the attack on Ronald Rosendale’s mansion happened because his assistant, Jonas, studied forbidden history. That led him to discover the Verdant Keepers—who probably wanted to remain hidden from society. As for the attack on our village… I believe it was because of our Echo.”
She paused, then looked at Kel and Gian. “But what about your town?”
Kel shrugged, shaking his head. “No idea. And there was no investigation either.”
Then Gian spoke up. “What if… it’s because of Rare Echoes?”
The group turned toward him. Oldman narrowed his eyes. “What do you mean?”
“A year before Tala was attacked, a patrolman in our village got a Rare Echo—the Echo of Duplicate,” Gian explained.
“What? There’s an Echo like that?” Oldman gasped.
Kel frowned, thinking hard. “Wait… now that you mention it, I think I heard about that too. Only briefly.”
“You’re right,” Gian continued. “After that day, when he returned from patrol in the Gobe Desert, my brother told me Warren was suddenly transferred to the mayor’s personal guard.”
“So they’re hunting everyone who has Rare Echoes?” Kel asked.
“Maybe… but I feel like there’s something more. Remember, there are other Rare Echo users out there. Why aren’t they hunting them, then?” Oldman muttered.
Silence fell over the group as they mulled it over.
“Well… at least now we have a lead,” Gian said.
“You’re right,” Oldman agreed.
“So our next step,” Kel added, “is to keep searching for Rare Echo users.”
“Yeah…” Gian replied, though deep inside, his thoughts drifted to the Rare Echo he refused to acknowledge—the Echo of Fate.
“Will you accept me as part of your group?” Anni asked.
Before anyone could answer, El immediately cut in.
“Anni… no.” 
“Please, Auntie El.” Anni’s voice trembled but her gaze was firm. “I want to avenge our clan. I know you want the same, but you’re afraid something might happen to me.”
El stayed silent for a long moment, torn. Finally, she exhaled.
“Fine. But promise me you’ll obey my orders.”
Anni nodded eagerly.
“If I tell you to fight, you’ll fight. If I tell you to act as bait, you’ll do it. Understand?” El’s tone was sharp.
“I… guess. Yeah, sure,” Anni muttered.
“And if I tell you to run, you run. No hesitation.” El’s eyes narrowed.
“Wait, I’m not going to abandon you!” Anni protested.
“Promise me,” El pressed, her piercing blue eyes locking onto hers.
Anni hesitated, then sighed. “Okay… I promise.”
El raised her hand, pinky finger extended.
Anni blinked. “What’s that?”
“It’s called a pinky swear. Something I read in the book I found. You hook your pinky with mine, then our thumbs touch. It seals the promise—and it must never be broken.”
Slowly, Anni lifted her right hand, curling her pinky around El’s.
“You promise to obey my orders, no matter what happens?” El asked quietly.
“Yes,” Anni whispered.
Their thumbs pressed together, sealing the vow in a quiet, unbreakable gesture.
The group rested for the night, and by the following day, El led them to the nearest lesser village, Takamine. There, she explained to the villagers that Diego had been slain. The news brought relief and joy, and the people insisted that Gian and the others remain for a while to rest and recover.
During their stay, Gian wrote a letter to Granny Emma, recounting the incident. He assured her there was no longer any need to fear the bandit Diego, though he apologized for the loss of the Fangnotes.
A few days later, a reply arrived. Granny Emma thanked them warmly, sharing that all was well in Graylatch village—the well had even been repaired. She told them not to worry about the money, for what mattered most was that Gian and the others were safe. She ended her letter by wishing them good luck on the journey ahead.

Inside the courtyard of the guest house in Takamine village, where the group was staying, Gian sat on a bench sharpening his short sword. Across from him, El was flipping through an old, faded book.
Closing the cover, El glanced up. “Hey, I just noticed. You’re always doing that.”
“It’s just a habit,” Gian replied without looking up. “A sword needs to be in good condition. That’s why I take care of it.”
“Sounds boring. Good thing I’ve mastered my Echo of Water I don’t even need to carry a weapon.” El smirked as a small dagger of ice formed in her hand. She twirled it playfully between her fingers.
Gian finally looked over. “What about you? I’ve noticed you keep reading that same battered book.”
“Oh, perceptive of you,” El said with a grin. She tapped the cover. “See, I’m a bookworn.”
“A what?” Gian asked, raising a brow.
“Bookworn. Someone who reads a book until it's worn out.”
Before Gian could reply, Anni walked into the courtyard. “Don’t mind her. She spouts nonsense from time to time. She’s not crazy… just weird.”
“Harsh, Anni,” El pouted. “I told you this book is sacred. You should read it too, since I always let you store it in your Wingclip relic.” 
“It’s just wasting space in my dimensional storage, honestly,” Anni said flatly, drawing her saber and starting her practice swings.
“What’s the title of the book?” Gian asked.
El handed it to him. “Actually, it doesn’t have one. I found it during my mercenary days. It’s a compilation of lost words and practices from long ago.”
Gian opened the book. On the first page, in messy handwriting, he read aloud:
I am No One. As I travel this ruined world, I realized my life was meaningless—an endless cycle of hiding from monsters, searching for food, and finding shelter. That is why I chose a goal: to record the words, sayings, and practices of the old world before me. As I meet new people, I ask them to share something to add to this book. I do this because I fear, one day, all of these will be forgotten. To whoever holds this in the future—write it down, spread it, keep the knowledge alive.
— No One, Koala, The Thing, …
Below the text was a long list of names.
Pointing at them, El said, “I believe these are all the people who copied the original and passed it down through generations.”
“Interesting,” Gian murmured as he flipped through more pages.
“It’s all rubbish, Auntie El,” Anni cut in. “Someone probably just wrote random nonsense.” She glanced at Gian. “Hey, you’re good with a sword, right? Let’s spar. I need the practice.”
Closing the book, Gian returned it to El and stood up, joining Anni in the courtyard for training.

After a while, Gian and Anni stopped sparring and sat down on the bench, both catching their breath.
“You’ve got good skill, Gian. You even managed to wear Anni out. No wonder you beat her last time,” El teased with a smirk.
“What?!” Anni shot up. “I admit he’s good, but he didn’t beat me last time! It was just luck. I was blinded by the sunlight!” 
“Haha, see? That’s your problem, Anni.” El chuckled, then turned to Gian. “Between the two of you, in terms of swordsmanship, who’s better?”
“Anni,” Gian answered instantly, taking a sip of water.
Anni blinked, surprised. “Why’d you admit that so quickly?”
“Because it’s true,” Gian said simply.
“But strength is a different matter, right?” El added.
“Of course,” Gian nodded.
“Exactly. That’s the difference, Anni. In a fight, it’s not just about swordsmanship. You also have to factor in the environment, your own strength, your enemy’s weakness, and the experience you clearly lack right now,” El explained. 
“Fine, I admit it,” Anni muttered, staring at her hands. “I lack experience. I’ve only ever fought you my whole life.”
“If you want, we can spar every day when we’re not traveling,” Gian offered.
Anni raised an eyebrow. “What’s the catch?”
“Nothing. I need to improve too,” Gian replied honestly.
Anni studied him, then gave a small nod. “Alright then. It’s settled. We’ll fight every day.”
El smiled at the sight of the two. “That reminds me, Gian. You were curious about this echotech, right?” She pulled out a small, tear-shaped glass vial filled with dark blue liquid. 
“This is a Vital Vial. When a drop of blood is placed inside, it tracks the user’s fatigue and life force. See this color? Bright blue means the wearer is energized. It gradually darkens as they tire, and the volume decreases as they take injuries.”
“Hey! Why are you showing him my Vital Vial?” Anni frowned, crossing her arms.
“Don’t get mad. I’m just showing him how it works,” El said calmly. “Besides, you’re clearly exhausted from sparring. No point denying it.”
“So… what happens when it all disappears?” Gian asked.
“If the liquid fully evaporates, it means the life it’s tied to is gone,” El answered softly. “That’s why I wasn’t alarmed that night—it was still more than half-filled.”
Suddenly, Oldman with Tata on his shoulder and Kel arrived, still in the middle of an argument.
“What are you two lovebirds quarreling about now?” El asked with a sly grin.
Kel sighed. “I said we should at least wait for a reply from Tillara City regarding our report about Diego.”
“And I said there’s no point in waiting,” Oldman shot back. “We already sent the report, and we should continue our travels. What’s the use of sitting around?”
El smirked. “Maybe it’s because of those young women who keep flocking to him.”
Tata squeaked in agreement. 
“Hey! That’s got nothing to do with this!” Kel said quickly, flustered.
Seeing Gian and Anni’s puzzled looks, El leaned closer and said, “You two hardly ever step outside this guest house, so you don’t notice it—but the villagers are thrilled with what we’ve done. Some of the single women, especially, have started seeing us as heroes—strong, dependable, the kind worth clinging to. And naturally, they’re drawn most to you young men, Gian.” She chuckled.
“W-Wait, that’s not true! Miss Anni, please believe me. I don’t look at them that way!” Kel stammered.
Anni shrugged coldly. “And why should I care? Stay here if you want.”
The others laughed at Kel’s miserable expression until Oldman raised a hand. “Alright, enough teasing. Kel, I understand your concern. You want to make sure the lesser villages are safe from other bandits, and you’re hoping the city will step in to help, right?”
Kel nodded silently.
“That’s not possible, kid,” El said, her tone softening. “And honestly, it’s not needed. These villagers live in the Unknown. They know the risks. Whether they like it or not, this is their life. I’ve heard the new Highlady in Tillara is kind, but there’s only so much she can do.”
Kel exhaled in defeat. “I guess you’re right. So… when and where do we go?”
“Tomorrow,” Gian said firmly. “Let’s head to the Eagle Order in Ravenscar City, in the Aethelgard Kingdom, like Oldman suggested.”
“That’s a good idea,” El said, then turned to Oldman with raised brows. “Wait—you’re a member?”
“Yeah,” Oldman replied casually. “Just a B-rank, though. You?”
El smirked proudly and pulled out a golden feather coin. “Back in my adventure days, I was A-rank.”
Kel leaned in to compare. “Wow, you can really see the difference from your gold coin to Oldman’s silver—”
Before he could finish, the Stickk Relic whacked him on the head.
Tomorrow, they’d leave Takamine Village. But tonight, they were just people—resting, laughing, and quietly preparing themselves for whatever awaited them next.
